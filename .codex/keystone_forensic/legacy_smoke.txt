Compared with a control group of patients with bacteremia or candidemia, the odds ratio for probiotic use was 14 (95% CI 4–44).[1] The case-fatality rate at day 7 was 22% (10 patients) and at day 28 was 37% (17 patients).[1] The authors explicitly state that *S. boulardii* probiotics are not recommended for patients who have indwelling catheters, are immunocompromised, or are critically ill, and that use should be carefully considered in those with compromised gastrointestinal tract integrity.[1] Furthermore, certain *Escherichia coli* strains harboring the pks pathogenicity island produce colibactin, a genotoxin that alkylates DNA and generates a distinct mutational signature detected in a subset of 5,876 human cancer genomes, predominantly in colorectal cancer.[2] The use of the _S. cerevisiae_ var. _boulardii_ probiotic was defined as use at the time of the positive culture or in the preceding 7 days.[1] A prior randomized trial in severe acute pancreatitis reported increased mortality in the probiotic arm.[1]
