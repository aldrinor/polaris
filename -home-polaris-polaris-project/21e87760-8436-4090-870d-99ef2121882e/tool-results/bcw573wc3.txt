# A literature review on the restructuring impact of Artificial Intelligence (AI) on the labor market

This report synthesizes the retrieved research evidence on the question above. It is organized as a coherent review: an introduction that frames the scope, thematic sections that group the evidence by sub-topic, a cross-study synthesis that surfaces where the findings agree and conflict, and a closing discussion of conclusions and open research gaps. Every quantitative claim is span-grounded to a cited source; claims that could not be verified against the underlying evidence were removed rather than paraphrased.

## Introduction and Scope of the Evidence

Within this framework, automation generates a displacement effect that reduces labor's share of value added, countered by a reinstatement effect as new tasks reinstate labor into a broader range of activities, and the net impact depends on the relative magnitudes of these opposing forces.[1] The contemporary context for these dynamics is the Fourth Industrial Revolution, characterized by a fusion of technologies blurring the lines between the physical, digital, and biological spheres and evolving at an exponential rather than linear pace.[2] Within this revolution, AI and smart robotics are described as emerging technologies that dramatically change not only productivity and the ways of work but also the types and number of available jobs.[3] The Fourth Industrial Revolution is also framed as a paradigm of cyber-physical systems constituted by general-purpose technologies applied across multiple industries, including artificial intelligence, blockchain, genomics, and the Internet of Things.[4] Empirically, a study tracking AI adoption from 2010 to 2023 found that when AI can perform most tasks comprising a particular job, the share of people in that role within a company falls by about 14 percent, but when AI's impact is concentrated in just a few tasks, employment in that role can grow.[5] Even workers in high-wage roles heavily exposed to AI saw their share of total employment grow by about 3 percent over five years, because AI boosted firm productivity and companies that used the technology grew faster.[5] A large increase in AI use is linked to about 6 percent higher employment growth and 9.5 percent more sales growth over five years, suggesting that firms adopting AI do not necessarily shed workers but can grow and use workers more efficiently.[5] Exposure to AI is greatest in high-paying roles, contrasting with earlier technology waves from the 1980s through early 2000s that mainly displaced middle-skill, routine jobs.[5] Business, financial, architecture, and engineering jobs shrank by about 2 percent to 2.5 percent over five years because of their high share of tasks matching what AI can do.[5] At a broader structural level, research measuring changes in the US labor market going back over a century found that the pace of occupational change has slowed over time, with the period from 1990 to 2017 being less disruptive than any prior period measured back to 1880.[6] The total number of jobs in the US economy grew by 14.5 percent between 2013 and 2023, while the number of light delivery service truck drivers grew by 29 percent and the occupation of stockers and order fillers grew from 1.8 million to more than 2.8 million jobs, an increase of nearly 60 percent.[6] These estimates further project that 11.8 million workers currently employed in declining demand occupations will be forced to change jobs by 2030.[7] The World Economic Forum has predicted that the adoption of AI will make 75 million jobs redundant and create 133 million new ones worldwide by 2022.[8] International Data Corporation projected that global spending in AI would reach $85.3 billion in 2021 and grow to more than $204 billion in 2025, representing a compound annual growth rate of 24.5 percent for the period 2021 through 2025.[8] Economic analyses by PwC, IBM, Deloitte, and Gartner Research suggest that adoption of AI will increase global GDP by 15 percent by 2030, representing an additional $15.7 trillion.[8] Despite these projections, a productivity paradox persists: aggregate labor productivity growth in the US averaged only 1.3 percent per year from 2005 to 2016, less than half the 2.8 percent annual growth rate sustained from 1995 to 2004.[9] AI's technological capabilities have advanced markedly, with error rates in labeling the content of photos on ImageNet falling from over 30 percent in 2010 to less than 5 percent in 2016 and as low as 2.2 percent, while error rates in voice recognition on the Switchboard corpus improved from 8.5 percent to 5.5 percent.[9] The scope of disruption extends across industries, with technological disruptions such as the Internet of Things, autonomous devices, enhanced analytical capabilities through AI, and rich media creating smart environments that are transforming industry structures, processes, and practices in tourism and hospitality.[10] In the MENA region specifically, new technologies including automation, robotics, and artificial intelligence are described as disruptive forces causing structural unemployment and inequalities among and within nations.[11] The Fourth Industrial Revolution is projected to alter the share of new occupations from 16 percent to 27 percent by 2022 while redundant jobs decline from 31 percent to 21 percent, with an estimated 0.98 million jobs lost and 1.74 million created.[12] This revolution builds on the Third Industrial Revolution's digital foundations and is distinguished by its scale, scope, and complexity, demanding integrated and comprehensive responses from all stakeholders including the public and private sectors.[2] Research indicates that the number of jobs for clerks in the USA may decrease by 1.6 million and retail salespeople by 830,000.[7]

## Theoretical Foundations: Task-Based Models of Automation and Reinstatement

This framework, building on prior contributions including Autor, Levy, and Murnane (2003), Acemoglu and Autor (2011), and Zeira (1998), posits that production requires tasks allocated to either capital or labor, and that technological change alters the task content of production rather than simply augmenting factor productivity.[1][13] (also mirrored) Within this framework, automation corresponds to the development of technologies enabling capital to substitute for labor in specific tasks, generating what the authors term a "displacement effect" that reduces labor's share of value added.[1] (also mirrored) Simultaneously, automation generates a "productivity effect" by increasing value added and raising demand for labor in non-automated tasks, meaning the net impact on labor demand depends on the relative magnitudes of these two forces.[1] (also mirrored) The framework identifies a third mechanism—the "reinstatement effect"—whereby new technologies create labor-intensive tasks in which labor has a comparative advantage, directly increasing the labor share and functioning as the polar opposite of the displacement effect.[1][13] (also mirrored) During this period, wage bill per capita grew at 2.5 percent per year, largely explained by the productivity effect of 2.4 percent per year.[16][13] (also mirrored) However, from 1987 to 2017, the pattern shifted markedly: the displacement effect accelerated to 0.7 percent per year while the reinstatement effect decelerated to 0.35 percent per year, generating a significant negative shift in the task content of production against labor.[16][13] (also mirrored) Cumulatively, changes in the task content of production reduced labor demand by 10 percent during this later period, and wage bill growth slowed to 1.33 percent per year, essentially stagnating since 2000.[13][14] In manufacturing, the displacement effect was particularly pronounced, reducing labor demand at about 1.1 percent per year or approximately 30 percent cumulatively.[16][13] (also mirrored) Autor further notes that in 1900, 41 percent of the US workforce was employed in agriculture, falling to 2 percent by 2000, illustrating the historical scale of automation-driven displacement.[17] Establishments whose task structures in 2010 were more suitable for AI subsequently posted relatively more AI vacancies, with a standardized coefficient of 9.19 and standard error of 1.21, suggesting that AI adoption follows task-based exposure patterns.[18] Short-term evidence from an online labor market provides striking results: following the release of ChatGPT, freelancers in highly affected occupations experienced a 2 percent decrease in monthly jobs and a 5.2 percent decrease in monthly earnings.[19] Image-based generative AI models produced similar effects, with a 2.1 percent reduction in monthly jobs and a 5.2 percent reduction in monthly earnings, suggesting that the displacement effect operates consistently across different AI modalities.[19] That study identifies "techies"—engineers and technicians with strong STEM skills—as a key factor: economies abundant in techies experienced strong skill upgrading, while polarization persisted in economies with few techies, and robotization was associated with skill upgrading in manufacturing.[20] In the Chinese context, digital transformation research finds that digitalization is associated with increased hiring in managerial, professional, and technical roles and reduced demand for auxiliary and manual labor, with abstract task demand rising while routine and manual tasks decline.[21] One analysis observes that digitalization represents a general-purpose technology whose net effect on employment will likely be negative, though the extent to which this is offset by the creation of new types of goods and services remains uncertain.[22] Another study employs a nuanced task-based approach to show that AI is reshaping inequality through complex interactions of skill restructuring, wage decoupling from productivity, and shifts in worker bargaining power, potentially accelerating capital-biased technological change.[23] Acemoglu and Restrepo developed a task-based framework for understanding automation's impact on labor markets, published in the Journal of Economic Perspectives.[1] (also mirrored) The distinction among these effects is essential because, as the authors emphasize, not all automation technologies increase aggregate labor demand; some automation technologies may reduce labor demand because they bring sizable displacement effects but only modest productivity gains.[1] Between 1947 and 1987, the displacement effect reduced labor demand by approximately 0.48 percent per year, while the reinstatement effect increased it by 0.47 percent per year, resulting in small changes in the task content of production.[16][13] (also mirrored) Acemoglu and Restrepo estimate that about half of employment growth over 1980–2015 took place in occupations in which job titles or tasks performed by workers changed.[1] Complementing this theoretical framework, Autor demonstrates that tasks which cannot be substituted by automation are generally complemented by it.[17] Autor's analysis of automated teller machines illustrates this complementarity: ATM numbers quadrupled from approximately 100,000 to 400,000 between 1995 and 2010, yet bank teller employment rose modestly from 500,000 to approximately 550,000 over a 30-year period.[17] As the routine cash-handling tasks of bank tellers receded, information technology enabled bank personnel to become involved in "relationship banking".[17] Legal occupations, which face little direct impact from automation and are often in firms that use AI heavily, saw a predicted 6.4% increase in employment.[5] The cited work presents a task-based framework for analyzing technology's implications for labor demand and productivity, including how technology displaces and reinstates labor.[1][13] (also mirrored)

## Occupational Exposure to AI: Measures and Susceptibility

Early foundational work by Frey and Osborne estimated the probability of computerisation for 702 detailed occupations using a Gaussian process classifier, establishing a task-based exposure methodology that became dominant in the field of AI labor market research.[24][25] Building on this paradigm, Felten, Raj, and Seamans developed a novel dataset measuring occupational, industry, and geographic exposure to artificial intelligence, published in the Strategic Management Journal.[26] Their approach, unlike the original Frey and Osborne framework, allowed temporal updating but still did not distinguish between AI types such as generative AI versus symbolic AI.[25] That study estimated that roughly 1.8% of jobs could have over half their tasks affected by LLMs with simple interfaces and general training.[27] When accounting for current and likely future software developments that complement LLM capabilities, this share jumps to just over 46% of jobs.[27] A separate text-mining analysis of the International Standard Occupation Classification found that 32.8% of occupations could be fully impacted by ChatGPT, while 36.5% might experience a partial impact and 30.7% are likely to remain unaffected.[28] For the 32.8% of occupations facing a full impact, the effects may be particularly pronounced and disruptive, whereas the 30.7% with no impact will likely continue performing their jobs as before.[28] That study found that the cognitive abilities most exposed to the recent surge of AI research are ideas-related, including attention and search, comprehension and expression, and logical reasoning.[29] This generative AI era finding calls into question pre-GenAI assessments of which workers and tasks are most exposed, as earlier automation primarily targeted routine tasks while GenAI can execute complex, nonstandardized functions traditionally reliant on human judgment.[29][30] The O*NET framework used to classify occupational tasks defines core tasks as those with a relevance rating greater than 67 percent and a mean importance rating of more than 3.0.[30] Global business investment in AI increased 13-fold from US$14.57 billion in 2013 to US$189 billion in 2023, with 35% of businesses already using AI in their operations and more than 50% intending to implement AI technologies.[31] However, more recent data show that the share of STEM jobs grew from 6.5 percent in 2010 to nearly 10 percent in 2024, representing an almost 50 percent increase.[32] Between 2013 and 2023, the share of retail sales jobs dropped from 7.5 to 5.7 percent of the job market, a reduction of 25 percent, with the e-commerce sector identified as an early adopter of predictive AI.[32] By 2023, the International Federation of Robotics reported that average robot density in manufacturing worldwide reached a new high of 141 robots per 10,000 employees.[33] The same study found that job replacement risk is highest in manufacturing and retail, closely followed by the transportation and materials sector, while healthcare and education will proportionately gain more than they lose from automation.[33] Jobs for AI and Machine Learning specialists rose at a compounding rate of 32% per year between 2016 and 2023, illustrating the dual nature of AI as both a destroyer and creator of employment.[33] A critical survey of the empirical literature on generative AI's labor market impact found that no consistent evidence has yet emerged of systemic labor market disruption attributable to generative AI.[25] One study using a difference-in-differences design over 1.2 million contracts on Upwork found that in the five months after ChatGPT's release, treated occupations experienced up to a 20% reduction in earnings.[25] Recent meta-analytic studies confirmed the absence of statistically significant average effects of AI adoption on employment or wages and detected no robust signs of publication bias.[25] Displacement occurs in certain occupations, especially within digital freelance markets, while other sectors exhibit productivity gains or wage premia, reflecting the still-emergent diffusion of AI and its uneven adoption across industries and countries.[25] A risk management perspective emphasizes that the extensive acceptance of AI is likely to result in employment displacement, as many previously human-performed tasks become automatable, necessitating investment in education to facilitate workforce transitions into new roles.[34] In the pharmaceutical sector, the adoption of generative AI could lead to an annual increase in value added by 2.6 to 4.5 percent, primarily via assistance with research and development of new drugs.[35] By 2018, the company had expanded to over 1,500 Frontend Assistants supporting 4 million monthly transactions, performing tasks such as checking connections, entering orders, generating invoices, and processing cancellations.[36] A global AI conceptual framework developed within the International Futures platform produces dynamic forecasts for 186 countries, modeling interconnected aspects of human development including economics, demographics, and energy, providing a macro-level system for understanding long-range AI impacts.[37] Because the associated information processing and problem-solving tasks are the most transversal across occupations, the analysis documented an exponential increase in AI exposure across all occupational categories, even though comparatively high-skilled occupations are more exposed than elementary occupations.[29] A systematic literature review covering publications from 2015 to July 2025 identified research on AI and job displacement with major contributions from China, the US, and Germany.[31] An organizational study of automation found divergent perspectives between management and employees: business leaders rated workflow simplification positively with a mean of 4.19, while employees rated it 4.03, and work-life balance was rated much lower by employees at 2.26 compared to 2.99 by business leaders.[36] ALMATO deployed around 400 software bots by 2016, automating more than 40 processes across 30 different applications and handling roughly 320,000 business transactions each month.[36] Researchers have employed varied methodologies, including Gaussian process classifiers, text-mining approaches, benchmark-linked cognitive ability mapping, and integrated assessment platforms, to assess occupational exposure to computerisation and AI technologies.[24][28][29][37]

## Generative AI: Early Empirical Evidence on Employment and Productivity

The study also documents a 1.2 percentage point decrease in the probability of receiving any employment in a given month—approximately a 10% drop relative to baseline—and a 4.7% reduction in the number of jobs conditional on being employed.[38] By contrast, aggregate labor-market indicators through 2024–2025 show limited economywide disruption; one analysis reports that 35.9% of U.S. workers used generative AI by December 2025 but finds only small positive wage effects and no statistically significant declines in job openings or employment in exposed occupations.[39] A prominent Harvard University study tracking 62 million workers across 285,000 U.S. firms finds that junior positions have been shrinking at companies integrating AI since 2023, with researchers warning that AI is eroding the bottom rungs of career ladders.[40] This finding is consistent with online labor market evidence that entry-level and less-experienced workers in highly exposed occupations bear the brunt of early displacement effects.[38][39] In quantitative terms, treated participants completed the post-treatment task 11 minutes faster—a 0.75 standard deviation reduction—while evaluator grades increased by 0.45 standard deviations.[41] The experiment also documents a reduction in productivity inequality: the correlation between first-task and second-task grades fell from 0.41 in the control group to 0.14 in the treatment group, driven by larger gains for participants who scored lower initially.[41] Workers exposed to ChatGPT were 2x as likely to report using it in their real job two weeks after the experiment and 1.6x as likely two months after.[41] In a large-scale field study, the staggered introduction of a generative AI conversational assistant to 5,172 customer-support agents increased productivity, measured by issues resolved per hour, by 15% on average.[42] Field experiments in online retail similarly document treatment effects ranging from 0% to 16.3% in sales, with smaller and newer sellers as well as less experienced consumers exhibiting disproportionately larger gains.[43] A comprehensive review of the empirical literature finds task-completion time reductions of 15% to more than 50% across writing, customer support, software development, accounting, law, and translation.[39] In software development specifically, GitHub Copilot users complete coding tasks 55.8% faster in controlled settings, with larger gains among less experienced developers.[39] A separate study of nearly 5,000 developers across three field experiments finds a 26.08% increase in weekly task completion, again driven by higher adoption and disproportionately larger gains for junior developers.[39] In a randomized trial with 300 professional translators, a tenfold increase in training compute reduced completion time by 12.3%, improved quality by 0.18 standard deviations, and raised earnings per minute by 16.1%, with lower-skilled translators experiencing gains roughly four times larger than higher-skilled peers.[39] These productivity gains exhibit a consistent pattern of skill compression, whereby less-experienced and lower-skilled workers benefit disproportionately from AI assistance.[39][41][42] Exposure measures across multiple studies converge toward high-wage jobs, with correlations between AI exposure and wages ranging from 0.27 to 0.54.[44] A separate review finds that automating firms show 2% to 10% higher employment and positive elasticities between 0.2% and 2%, suggesting complementarity at the firm level even where aggregate displacement is detectable.[45] The analysis finds that a 1% increase in freelancers' earnings is associated with an additional 0.5% reduction in the number of jobs and a 1.7% reduction in monthly income for text-based services.[38]

## AI, Skills, and Task Transformation

A systematic review of AI's impact on employment and skill demand finds that the effects are multifaceted, with new high-skilled jobs and rising demand for interdisciplinary talent emerging alongside the replacement of low-skilled jobs and polarization of the employment structure.[46] This review further notes that AI adoption necessitates a balanced approach to developing both hard and soft skills, ensuring individuals can effectively collaborate with and leverage AI technologies.[47] Gaps in digital skills, critical thinking, and self-management hinder AI adoption, with financial resources, mindset, and a mismatch between graduate skills and job market demands further complicating the integration of advanced technologies.[47] Research linking advances in specific AI applications—such as image recognition, translation, or strategic game-playing—to workplace abilities and occupations through the AI Occupational Impact (AIOI) measure provides evidence that, on average, occupations impacted by AI experience a small but positive change in wages, but no change in employment.[48] Critically, the positive correlation with wages is driven primarily by occupations with higher software skill requirements, and higher-income occupations have a strong positive relationship between AI impact and both employment and wages, suggesting that access to complementary skills and technologies plays an important role in determining AI's impact.[48] These findings imply that AI has the potential to exacerbate labor market polarization.[48] Workers who perform a high share of non-routine cognitive tasks, such as white-collar professionals, have been the most exposed to advances in AI.[49] On average across OECD countries, the most exposed occupations are Business Professionals, Managers, Chief Executives, and Science and Engineering Professionals, while Food preparation assistants, Agriculture, forestry and fishery labourers, and Cleaners and helpers are the least exposed.[49] Although high-skill workers are more exposed to AI, many studies find that high-skill workers have had better employment prospects after the introduction of AI.[49] However, there is also evidence that low-skilled workers may face declining employment prospects because of AI, and within customer firms for AI products, higher skilled occupations were often at the greatest disadvantage because when their tasks were automated, they were often the most difficult to retrain or move to another position within the firm.[49] Census Bureau survey of 300,000 businesses finding that just 3.2% of firms used AI between 2016 and 2018, consistent with Eurostat data showing AI adoption ranging from 23% in Ireland, 12% in Finland, and 11% in Denmark to 3% in Hungary among OECD EU countries.[49] Research on large language models estimates that roughly 1.8% of jobs could have over half their tasks affected by LLMs with simple interfaces and general training, but when accounting for current and likely future software developments that complement LLM capabilities, this share jumps to just over 46% of jobs.[50] The gendered dimension of AI's labor market impact is significant: in 2023, the share of jobs with high automation potential was 7.8% for women compared to 2.9% for men, and by 2025, both figures had risen to 9.6% for women and 3.5% for men.[51] One explanation is that women are statistically more concentrated in the service and administrative sectors, where tasks are often routine and therefore more rapidly and easily automated.[51] The industries most affected are mining, retail, manufacturing, and employment by government, while for construction, between 2024 and 2035 in the U.S., the projection is for an increase of 4.4%, and for architects and engineers an increase of 6.8% in employment.[52] Technology adoption also reshapes the educational requirements of existing roles: one additional technology requirement listed in a job ad is associated with an increase in the likelihood that a job will require a college degree by 3.9 percentage points, and with a 4.2 percentage point increase in the likelihood that the ad requires a certain minimum experience level.[53] In expensive MSAs, the share of employed workers in clerical jobs declined by 6.5 percentage points through 2015, while affordable MSAs faced just a 2.3 percentage point decline.[53] The AI transition is also reshaping hiring processes themselves: 92% of recruiting leaders say AI-generated resumes are now commonplace in their applicant pools, with half describing them as very common, while 68% of candidates would prefer a hiring process that deprioritizes the resume.[54] Only a third of employers say they are very confident that resumes accurately reflect a candidate's true skills, based on a survey of 998 hiring leaders.[54] In the content creation field, a 49% year-over-year increase in marketing content production reflects how brands are adapting to evolving search algorithms and the growing complexity of multi-channel engagement, making experienced content writers even more essential, especially those who can effectively harness AI solutions.[55] Academic research on generative AI has surged significantly since 2017, and following the public release of ChatGPT in late 2022, the number of academic articles on generative AI with experimental research methods has grown notably.[56] A portfolio investing in firms with high exposure to generative AI and shorting those with low exposure yields 0.45% daily returns, representing the difference in stock market performance between high- and low-exposure firms.[56] The second wave of the fourth industrial revolution, often called Industry 4.0, is transforming how services are created and delivered, with major changes not only in jobs and skills but also in the competencies and educational qualifications needed for those roles.[57] The demand for skills driven by AI is moving toward higher levels of expertise and multidisciplinary integration.[46] A thematic analysis synthesizes the selected articles into six categories, noting that most articles focus on organizational and manufacturing implications for skills development and workforce dynamics.[47] Unemployment is estimated to increase by half a percentage point during the AI transition period as displaced workers seek new positions, and if current AI use cases were expanded across the economy, 2.5% of US employment would be at risk of related job loss.[52] As technology advances, some people struggle to find good jobs due to a lack of the right skills, while others worry that automation threatens low-skilled jobs, and skill gaps must be addressed to ensure a future-ready workforce.[57]

## Distributional Impacts: Inequality, Job Quality, and the Digital Divide

A joint analysis by the Brookings Institution and the Stanford Digital Economy Lab finds that over 30 percent of U.S. workers could see at least half of their job tasks affected by generative AI, with exposure concentrated in white-collar and entry-level occupations rather than in traditional manufacturing or manual labor.[58] One comprehensive displacement analysis projects that 85 million jobs will be displaced by 2025, while 97 million new roles will simultaneously emerge, representing a net positive job creation of 12 million positions globally.[59] However, the gendered dimension of this restructuring is significant: geographic analysis indicates that 58.87 million women in the U.S. workforce occupy positions highly exposed to AI automation compared to 48.62 million men, highlighting substantial gender disparities in vulnerability.[59] Customer service representatives face the highest immediate risk with an 80 percent automation rate by 2025, followed by data entry clerks with 7.5 million jobs eliminated by 2027 and retail cashiers facing 65 percent automation risk by 2025.[59] Regional variations show North America leading automation adoption at 70 percent by 2025.[59] The OECD Employment Outlook 2023 finds that the share of the employed population with AI skills is small—at most 0.3 percent of those employed in OECD countries on average—but growing rapidly.[60] Researchers find that a one standard deviation increase in exposure to AI is associated with a 0.4 percentage point increase in wage growth, largely driven by occupations that require a high level of familiarity with software.[60] However, men—particularly in finance—were more likely than women to expect a wage increase and less likely to expect a wage decrease due to AI, indicating that AI may put further pressure on currently existing wage inequalities.[60] The OECD case studies of AI implementation find that, so far, AI has led to little wage change.[60] Beyond wages, AI affects job quality through changes in task content and the working environment, with the potential to reduce tedious or dangerous tasks but also to leave workers with a higher-paced work environment.[60] Workers exposed to ChatGPT during the experiment were 2 times as likely to report using it in their real job 2 weeks after the experiment and 1.6 times as likely 2 months after the experiment.[61] The international digital divide compounds these distributional effects, as cross-country differences in occupational structure and digital infrastructure create an asymmetry between advanced and developing economies.[62] In Mali, just 12 percent of today's jobs are exposed to GenAI, but only 10 percent of such workers have internet access, meaning just 1.2 percent of workers in Mali hold jobs that are both exposed to GenAI and can make practical use of it.[62] In contrast, about one out of three workers in Switzerland and Singapore have jobs exposed to GenAI, and all of them have access to the internet.[62] Among all countries in the sample, there are 441.8 million jobs that fall within exposure categories that could leverage GenAI for job augmentation, but 66.9 million, or 15.1 percent, of those jobs do not have internet access.[62] Workers in positions vulnerable to automation typically maintain sufficient internet connectivity to experience displacement effects even in low-income settings, while those who could benefit from GenAI augmentation face substantial digital infrastructure gaps that may prevent them from realizing productivity gains.[62] This finding suggests that developing countries may experience the disruptive effects of GenAI faster than its productivity benefits.[62] There are age disparities in exposure as well, particularly in upper-middle-income countries, where about 10.4 percent of workers aged 16 to 35 face automation risk, while only 4.3 percent of workers aged 56 to 65 are in that position.[62] Among college graduates in low-income countries, 13.2 percent face GenAI automation exposure, and 72 percent of these workers already have internet access, implying that initial displacement effects could concentrate among the narrow segment of skilled and digitally-connected workers.[62] Generative AI also affects labor market matching efficiency, with a field experiment finding that AI-assisted job postings made employers 19 percent more likely to post a job and saved them 44 percent of writing time, but there was no discernible increase in matches formed.[63]

## Policy Responses to AI-Driven Labor Market Restructuring

In the United States, the House announced a bipartisan task force on AI in February 2024, reflecting growing congressional interest in the economic impacts of the technology.[64] Survey data from February 2024 show that 84.6% of businesses reporting a "small number" of tasks replaced by AI declined to 79.2% six months later, while those reporting a "moderate number" rose from 13.0% to 17.7%, and those reporting a "large number" increased from 2.4% to 3.1%.[64] Employment effects remained largely unchanged in the near term, with 94.6% of firms reporting "no change" in employment due to AI, declining to 87.4% six months later, while those reporting employment increases rose from 2.8% to 6.5% and those reporting decreases rose from 2.6% to 6.1%.[64] As of 2021, the EU average for AI usage among enterprises stood at 7.9%, with 21 out of 27 countries not exceeding 10%, while Denmark led at 23.9%, followed by Portugal at 17.3%, Finland at 15.8%, and the Netherlands at 13.1%.[65] Eurostat's 2023 survey found that approximately 8% of EU businesses use at least one AI technology, up from 7.6% in 2021, with adoption rates ranging from 1.5% in Romania to 15% in Denmark.[66] Regarding labor displacement mitigation, academic studies have proposed several policy options including a tax on robots that slows the accumulation of automating capital, or a higher capital income tax combined with an unskilled wage tax cut, which research suggests delivers the largest welfare benefits.[65] When imperfect competition is included in models, a markup tax emerges as the second-best policy after a robot tax, providing an additional lever for policymakers to manage automation-driven displacement.[65] In high-income countries, 5.5% of total employment is exposed to automation from AI while 13.4% is exposed to augmentation, whereas in low-income countries the corresponding figures are 0.4% for automation and 10.4% for augmentation.[65] Notably, only 23% of exposed jobs, representing 8% of total U.S. non-farm jobs, have at least one task that is economically attractive to automate, and when accounting for the contribution of automated tasks to compensation, only 0.4% of non-farm wages would potentially be lost to automated computer vision.[65] Governance of AI presents substantial challenges due to the high degree of uncertainty and complexity in the AI landscape, requiring governments to develop both specific governance mechanisms for sectors such as healthcare and transport, as well as a broader global governance framework.[67] The conceptual framing of AI is described as a crucial determinant of how problems are understood, whether they are included in policies, and the priority afforded to them in public policy.[67] Private-sector estimates of aggregate productivity impacts vary widely, with one study estimating that AI adoption could contribute between 0.1 and 0.6 percentage points of productivity growth annually between 2022 and 2040, while Goldman Sachs projects 1.5 percentage points annually for the 10 years following widespread adoption.[65] Using the Acemoglu (2024) framework, the wage bill-weighted share of exposed tasks in the U.S. economy is calculated at 19.9%, with tasks automated by AI assumed to reduce labor costs by 27%, translating into 15% total cost savings given that labor costs account for 53% of output.[68] Capital spending by the five largest U.S. technology firms on AI is scaling at approximately 50% per year, from $155 billion in 2022 to a forecast $755 billion in 2026, implying AI-sector productivity gains of roughly 2.7x per boom cycle that could add between 5% and 58% to cumulative GDP growth over a seven-year transition.[69] The automation of tasks through AI could exacerbate income and social inequalities, as high- and low-skilled occupations benefit from increased wage premiums while middle-skilled jobs are phased out.[67] Adoption is strongly skewed by firm size, with uptake rates exceeding 30% among large firms (at least 250 employees) but only 6% among small firms (10 -49 employees).[66] Studies analyzing automation and augmentation effects find that the latter outweigh the former across both low- and high-income countries.[65] The rapid developments in AI and intensification in adoption across domains pose serious challenges to governments that must manage the scale and speed of socio-technical transitions, requiring better understanding of risks to minimize adverse effects.[67]

## Conclusions and Research Gaps

Early research findings focusing on how AI affects labor demand are collectively inconsistent, and any findings based on current data are necessarily weak signals about the future.[82] Multiple studies using the Current Population Survey—including work by Gimbel et al. (2025), Eckhardt and Goldschlag (2025), and Dominski and Lee (2025)—show at most small changes in hiring in AI-exposed jobs.[84] Separately, Frank et al. (2026) found similar results, and some analyses argue that the timing of declines in job postings corresponds better to rising interest rates than to the launch of large language models.[82] Despite this uncertainty, one source reports that AI exposure measures from Eloundou et al. (2024) have very high correlation with Microsoft Copilot usage from 2025, suggesting that earlier predictions were largely borne out.[84] However, existing measures of AI exposure are still criticized for lacking validation on real-world economic outcomes, and the mismatch between supply and demand for research on this topic is described as unlike anything previously seen.[84] The most experienced and highest-skilled workers saw minimal or even slightly negative effects, suggesting that generative AI functions as a skill-leveling technology rather than a skill-amplifying one.[85] AI compressed the learning curve substantially, with agents having two months of experience performing like agents with six months without AI.[85] Separately, gains from AI adoption were found to be largest for moderately rare problems, where human agents have less baseline experience but the system still has adequate training data.[86] The market structure of AI itself may shape these outcomes, as the two top-ranked foundation model systems differ by only 37 points on the LMSYS scale, translating to approximately a 55.3% chance of one outperforming the other, indicating that frontier models are close substitutes.[87] One analysis characterizes AI's impact on work thus far as "routine-biased technological deployment on steroids," and studies on the impact of machine learning–based AI conclude that, as in prior eras of automation, jobs remain in spite of these shifts.[88] (also mirrored) Whether GenAI will support a continuation of the polarization of work, which analysts widely agree has been underway, or whether it will open possibilities for the augmentation of worker opportunities and the creation of new categories of high-quality jobs, remains an open question.[88] (also mirrored) AI also has unclear effects on matching between employers and job candidates: algorithmic writing assistance for resumes leads to causal increases in hiring and wages for prospective employees, and AI-assisted interviewing leads to better candidate selection.[84] On the other hand, AI usage improves cover letters but makes them less informative signals of worker ability, prompting employers to shift toward alternative signals such as past reviews, and if all resumes begin to look the same, it may become harder to form good matches.[84] The growing integration of AI into academic research workflows itself poses challenges, as AI-generated literature reviews are increasingly being rejected for lacking genuine academic analysis, with journals tightening standards around AI-assisted writing.[91] A systematic review of AI applications in academic writing identified thematic approaches including possible applications, specific tools, plagiarism detection, and ethical use, underscoring the need for further investigation of how AI can improve clarity and accuracy in scientific communication.[92] The evidence on how AI is affecting the labor market today is inconclusive, and claims about harmful impacts on particular groups of workers are premature.[82] One pessimistic estimate from Acemoglu (2024) suggests that the growth effects of generative AI will be only 0.07% per year over the coming decade, which, if correct, would render the market concentration implications a footnote in economic history.[87] The German debate over the potential of "Industry 4.0" predominantly hinges on different views about who controls the production process and how the tools are used.[88] (also mirrored) Platform firms have become a crucial part of the market economy and GenAI may drive significant changes in the organization of work and in market competition.[88] (also mirrored) A study measures the impacts of labor in the platform economy in terms of new work created, old work reorganized, and value creation reconfigured.[88] (also mirrored) The available evidence on AI's labor market effects remains nascent and uncertain, with research having barely scratched the surface.[84][82] The safe deployment of AI is identified as an essential question, with implications for social and economic well-being as well as the ultimate risk to human existence from AI.[88] (also mirrored) In sum, the evidence suggests that AI is beginning to have measurable effects on productivity, particularly for entry-level and lower-skilled workers, but important questions about AI's broader effects on the labor market remain unanswered[84][82][85]
