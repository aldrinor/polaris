### Efficacy

Population: patients with advanced Parkinson's disease and severe motor symptoms.[1] Intervention: this treatment plus medication.[1] Comparator: medication alone.[1] Primary endpoint: the changes from baseline to six months in the quality of life, as assessed by the Parkinson's Disease Questionnaire (PDQ-39), and the severity of symptoms without medication, according to the Unified Parkinson's Disease Rating Scale, part III (UPDRS-III).[1] Timepoint: six months.[1] Study design: randomized-pairs trial.[1]

### Mechanism

Complication type: hardware complications.[2] The Movement Disorder Society-sponsored Unified Parkinson's Disease Rating Scale, known as the MDS-UPDRS, structures the clinical assessment of Parkinson's disease into four distinct and complementary parts.[3] Whereas Part I addresses the non-motor domain, Part II shifts the focus to motor aspects of daily function.[3] However, Part IV specifically records motor complications, distinguishing them from the core motor examination.[3] The combined clinimetric results of this study support the validity of the MDS-UPDRS for rating PD.[3] The primary source identifies hardware complications as a distinct category of DBS-related adverse events.[2] In contrast, the source does not offer guidance on daily life adjustments or support strategies for patients experiencing hardware complications.[2]

### Long-term Outcomes

The Movement Disorder Society-sponsored revision of the Unified Parkinson's Disease Rating Scale (MDS-UPDRS), validated in a study of 877 patients from 39 sites, demonstrated high internal consistency (Cronbach's alpha = 0.79–0.93 across parts) and strong correlation with the original UPDRS (ρ = 0.96), providing a comprehensive framework for tracking these changes across four parts: non-motor experiences of daily living, motor experiences of daily living, motor examination, and motor complications.[3] In advanced stages, symptoms are severe and daily life usually requires full-time support.[5]

### Safety

The FDA labeling for carbidopa-levodopa explicitly warns that patients should be observed carefully when the dosage is reduced abruptly or discontinued, especially if they are receiving neuroleptics.[6] DBS hardware complications are relatively common; in a prospective series of 81 consecutive patients with PD undergoing subthalamic nucleus (STN) DBS followed for a mean of 17 months, hardware complications occurred in 26.2% of patients (atom_009).[2] Specific complications included infections requiring system removal in 2.5% (atom_010) and implantable pulse generator (IPG) removal in 3.7% (atom_011), misplaced leads in 12.5% (atom_012), and lead migration, fracture, or IPG malfunction.[2] A review of the FDA MAUDE database over 10 years identified 221 unique adverse events, with infection being the most common (16.2%) (atom_013) and lead migration accounting for 8.6% (atom_014).[7] Over a third (40%) of all devices with adverse events required return to the operating room for explant or revision (atom_003, atom_004).[7] A review of postoperative psychosis after DBS reported a lifetime incidence of psychosis of 28.3% (atom_018) in PD patients following DBS, underscoring the need for vigilance.[8] Additionally, implantable pulse generator expiration can precipitate a movement disorder emergency with severe parkinsonian worsening or akinetic crisis, necessitating urgent medical evaluation.[9] Abrupt reduction or discontinuation of carbidopa-levodopa has been associated with a symptom complex resembling neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS), characterized by hyperthermia, muscle rigidity, altered consciousness, and autonomic dysfunction.[6] Psychosis can emerge after STN DBS, as illustrated by clinical cases where patients developed paranoid ideation, hallucinations, and delusions within months of surgery, requiring antipsychotic treatment.[8] Patients taking dopaminergic medications may experience intense urges to gamble, increased sexual urges, binge eating, and other compulsive behaviors, which may resolve with dose reduction or discontinuation[6]

### Population Subgroups

The choice between rechargeable and non‑rechargeable IPGs was significantly associated with affordability (χ²=19.13, p<0.001), and remote programming capability influenced brand selection.[10] Despite this, a preliminary survey of 17 DBS patients (85% of those approached) found that 76.5% used their patient programmer once per month or less, 17.7% never used it, and the most common function was checking battery life (58.8%).[11] Adaptive DBS shows promise, as one study reported that overall well‑being significantly improved with adaptive stimulation (p=0.007).[12] Caregivers of intermediate PD spent 6.6 hours per day caring, compared to 3.2 hours for early PD, and were less likely to be employed (25.3% vs 42.4%).[13] Personalized DBS programming using machine learning to optimize gait has produced meaningful improvements in walking, addressing a common post‑DBS challenge.[14] Remote patient monitoring devices led 79.9% of participants to agree that they improved mobility, and 48% perceived themselves as safer.[15] However, a meta‑synthesis of wearable device experiences identified barriers including discomfort, emotional distress, and interface difficulties that hinder long‑term adherence.[16] 87.3% of DBS patients used rechargeable IPGs (r-IPGs), 87.7% were satisfied with their device; among those with r-IPGs, 40.6% felt confident handling the device within one week of discharge, 71.4% recharged independently, and the mean recharge interval was 4.3 days.[10] Caregivers of people with advanced PD had significantly higher perceived burden (ZBI 34.5 vs 24.7 for early PD) and were more likely to take medication due to caregiving (34.7% vs 13.8%).[13] Assistive technologies also contribute: a scoping review reported that home automation significantly increased quality of life (p<0.001), and 63.5% of participants used speech‑to‑text functions to cope with tremors.[15]
