### Safety

Among 125 patients with nonblood *Saccharomyces* culture findings, 24 (19%) had used the probiotic, versus 2% (3/123) of controls (OR 10, 95% CI 3–32).[4] Consequently, *S. cerevisiae* var. *boulardii* probiotics are not recommended for patients with indwelling catheters, those who are immunocompromised, or the critically ill.[4] In one trial, a multispecies probiotic preparation administered to patients with severe acute pancreatitis was associated with increased mortality. *Escherichia coli* carrying the pks pathogenicity island produces colibactin, a genotoxin that alkylates DNA on adenine residues and induces double-strand breaks.[4].[3] Cyclomodulin-producing *E. coli* were present in biopsies from 70% of colorectal cancer patients compared with only 3% of healthy controls.[5] Lipopolysaccharide (LPS) from Gram-negative bacteria such as *Fusobacterium* and *Bacteroides* activates NF-κB signaling, driving inflammation and colorectal cancer metastasis.[6].[7] A retrospective registry study across five Finnish university hospitals from 2009 to 2018 identified 46 patients with *Saccharomyces* fungemia; at least 20 (43%) were using *Saccharomyces boulardii* probiotic, and compared with a control group with bacteremia or candidemia, the odds ratio for use of an *S. boulardii* probiotic was 14 (95% CI 4–44).[4] Human intestinal organoids were exposed to genotoxic pks+ *E. coli* by repeated luminal injection over five months; whole-genome sequencing of clonal organoids before and after this exposure revealed a distinct mutational signature that was absent from organoids injected with isogenic pks-mutant bacteria.[3] The same mutational signature was detected in a subset of 5,876 human cancer genomes from two independent cohorts, predominantly in colorectal cancer.[3] Butyrate, a short-chain fatty acid, stimulates proliferation at low concentrations but suppresses it at high concentrations.[6]

### Dose Response

For total dietary fiber, each 10 g/day increment was associated with a summary relative risk (RR) of 0.90 (95% CI 0.86 to 0.94) across 16 studies including 14,514 cases among 1,985,552 participants, with no significant heterogeneity (I²=0%).[1] In contrast, fruit fiber intake was not significantly associated with CRC risk (RR 0.93, 95% CI 0.82 to 1.05 per 10 g/day), and vegetable fiber showed no association (RR 0.98, 95% CI 0.91 to 1.06 per 10 g/day).[1] Similarly, a review of harnessing gut microbiota for CRC therapy highlights the potential of microbiome modulators but emphasizes the need for rigorous validation and biomarker development before clinical translation.[8] A systematic review and meta-analysis of 25 prospective studies examined the association between dietary fiber and whole grain intake and colorectal cancer (CRC) risk[1] Cereal fiber demonstrated a similar protective dose-response: each 10 g/day increase yielded an RR of 0.90 (0.83 to 0.97) based on 8 studies with 9,487 cases among 1,471,756 participants.[1] Whole grain intake also exhibited a significant inverse dose-response: an increment of three servings daily (90 g/day) was associated with an RR of 0.83 (0.78 to 0.89) across 6 studies with 7,941 cases among 774,806 participants.[1]

### Long-term Outcomes

A meta-analysis across solid tumors indicated that combining FMT with immune checkpoint inhibitors improved pooled objective response rates to 43.1% compared to 36.9% with immunotherapy alone.[9] Fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) has shown potential to augment therapies for colorectal cancer.[9] In a study, patients with refractory, MSS metastatic CRC receiving tislelizumab and fruquintinib achieved a median progression-free survival of 9.6 months and median overall survival of 13.7 months, with an objective response rate of 20% and a disease control rate of 95%.[9] Probiotic modulation with CBM588 (Clostridium butyricum) combined with cabozantinib and nivolumab significantly increased response rates (74% vs 20%, P=0.01) in a trial of metastatic renal cell carcinoma.[9]
