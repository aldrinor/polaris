In adults with overweight or obesity, semaglutide 2.4 mg once weekly demonstrates significant weight loss efficacy compared to placebo. In the STEP 3 trial, when added to intensive behavioral therapy, semaglutide 2.4 mg led to an average body weight loss of 16.0% from baseline after 68 weeks [#ev:ev_006:1430-1494]. The long-term STEP 5 trial showed a mean body weight change from baseline to week 104 of -15.2% with semaglutide versus -2.6% with placebo, for an estimated treatment difference of -12.6 percentage points [ev_008]. More participants achieved weight loss of at least 5% at week 104 with semaglutide (77.1%) than with placebo (34.4%) [#ev:ev_008:1254-1318]. A post hoc analysis of STEP trials 1-3 and 5 found that efficacy was maintained in participants using antidepressants at baseline, with a mean weight change of -15.7% for semaglutide versus -0.2% for placebo in STEP 1 for that subgroup [#ev:ev_003:1200-1265]. In the STEP UP trial, which included a 7.2 mg comparator, the 2.4 mg dose resulted in a weight loss of 17.5% based on the trial product estimand after 72 weeks [#ev:ev_013:750-814]. Regarding safety, gastrointestinal adverse events were more common with semaglutide than placebo, with 82.2% versus 53.9% reporting them in STEP 5, and these events were mostly mild-to-moderate [#ev:ev_008:1406-1470]. The prevalence of adverse events was generally similar between semaglutide and placebo in participants on or off antidepressants [#ev:ev_003:0-100].
