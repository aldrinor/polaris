# Research report: How is the FDA regulating AI-enabled medical devices under the current Predetermined Change Control Plan framework?

### Regulatory

A PCCP is documentation describing what modifications will be made to a device and how those modifications will be assessed.[1] The FDA's final guidance on PCCPs for AI-enabled device software functions, issued in December 2024, states that a PCCP must comprise at least three sections: a description of modifications, a modification protocol, and an impact assessment.[2] This approach is particularly relevant for software-based and AI-enabled devices, where updates can be used to add features and maintain performance and security.[3] The framework aims to ensure the ongoing safety and effectiveness of devices throughout the product lifecycle by managing changes proactively.[4] Notably, modifications under a PCCP must maintain the device's intended use, as changes to intended use or indications typically require additional regulatory review.[3]

### Mechanism

A Predetermined Change Control Plan (PCCP) is FDA documentation that allows manufacturers to pre-define specific AI/ML software changes and the methods to develop, validate, implement, and monitor them, so those updates can be made without a new marketing submission when executed exactly as authorized.[5] The FDA recommends a PCCP describe the planned device modifications, the associated methodology to develop, validate, and implement those modifications, and an assessment of the impact of those modifications.[6] According to the guidance, a PCCP must include three essential components: a Description of Modifications, a Modification Protocol, and an Impact Assessment.[5][7] The Description of Modifications requires defining anticipated changes, including their scope and alignment with the device’s intended use.[7] The Modification Protocol details the methods for developing, validating, and implementing the planned modifications.[7] This framework is intended to support iterative improvement for AI-enabled device software functions while providing a reasonable assurance of safety and effectiveness.[6]

### Safety

Current regulatory frameworks are not intended to accommodate the adaptive nature of AI and have prolonged approval timelines, sometimes exceeding one year for some AI-enabled devices.[8] In response, regulatory agencies like the FDA have introduced guidelines including the concept of predetermined change control plans (PCCPs).[8] A PCCP can streamline oversight by reducing the need for repeated approvals while ensuring safety and compliance.[8] This supports iterative improvement of AI-enabled devices with reasonable safety and effectiveness without needing additional marketing submissions for each modification outlined in the PCCP.[8] The complex and dynamic processes involved in the development, deployment, use, and maintenance of AI technologies benefit from careful management throughout the medical product life cycle.[9]
