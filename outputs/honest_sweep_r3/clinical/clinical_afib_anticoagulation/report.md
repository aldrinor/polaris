# Research report: What are current clinical guidelines for oral anticoagulation in adults with non-valvular atrial fibrillation?

### Efficacy

Direct-acting oral anticoagulants (DOACs), including apixaban, rivaroxaban, and dabigatran, are effective at reducing harm and mortality from thromboembolic events like stroke in patients with non-valvular atrial fibrillation.[1] For non-valvular atrial fibrillation (NVAF), current NHS Lothian formulary recommendations list Apixaban or Edoxaban as joint first-line anticoagulants to prevent thromboembolism.[2] Apixaban and rivaroxaban are also considered first-line oral anticoagulants for AF in patients with a body mass index over 35 kg/m² or actual body weight over 120 kg.[1] All DOACs require dose adjustment in people with moderate kidney impairment for safe and effective use.[1] Routine laboratory measurement of drug concentrations or coagulation function assays is not required for the safe and effective use of DOACs, though it may be beneficial in certain emergency scenarios.[1]

### Safety

Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) decrease the risk of major bleeding, particularly intracranial haemorrhage, compared to warfarin, but gastrointestinal bleeding is increased with some DOACs.[3] For life‑threatening uncontrollable bleeding, specific reversal agents can be used, such as andexanet for apixaban and rivaroxaban and idarucizumab for dabigatran.[3] If these specific agents are not available, pro‑haemostatic agents such as prothrombin complex concentrates or activated prothrombin complex concentrates can be considered.[3] A meta‑analysis of perioperative DOAC interruption reported a major bleeding rate of 1.8%.[3]

### Dose Response

The available evidence does not provide specific dosing regimens for direct oral anticoagulants based on patient characteristics or renal function.[4][5][6] The provided evidence blocks contain PDF data and formatting information but no extractable text detailing dose adjustments.[4][5][6] One document is titled "DOAC Dosing for Atrial Fibrillation (AFib)" but its content is not presented in a readable format.[4] A third source discusses atrial fibrillation in patients with end-stage kidney disease on dialysis, but the provided excerpt does not contain specific dosing recommendations.[6]

### Regulatory

The 2019 ACC/AHA/HRS guidelines established direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) as first line therapy over warfarin for non-valvular atrial fibrillation.[7] Direct oral anticoagulants are first-line agents for eligible patients for preventing stroke in those with nonvalvular atrial fibrillation.[8] Vitamin K antagonists like warfarin are recommended for patients with mechanical valves and valvular atrial fibrillation, not for non-valvular cases.[8] Validated bleeding risk assessments such as HAS-BLED should be performed at each visit and modifiable factors should be addressed.[8] In a provider survey, the majority of providers noted bleeding risk (35.1%) and cost (25.0%) to be the biggest barriers to DOAC use.[7] Clinical factors associated with decreased oral anticoagulation use include vascular disease, high risk ATRIA bleed score, renal disease, prior hemorrhage, and left atrial appendage occlusion.[7]

### Limitations
