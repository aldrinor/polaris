# Research report: What is the efficacy and safety of tirzepatide for glycemic control and weight loss in adults with type 2 diabetes?

### Efficacy

This trial also demonstrated significantly greater weight loss with tirzepatide, with reductions of −7.8 kg, −10.3 kg, and −12.4 kg for the three doses versus −6.2 kg with semaglutide.[1] A systematic review and meta-analysis of RCTs confirms that tirzepatide induces a mean weight reduction of –10.39 kg versus placebo.[2] The dual GIP/GLP-1 receptor agonism of tirzepatide is noted in a systematic review to achieve greater reductions in HbA1c (up to 2.4%) and body weight (up to 22%) compared to selective GLP-1 receptor agonists.[3] The efficacy profile is dose-dependent, as illustrated by data from multiple trials showing that HbA1c reductions and weight loss magnitudes are generally greatest with the 15 mg dose.[4][5]

### Safety

The safety profile of tirzepatide in type 2 diabetes populations is consistent with the GLP-1 receptor agonist class, with gastrointestinal adverse events being the most common.[5] These events are typically mild to moderate and transient.[5] One meta-analysis found no significant increase in the risk of hypoglycemia with tirzepatide versus placebo overall (OR=2.05, 95% CI: 0.95 to 4.42).[2] During the subsequent double-blind period, participants continuing tirzepatide experienced gastrointestinal events including diarrhea (10.7% vs 4.8% placebo), nausea (8.1% vs 2.7%), and vomiting (5.7% vs 1.2%).[6] Treatment discontinuation due to adverse events in that study's double-blind phase was low (1.8% tirzepatide vs 0.9% placebo).[6]

### Comparative

The estimated treatment differences versus semaglutide were −0.15 percentage points (95% CI, −0.28 to −0.03) for tirzepatide 5 mg, −0.39 percentage points (95% CI, −0.51 to −0.26) for the 10 mg dose, and −0.45 percentage points (95% CI, −0.57 to −0.32) for the 15 mg dose.[1] A greater proportion of participants achieved HbA1c targets with tirzepatide, including 82-86% achieving <7.0% versus 79% with semaglutide, and 69-80% achieving ≤6.5% versus 64% with semaglutide.[1] One systematic review notes that across phase 3 trials, dual agonists like tirzepatide achieved greater HbA1c reductions (up to 2.4%) compared to GLP-1 receptor agonists.[3] Adverse events leading to discontinuation occurred in 6.0%, 8.5%, and 8.5% of the tirzepatide 5 mg, 10 mg, and 15 mg groups, respectively, compared to 4.1% in the semaglutide group.[1] One meta-analysis of trials including people with type 2 diabetes found no significant increase in the risk of hypoglycemia with tirzepatide compared to placebo.[2] The safety profile of tirzepatide has been described as consistent with that of the GLP-1 receptor agonist class, with mild to moderate and transient gastrointestinal side effects being most common.[5]

### Dose Response

Corresponding weight loss was also dose-dependent at −7.8 kg, −10.3 kg, and −12.4 kg for the three tirzepatide doses versus −6.2 kg with semaglutide.[4][1] A systematic review and meta-analysis confirms this pattern, reporting that tirzepatide induced a mean weight reduction of –10.39 kg versus placebo, with doses of 5 mg, 10 mg, and 15 mg resulting in reductions of –7.5 kg, –9.3 kg, and –10.7 kg, respectively.[2] Gastrointestinal adverse events, while common across the class, also show some dose-dependency, with incidence of nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea generally higher with the 15 mg dose compared to lower doses across trials.[4][1]

### Long-term Outcomes

For the treatment regimen estimand, the mean percent change in weight from week 36 to week 88 was −5.5% with continued tirzepatide versus a 14.0% increase with placebo, a difference of −19.4% (95% CI, −21.2% to −17.7%; P <.001).[6] At week 88, 89.5% of participants continuing tirzepatide maintained at least 80% of their initial weight loss compared to only 16.6% on placebo (P <.001).[6] A systematic review and meta-analysis of 14 RCTs concluded that tirzepatide induced a mean weight reduction of –10.39 kg versus placebo (95% CI: –10.80 to –9.99) and was associated with achieving weight loss targets of ≥5%, ≥10%, and ≥15%.[2][7] One meta-analysis notes that the incidence of adverse events leading to discontinuation was higher with tirzepatide compared to placebo or active comparators.[4]
