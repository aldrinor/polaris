# Research report: What are current clinical guidelines for oral anticoagulation in adults with non-valvular atrial fibrillation?

### Efficacy

Oral anticoagulation is effective at reducing harm and mortality from thromboembolic events like stroke in patients with atrial fibrillation.[1] For non-valvular atrial fibrillation, most patients can be treated with a direct-acting oral anticoagulant (DOAC), which include apixaban, rivaroxaban, and dabigatran.[1] In this population, DOACs have demonstrated non-inferiority to warfarin with regard to the prevention of stroke and systemic embolism.[2] Specifically, dabigatran at a dose of 150 mg twice daily demonstrated superiority over warfarin in stroke prevention for patients with AF.[2] One source notes that DOACs are preferred over warfarin in many guidelines.[2] In contrast, warfarin remains the first-line oral anticoagulant for valvular AF in patients with mechanical heart valves or rheumatic mitral stenosis.[1]
