# Research report: How is the FDA regulating AI-enabled medical devices under the current Predetermined Change Control Plan framework?

### Regulatory

The FDA's final guidance on Predetermined Change Control Plans (PCCPs) for AI-enabled device software functions was issued on December 3, 2024.[1] This guidance finalizes core concepts allowing manufacturers to avoid new marketing submissions for each significant modification after initial clearance or approval.[1] A PCCP is documentation describing what modifications will be made to a device and how they will be assessed.[2] The FDA recommends a PCCP describe the planned modifications, the associated methodology to develop, validate, and implement them, and an assessment of their impact.[2] If the FDA authorizes a manufacturer's PCCP, that manufacturer can implement modifications in line with the PCCP without submitting new marketing applications.[1] The FDA reviews the PCCP as part of a marketing submission to ensure continued safety and effectiveness without necessitating additional submissions for each described modification.[2] The principles for PCCPs draw upon earlier international Good Machine Learning Practice (GMLP) guiding principles, including monitoring deployed models and managing re-training risks.[3]

### Mechanism

A Predetermined Change Control Plan (PCCP) is FDA documentation that allows manufacturers to pre-define specific AI/ML software changes and the methods to develop, validate, implement, and monitor them, so those updates can be made without a new marketing submission when executed exactly as authorized.[4] The FDA recommends that a PCCP describe the planned device modifications, the associated methodology to develop, validate, and implement those modifications, and an assessment of the impact of those modifications.[5] According to the guidance, a PCCP must include three essential components.[4][6] The first component is a Description of Modifications, which requires manufacturers to define anticipated changes, including their scope and alignment with the device’s intended use.[6] The second component is the Modification Protocol, detailing the methods for developing, validating, and implementing the planned changes.[4][6] The third component is an Impact Assessment of those modifications.[4] This framework is intended to support iterative improvement through modifications to AI-enabled devices while continuing to provide a reasonable assurance of device safety and effectiveness.[5]

### Safety

The PCCP framework is designed to ensure safety and effectiveness by allowing controlled modifications to AI-enabled devices without needing repeated, separate approval submissions for each change.[7] This streamlines regulatory oversight while maintaining compliance, as modifications are implemented under the original plan's authorization.[7] The FDA's guidance on AI in medical devices introduces PCCPs specifically to manage future updates to AI algorithms, addressing the unique challenges posed by their dynamic nature.[8] This approach supports the iterative improvement of AI-enabled devices with reasonable safety and effectiveness.[7] The complex and dynamic processes of AI technologies benefit from careful management throughout the medical product life cycle, which the PCCP framework facilitates.[9]
