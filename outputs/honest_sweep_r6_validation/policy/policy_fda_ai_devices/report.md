# Research report: How is the FDA regulating AI-enabled medical devices under the current Predetermined Change Control Plan framework?

### Regulatory

The FDA has established the Predetermined Change Control Plan (PCCP) as a regulatory pathway for AI-enabled medical devices.[1] A PCCP is documentation describing what modifications will be made to a device and how those modifications will be assessed, allowing for iterative improvement while maintaining safety and effectiveness.[2][3] The FDA reviews the PCCP as part of an initial marketing submission to ensure continued safety and effectiveness without necessitating additional submissions for each planned modification.[1][2] The final guidance for AI-enabled devices, issued in December 2024, recommends a PCCP contain at least three sections: a description of modifications, a modification protocol, and an impact assessment.[4] In October 2023, the FDA, Health Canada, and the UK's MHRA jointly identified five guiding principles for PCCPs for machine learning-enabled medical devices, building upon earlier Good Machine Learning Practice principles.[5][6][7] The FDA describes the PCCP approach as a way to ensure AI/ML-enabled devices can be safely, effectively, and rapidly modified in response to new data.[7]

### Mechanism

A Predetermined Change Control Plan (PCCP) is FDA documentation that allows manufacturers to pre-define specific AI/ML software changes and the methods to develop, validate, implement, and monitor them, enabling updates without a new marketing submission when executed exactly as authorized.[8] The FDA recommends that a PCCP describe the planned device modifications, the associated methodology to develop, validate, and implement those modifications, and an assessment of the impact of those modifications.[9] According to one source, PCCPs include three components: Description of Modifications, Modification Protocol, and Impact Assessment.[8] The FDA reviews the PCCP as part of a marketing submission for an AI-enabled device to ensure continued safety and effectiveness without necessitating additional submissions for each modification described in the plan.[9] This approach addresses the iterative nature of AI/ML development while maintaining safety and effectiveness standards.[8] The FDA encourages the development of innovative medical devices, including AI-enabled ones, and maintains a list of authorized devices.[10]

### Comparative

The traditional regulatory framework is not intended to accommodate the adaptive nature of AI and can lead to approval timelines sometimes exceeding one year for AI-enabled devices, creating challenges for manufacturers who must submit multiple approval requests for updates.[11] In response, the FDA has introduced the concept of Predetermined Change Control Plans (PCCPs) to streamline oversight by reducing the need for repeated approvals.[11] A PCCP supports the iterative improvement of AI-enabled devices without the need for additional marketing submissions to implement each of the modifications it outlines, provided safety and effectiveness are maintained.[11] This approach is part of the FDA's broader action plan for AI/ML-based Software as a Medical Device, which includes issuing draft guidance on a predetermined change control plan for software's learning over time.[12] The FDA's coordinated guidance aims to manage the future updates to AI algorithms that the dynamic nature of the technology necessitates.[13][14]
