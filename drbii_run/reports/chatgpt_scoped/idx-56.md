# Generative AI and the Future Labor Market Before June 2023

## Scope and evidence base

This report synthesizes academic work on generative AI and employment that was publicly available by May 31, 2023. Because that cutoff falls very early in the diffusion of ChatGPT and related systems, the strongest evidence available at the time was concentrated in working papers, preprints, editorials, and early peer-reviewed articles rather than in mature long-run labor-market studies. In practical terms, the pre-June 2023 literature is much stronger on **task-level productivity**, **occupational exposure**, and **adoption challenges** than on realized macro outcomes such as employment levels, wages, or firm reorganization over multiple years. The core evidence base therefore combines early causal and experimental studies with exposure-mapping papers and domain-specific academic commentaries. citeturn24view0turn25view0turn18view0turn18view1turn16view3

A second scope point matters for interpretation: several influential early papers did **not** claim that exposure automatically equals displacement. Eloundou and coauthors explicitly framed their results as estimates of the share of tasks that could be affected or accelerated by large language models, while Felten, Raj, and Seamans focused on which occupations were more exposed to generative AI capabilities. That means the early literature should be read as identifying the **direction and magnitude of potential labor-market pressure**, not as proving that a given percentage of jobs would disappear. citeturn18view1turn16view3

## Positive views in the early literature

The most concrete positive finding in the pre-June 2023 literature is that generative AI can measurably raise productivity in knowledge-intensive tasks. Noy and Zhang’s preregistered experiment with 444 college-educated professionals found that access to ChatGPT substantially increased performance on occupation-specific writing tasks: time taken fell by 0.8 standard deviations and output quality rose by 0.4 standard deviations. Just as important for labor-market interpretation, the gains were larger for lower-ability workers, so the productivity distribution compressed rather than widened within the experimental setting. The paper also found that participants reported greater job satisfaction and self-efficacy when using the tool. citeturn24view0

A field setting produced a similar result. Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond studied the staggered rollout of a GPT-based assistant across roughly 5,000 customer-support agents at a Fortune 500 software company. The measured effect was a nearly 14 percent increase in productivity overall, with much larger gains among the lowest-skilled and least-experienced workers. NBER’s summary of the study further reports that the least skilled workers improved by about 35 percent, customer satisfaction did not deteriorate, novice workers reached experienced-worker performance more quickly, and attrition was lower among workers with access to the tool. Read narrowly, this is a productivity story; read more broadly, it suggests that generative AI can act as a mechanism for **faster skill transfer** from top performers to newer workers. citeturn16view0turn25view0

The early evidence in software work pointed in the same direction. Peng and coauthors found in a controlled experiment that developers using GitHub Copilot completed a JavaScript HTTP-server task 55.8 percent faster than the control group. The authors also concluded that the heterogeneous effects “show promise” for helping people transition into software-development careers, which is one of the clearest early statements that generative AI might lower some occupational entry barriers rather than merely displace incumbents. citeturn18view0

Outside these direct productivity studies, several early academic papers argued that generative AI could augment high-skill occupations by making expertise more accessible. In healthcare, Kung and colleagues found that ChatGPT performed at or near the passing threshold on the United States Medical Licensing Examination and displayed comprehensible reasoning and valid clinical insights, leading them to suggest a near-term role in medical education and, later, clinical decision support. Nature Medicine’s March 2023 editorial made a similarly cautious but positive claim: LLMs may enhance healthcare delivery and patients’ quality of life, provided they are tailored to specific clinical needs. In higher education, Fuchs argued that generative language models could support personalized learning, on-demand help, feedback generation, and other forms of educational assistance. Taken together, these studies framed generative AI as a means of **widening access to cognitive support** in professions built around language, explanation, and synthesis. citeturn16view8turn28view0turn16view9

## Negative views in the early literature

The strongest negative theme in the pre-June 2023 literature is the scale of potential disruption. Eloundou and coauthors estimated that around 80 percent of the U.S. workforce could see at least 10 percent of their tasks affected by LLMs, and about 19 percent of workers could have at least half of their tasks affected. They also estimated that about 15 percent of all worker tasks could be completed significantly faster with an LLM alone, rising to 47 to 56 percent when software and tooling are built on top of the base model. Their results therefore suggested that the labor issue was not confined to a few routine clerical jobs; instead, generative AI had the characteristics of a broad, general-purpose technology with unusually wide reach across cognitive work. citeturn18view1

Felten, Raj, and Seamans sharpened the distributional concern. Their April 2023 paper argued that highly educated, highly paid, white-collar occupations may be among the most exposed to advances in generative AI language and image generation. This was a major departure from older automation narratives that focused mainly on routine manual work. Their abstract also emphasized demographic variation in likely exposure and concluded that government would have an important role in helping people adapt. In other words, the early literature did not simply predict “more automation”; it warned of a possible reshuffling of risk toward office, professional, and communication-heavy jobs that many workers had previously viewed as sheltered from automation. citeturn16view3turn26search0

Even some of the most optimistic productivity papers contained a more ambivalent employment signal. Noy and Zhang found that ChatGPT “mostly substitutes for worker effort rather than complementing worker skills,” while also shifting activity away from rough drafting and toward idea generation and editing. That distinction matters. A technology can raise output and improve worker experience in the short run while still reducing the amount of human effort needed per unit of output. For employers, that can be attractive; for workers, it raises questions about task loss, shrinking training pathways in entry-level drafting work, and the long-run bargaining power of human labor in text-heavy occupations. citeturn24view0

The broader foundation-model literature supplied an additional negative perspective: concentration and systemic risk. Bommasani and coauthors argued that foundation models derive power from scale and broad adaptability, but that this very breadth incentivizes “homogenization,” meaning many downstream applications depend on a small set of shared base models. Their warning was that homogenization provides leverage but also spreads defects from the foundation model into all adapted systems downstream. They explicitly linked foundation models to societal risks including inequity, misuse, and economic and environmental impacts. Likewise, Weidinger and coauthors included “automation, access, and environmental harms” among the major risk categories for language models. In labor-market terms, the early concern was not only replacement of tasks, but also the possibility that a few model providers could shape many occupations at once while reproducing bias, unequal access, and common failure modes at scale. citeturn21view1turn21view2turn20view1

## Specific challenges identified by researchers

The most immediate operational challenge identified by early researchers was **reliability**. In legal writing, Armstrong showed that ChatGPT could perform some legal research and writing tasks impressively, but also produce incorrect or fabricated legal authorities with high confidence. She explicitly linked these failures to professional-responsibility concerns, including competence, candor, and confidentiality. Perlman, writing about legal services more broadly, argued that ChatGPT pointed toward an imminent reimagination of legal work, but also stressed the risks of misuse, excessive reliance, and uncertainty about attribution of written work. In medicine and research communication, Mesko similarly stressed that LLMs may hallucinate references, trigger authorship controversies, raise cybersecurity concerns, and reflect bias. These are not cosmetic problems: in occupations such as law, healthcare, and compliance, false but plausible output can directly undermine professional judgment and institutional trust. citeturn19view1turn16view7turn16view10

A second challenge was **bias, privacy, and unequal treatment**. Weidinger and colleagues’ taxonomy identified discrimination, exclusion, toxicity, private-data leakage, misinformation harms, malicious uses, and automation/access/environmental harms as core LM risk categories. Fuchs translated similar concerns into the higher-education context, warning that overreliance on generative models could reinforce existing biases and inequalities, weaken critical thinking, and require explicit institutional protection of student privacy. The common theme across these works is that generative AI is not neutral infrastructure: its outputs and effects depend on training data, deployment rules, and the social settings in which people use it. That makes labor-market adaptation partly a technical challenge and partly a governance challenge. citeturn20view1turn29view0turn29view3

A third challenge was the field’s limited understanding of the systems themselves. Bommasani and coauthors argued that foundation models were being deployed widely even though researchers still lacked a clear understanding of how they work, when they fail, and what they are really capable of. That uncertainty is especially important in employment contexts because firms are tempted to deploy systems in workflows before institutions have fully developed evaluation standards, liability rules, or auditing methods. Solaiman’s work on the “gradient of generative AI release” framed the issue in governance terms: release methods involve tradeoffs, especially between concentrating power and mitigating risk, and require diverse, multidisciplinary perspectives and investments in guardrails. In a labor-market setting, this means managers and policymakers face difficult choices even before they can cleanly measure what the technology will do to jobs. citeturn21view1turn23search0

A fourth challenge was **organizational adaptation and training**. Felten, Raj, and Seamans argued that government has a role in helping people adapt to changing work. Fuchs argued that universities should train faculty, involve students in implementation, and provide support so that generative AI enhances learning rather than substitutes for human interaction. These points imply a broader labor-market lesson: the disruptive force of generative AI will not be determined only by model capability. It will also depend on whether organizations invest in retraining, redesign workflows, and create human-in-the-loop practices that preserve accountability. citeturn16view3turn29view3

## Future opportunities identified by researchers

The clearest future opportunity in the early literature is **capability leveling**. Both Noy and Zhang and Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond found that lower-performing or less-experienced workers gained more than top performers. That pattern matters because it suggests one plausible future in which generative AI functions as a “best-practice diffuser,” helping organizations spread expert methods more quickly to junior staff. If that pattern generalized, firms could shorten ramp-up times, reduce training bottlenecks, and broaden access to work that previously required stronger writing, coding, or service-interaction skills at entry. citeturn24view0turn25view0

A second opportunity lies in **workflow redesign rather than headcount reduction**. Eloundou and coauthors emphasized that LLM-powered software, not just raw models, could greatly expand the share of tasks completed faster at equal quality. That implies that some of the largest labor-market effects may come not from workers talking directly to chatbots, but from redesigned enterprise tools embedded into CRM systems, coding environments, legal drafting tools, or educational platforms. In that sense, generative AI’s future opportunity resembles earlier general-purpose technologies: not merely substituting for existing work, but reorganizing tasks, sequencing, supervision, and knowledge access across many occupations. citeturn18view1

A third opportunity is **professional mutation rather than professional extinction**. Early legal scholarship was especially explicit on this point. Noonan argued that the legal profession has historically adapted to waves of technological change and that LLMs are best understood as another stage of “creative mutation,” changing tasks drastically without eliminating the profession. Perlman similarly argued that ChatGPT implies a reimagination of how legal and other knowledge services are delivered. The key implication for labor-market research is that occupational futures may hinge less on whether jobs disappear in aggregate and more on which component tasks remain distinctly human: supervision, accountability, client trust, institutional responsibility, and judgment under uncertainty. citeturn19view0turn16view7

A fourth opportunity concerns **education, retraining, and workforce preparation**. Fuchs saw opportunities for personalized, always-available learning support in higher education, while Kung and colleagues pointed to medical-education uses and a path toward future clinical decision support. Eysenbach’s JMIR piece explicitly called for research on automated feedback, virtual patients, simulations, clinical decision tools, training curricula, and rigorous evaluation of quality. Taken together, these papers imply that one of the most important labor-market opportunities may be recursive: generative AI can help train the very workforce that will need to work alongside generative AI. If that promise is realized responsibly, the technology could accelerate reskilling rather than merely render existing skills obsolete. citeturn16view9turn16view8turn28view1

The broadest opportunity, then, is policy-dependent. The early literature already identified the ingredients of a better transition: targeted retraining, careful workflow integration, privacy and bias safeguards, domain-specific evaluation, and release strategies that balance openness with safety. The same studies that warned about displacement and concentration also pointed toward a constructive agenda for shaping complementarity. In that sense, the pre-June 2023 literature was not fatalistic. It was cautious, but it left room for a labor market in which generative AI expands productivity and access while preserving human responsibility—if firms, educators, and governments actively design for that outcome. citeturn16view3turn21view1turn20view1turn23search0turn29view3
