# Generative AI and the Future Labor Market

## Research scope and current state of evidence

The strongest evidence base on Generative AI and employment now comes from four different literatures that answer different questions. Task-exposure studies estimate which jobs are technically susceptible to AI assistance or automation; field experiments and randomized trials estimate short-run productivity effects inside particular occupations; employer, vacancy, and administrative datasets track whether adoption is already changing hiring, wages, and employment; and worker or employer surveys capture adoption, expectations, and anxiety. Read together, these literatures suggest a consistent picture: Generative AI is already reshaping many work tasks and skill requirements, but the evidence for large aggregate job loss is still limited and heterogeneous as of July 2026. Exposure estimates are high, adoption is rising quickly, and measured productivity gains are real in many settings, yet aggregate labor-market effects remain modest or mixed so far, with clearer stress emerging in clerical, freelance, and some early-career segments. ## Positive views

The optimistic case for Generative AI is grounded first in measured productivity and quality gains. In customer support, Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond find that access to a generative conversational assistant raised issues resolved per hour by about 14% to 15% on average, with gains of about 34% for novice and lower-skilled agents and little effect on the most experienced workers. In a randomized experiment on mid-level professional writing tasks, Noy and Zhang find that ChatGPT substantially increased productivity, cutting time spent by roughly 40% and improving output quality by about 18%. In consulting, DellŌĆÖAcqua and coauthors find that consultants using GPT-4 on tasks within the modelŌĆÖs capability frontier completed 12.2% more tasks, finished them 25.1% faster, and produced outputs rated more than 40% higher in quality. In software development, a controlled experiment with GitHub Copilot found that developers completed a coding task 55.8% faster. ## Negative views

The pessimistic view begins with the sheer breadth of exposure. Unlike earlier automation waves that concentrated more on routine physical or clerical work, Generative AI reaches deeply into cognitive and nonroutine office work. IMF, ILO, Brookings, and OpenAI-anchored studies all point toward significant exposure among higher-paid professional, business, legal, administrative, and technical occupations. Brookings, for example, estimates that more than 30% of U.S. workers could see at least half of their occupationŌĆÖs tasks disrupted by existing Generative AI technology, and around 85% could have at least 10% of their tasks affected. ## Specific challenges

One major challenge is methodological. Exposure studies measure what AI could do in principle, but realized labor-market effects depend on adoption, workflow redesign, regulation, complementary software, and managerial choices. AnthropicŌĆÖs usage research illustrates this gap vividly: jobs in computer and mathematics are theoretically highly coverable by LLMs, but actual observed coverage on Claude remains much lower. Brookings describes current labor-market research as being ŌĆ£in the first inning,ŌĆØ and the New York Fed finds little evidence in job-posting data of a distinct AI-driven fall in labor demand after the public release of ChatGPT. This means analysts must resist two opposite mistakes at once: assuming mass displacement is already happening everywhere, or assuming current calm means future disruption will also be mild. A second challenge is organizational and educational transition capacity. The WEF expects 39% of workersŌĆÖ existing skill sets to be transformed or become outdated by 2030, and OECD work on job vacancies shows that AI-exposed occupations are already seeing changes in the skills employers seek, particularly around management, business processes, and digital capabilities. McKinseyŌĆÖs U.S. scenario work adds that Generative AI could accelerate occupational transitions, with an additional 12 million occupational shifts through 2030 in the United States. The policy challenge, therefore, is not simply ŌĆ£teach everyone AI,ŌĆØ but build scalable systems for retraining, occupational switching, credentialing, and on-the-job learning quickly enough to match the pace of task redesign. A third challenge is unequal access across workers, firms, regions, and countries. OECD data show that more than one-third of individuals across OECD countries used GenAI tools in 2025, and usage was already 41.1% among people in employment, but adoption remains uneven across population groups. Geographically, the OECD finds stronger exposure in cities and metropolitan regions than in non-urban areas, with one in three urban workers exposed to Generative AI versus one in five in non-urban regions. In Latin America and the Caribbean, the ILOŌĆōWorld Bank study estimates that up to half of the jobs that could become more productive with GenAI are blocked by digital-access and infrastructure gaps, affecting about 17 million jobs. ## Future opportunities

The largest opportunity lies in redesigning work around distinctly human strengths. The most credible studies do not show humans becoming irrelevant; they show value shifting toward problem framing, judgment, communication, creativity, coordination, empathy, and oversight. PwCŌĆÖs 2026 analysis argues that the new tasks being added to AI-exposed roles are 2.5 times more likely to rely on empathy, judgment, and creativity than the tasks being absorbed by automation, and that some roles are being ŌĆ£professionalisedŌĆØ rather than hollowed out. That aligns closely with the field experiments showing that AI is most useful when workers can validate outputs, decide when not to trust the model, and integrate AI-generated fragments into broader human workflows. A second opportunity is expertise diffusion. The customer-support evidence suggests that GenAI can spread the practices of top workers to novices, while the writing and consulting experiments suggest that AI can help average workers perform closer to expert level on bounded tasks. If firms redesign roles accordingly, the labor-market upside could be substantial: faster onboarding, lower training costs, broader access to higher-value work, and potentially more inclusive hiring if capability can be demonstrated through work rather than only through credentials. PwCŌĆÖs 2025 research explicitly argues that stronger demand for AI skills could push employers to look beyond narrow formal-training pipelines. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn1search0Ņłéturn1search6Ņłéturn1search1Ņłéturn7search0Ņłü

A third opportunity is the creation of new hybrid jobs and skill markets. The WEF reports that AI and big data are among the fastest-growing skill areas through 2030, and Stanford AI Index labor-market analysis, powered by Lightcast, reports a 20% rise in U.S. demand for AI skills between 2023 and 2024. These data point toward growing demand not only for AI engineers, but also for implementation specialists, product managers, risk and governance professionals, AI trainers, evaluators, domain experts, workflow designers, and workers who can combine sector expertise with AI fluency. This is especially important because the occupations that benefit most may not be ŌĆ£AI jobsŌĆØ in a narrow sense, but ordinary jobs into which AI competence becomes embedded. A fourth opportunity is sectoral relief in areas with chronic labor shortages and administrative overload. Healthcare is the clearest near-term example. Evidence on radiology reporting and ambient AI scribes suggests gains in documentation efficiency and reductions in burnout, while PwC notes that healthcare adoption has been slower but the underlying need is acute because of workforce shortages. In the public sector, OECD reports that AI is already being used in at least one area of government in 35 of 36 OECD countries, especially in internal processes and public services. If deployed carefully, GenAI could allow labor to shift from repetitive administrative processing toward complex casework, service design, and direct client interaction. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn11search2Ņłéturn11search1Ņłéturn8view0Ņłéturn6search12Ņłü

A fifth opportunity is for developing economies and lagging regions to use AI as a productivity bridge rather than merely endure it as an imported disruption. World Bank and ILO work on Latin America stresses that many jobs are exposed to productivity-enhancing transformation, but benefits are being constrained by digital bottlenecks. That implies a clear policy opportunity: where broadband, reliable power, device access, language support, and workforce training are improved, GenAI could expand service exports, support SMEs, raise administrative productivity, and make scarce expertise more scalable. The optimistic scenario for the Global South is therefore not automatic lower exposure; it is active readiness that converts lower current adoption into future catch-up. The practical implication is that the future labor market will be shaped less by the abstract capabilities of models than by institutional choices. IMF, ILO, OECD, and WEF sources converge on this point: reskilling systems, worker protections, social dialogue, trustworthy governance, and inclusive infrastructure will determine whether Generative AI mainly deepens labor-market polarization or broadens access to productivity and better work.
