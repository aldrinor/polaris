ABSTRACT
Objectives
To evaluate the effectiveness of semaglutide 2.4 mg for weight reduction and improvement in cardiometabolic biomarkers at 52 and 68 weeks in a real-world setting in the United States.
Methods
This noninterventional, retrospective cohort study used the Komodo Health database and included adults with obesity or overweight with ≥1 weight-related condition who initiated treatment with semaglutide 2.4 mg between June 2021 and August 2022 and remained on treatment for 1 year. A paired t-test was used to assess changes in weight, body mass index (BMI), and cardiometabolic biomarkers (systolic blood pressure [SBP], diastolic blood pressure [DBP], glycated hemoglobin [HbA1c], HDL [high-density lipoprotein] cholesterol, LDL [low-density lipoprotein] cholesterol, and triglycerides) from baseline to 52-week and 68-week follow-up.
Results
Among 4,424 eligible patients, 77% were women and the mean (SD) age was 46.7 (10.0) years and BMI was 36.6 (3.6) kg/m2. Dyslipidemia and hypertension were the most common obesity-related comorbidities at baseline. The mean (%) change in weight from baseline was −15.5 kg (−14.5%; p < 0.001; n = 594) at 52 weeks and −15.9 kg (−14.8%; p < 0.001; n = 391) at 68 weeks. The mean change in BMI from baseline was − 4.8 kg/m2 (n = 1124) at 52 weeks and − 4.9 kg/m2 (n = 700) at 68 weeks. At 52weeks, statistically significant improvements in mean values were observed for SBP (−6.3 mmHg), DBP (−3.1 mmHg), HbA1c (−0.4%), LDL cholesterol (−8.1 mg/dL), and triglycerides (−38.4 mg/dL) (all p < 0.001). Mean change at 52 weeks for HDL cholesterol was 1.0 mg/dL; p = 0.109. Results at 68 weeks were similar.
Conclusions
In this retrospective cohort study, the real-world effectiveness of semaglutide 2.4 mg was demonstrated by reductions in weight and BMI along with improvements in BP, HbA1c, and lipid panel among patients with obesity or overweight.
Plain Language Summary
We studied 4,424 adults with excess body weight in the United States who took semaglutide 2.4 mg (Wegovy®) for a full year. Semaglutide is a medicine that can help people lose weight and improve other health conditions. We looked at how much weight people lost and how certain health markers, like laboratory values and blood pressure, improved. We used information about doctor visits and insurance claims from a large health database.
Most people taking semaglutide 2.4 mg were female and the average age was 47 years. After 1 year, weight was reduced by 15.5 kg (about 34 pounds). Most people (88%) lost at least 5% of their body weight. Almost three-quarters of people (72%) lost 10% or more of their weight. Nearly half lost 15% or more. More than 1 of every 4 people lost 20% or more of their weight.
Other health improvements were also seen. Blood pressure was lowered by an average of 6.3 mmHg for systolic blood pressure (the top number) and 3.1 mmHg for diastolic blood pressure (the bottom number). ‘Bad’ LDL cholesterol and triglycerides (fats in the blood that can increase heart disease risk) also decreased.
We found that treatment with semaglutide 2.4 mg helped people lose a large amount of weight and improved many other factors important to heart health that were measured after 1 year of treatment.
Introduction
Obesity is a significant public health concern in the United States (US), and rates have risen substantially, with the prevalence of overweight and obesity among adults projected to exceed 80% of the US population by the year 2050 [Citation1]. Obesity increases the risk of cardiometabolic conditions, such as type 2 diabetes (T2D), hypertension, and hyperlipidemia [Citation2], and deaths associated with obesity-related cardiovascular disease in the US have tripled from 2.2 per 100,000 population in 1999 to 6.6 per 100,000 in 2020 [Citation3].
The Obesity Society, the Endocrine Society, and the American Association of Clinical Endocrinologists recommend including pharmacological treatment as part of a comprehensive disease management strategy for patients with a body mass index (BMI) ≥30 kg/m2 or a BMI of ≥27 kg/m2 and ≥1 weight-related comorbidity [Citation4]. Semaglutide 2.4 mg, a glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonist (GLP-1 RA), is a once-weekly treatment approved by the US Food and Drug Administration in June 2021 for reducing excess body weight and maintaining long-term weight reduction in adults with obesity or overweight and at least one weight-related comorbidity, and in pediatric patients aged 12 years and older with obesity. Semaglutide 2.4 mg is also approved to reduce the risk of major cardiovascular events such as cardiovascular death, nonfatal myocardial infarction, or nonfatal stroke in adult patients with established cardiovascular disease and either obesity or overweight [Citation5].
The safety and efficacy of semaglutide 2.4 mg in treating obesity was evaluated in the Semaglutide Treatment Effect in People with Obesity (STEP) program, a series of phase 3, randomized, placebo-controlled trials. While study designs, patient populations, and research questions varied, the duration of treatment was largely consistent at 68 weeks (e.g. 1 full year after a 16-week dose-escalation period); one trial had a duration of 104 weeks [Citation6–11]. The largest of these trials, STEP 1, included 1,961 patients without T2D diabetes and found mean reductions in body weight from baseline of 14.9% and 2.4% at 68 weeks in patients treated with semaglutide 2.4 mg and placebo, respectively (p < 0.0001) [Citation11]. In the STEP 2 trial of 1,210 patients with overweight or obesity and T2D, mean weight reductions from baseline to week 68 were 9.6% with semaglutide 2.4 mg and 3.4% with placebo (p < 0.0001) [Citation6].
Whereas clinical trials are conducted to test safety and efficacy in selected populations and under controlled conditions, real-world studies provide an opportunity to examine treatment effects in an environment that more closely reflects clinical practice. Ghusn et al. examined real-world effectiveness of semaglutide at varying doses in two retrospective cohort studies [Citation12,Citation13]. One included 175 patients treated at a weight management center and found a mean weight reduction of 10.9% at 6 months [Citation12]; the other found a mean reduction of 13.4% at 1 year in 304 patients treated at multiple sites within the same health system [Citation13]. In a US-based cohort study examining records of 3,389 patients, mean weight reduction with semaglutide (5.1%) was shown to be greater than with liraglutide (2.2%) at 1 year (p < 0.001) [Citation14]. Our previous real-world study (called SCOPE), which used a large US claims-linked electronic health record database, assessed weight reduction and improvement of cardiometabolic biomarkers after 6 months of treatment with semaglutide 2.4 mg [Citation15].
In this extension of our previous work, we evaluated weight change at 52 and 68 weeks (e.g. 1 year after initiating and 1 year after escalation to 2.4 mg, respectively) in a large, diverse population of privately insured individuals in the US who were treated with semaglutide 2.4 mg. We also examined changes in BMI and cardiometabolic biomarkers, including blood pressure (BP), hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c), and lipids.
Materials and methods
Study design and patient selection
This retrospective, noninterventional cohort, real-world study used the Komodo Health database in the US. Patients were followed for 52 weeks, and the analysis was repeated among those with 68 weeks of follow-up. The Komodo Health database contains 65 billion records from 320 million individuals from 2012 to 2024 and includes electronic medical records (EMRs); pharmacy, laboratory, and medical claims data; and data on patient characteristics, medical conditions, treatments, and healthcare utilization patterns [Citation16].
Patients included in the analysis were 18 years of age or older with a BMI in the range of obesity (BMI ≥30.0 kg/m2) or overweight (BMI ≥27.0–29.9 kg/m2) with at least one weight-related condition (hypertension, dyslipidemia, T2D, obstructive sleep apnea, or cardiovascular disease). Patients also had to have a first pharmacy claim for semaglutide 2.4 mg treatment between June 2021 and August 2022 and continuous use of semaglutide 2.4 mg for at least 52 weeks. In this study, we refer to the drug marketed as Wegovy® as ‘semaglutide 2.4 mg’ to differentiate it from other brands of semaglutide (i.e. Ozempic®, Rybelsus®) that have varying maintenance dosages, indications, and/or routes of administration. Patients with obesity or overweight identified based on International Classification of Diseases codes, BMI recorded in EMRs, or both who escalated to and stayed on 2.4 mg of semaglutide in the follow-up period were included in the study. For patients without BMI measurements in the database, BMI was calculated based on height and weight measurements. Weight-related conditions, for the purposes of including patients with overweight or BMI 27–29.9 kg/m2, were hypertension, dyslipidemia [hypercholesterolemia, hyperglyceridemia, or hyperlipidemia], T2D, obstructive sleep apnea, or cardiovascular disease within 30 days prior to or on the index date. The index date was the date of the first prescription fill (claim) for any dose less than 2.4 mg of semaglutide (0.25 mg, 0.5 mg, 1.0 mg, or 1.7 mg) indicated for weight management to ensure patients included in the study were treatment-naïve. Continuous enrollment in medical and pharmacy insurance plan for ≥6 months prior to the index date and ≥52 weeks following the index date was required for the 52-week analysis, and 68 weeks of continuous use and enrollment was required for the 68-week analysis. Data from these periods (baseline and follow-up) were analyzed for a total study period of 1 January 2021, to 21 August 2023 () . Patients were excluded if, in the baseline period, they had bariatric procedure or used other FDA-approved antiobesity medications (listed in Supplemental Table S1). Additional exclusion criteria are shown in Supplemental Table S2.
This study was conducted in accordance with relevant guidelines and regulations, including the Declaration of Helsinki. Because the study used only de-identified patient records and did not involve the collection, use, or transmittal of individually identifiable data, Institutional Review Board (IRB) approval was not necessary. This study followed the STROBE (STrengthening the Reporting of OBservational studies in Epidemiology) guidelines regarding the conduct and reporting of observational studies.
Outcome measures
Baseline demographics and clinical characteristics for all patients were assessed during a 6-month baseline period prior to and including the index date. Supplemental Tables S3 through 10 show baseline demographics for patients with baseline and endpoint data on weight, BMI, systolic blood pressure (SBP), diastolic blood pressure (DBP), hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c), low-density lipoprotein cholesterol (LDL-C), high-density lipoprotein cholesterol (HDL-C), and triglycerides.
Weight status subgroups were defined by baseline obesity or overweight International Classification of Diseases, Tenth Revision, codes or BMI categories: overweight (BMI 27.0–29.9 kg/m2), obesity class I (BMI 30.0–34.9 kg/m2), obesity class II (BMI 35.0–39.9 kg/m2), and obesity class III (BMI ≥40 kg/m2) [Citation17]. Changes in body weight (absolute and percentage of initial body weight) and changes in BMI were assessed from baseline (−30 to 0 days of index date) to 52 weeks (±30 days) and 68 weeks (±30 days). Reductions in body weight of ≥5%, ≥10%, ≥15%, and ≥20% from baseline were assessed categorically. Patient subgroups by glycemic status were defined according to HbA1c ranges corresponding to normoglycemia (<5.7%), prediabetes (5.7–6.4%), and diabetes (≥6.5%) [Citation18].
Changes in cardiometabolic biomarkers (SBP, DBP, HbA1c, LDL-C, HDL-C, and triglycerides) were assessed from baseline (−90 to 0 days of index date) to 52 weeks (±90 days) and 68 weeks (±90 days). If multiple measurements of the clinical characteristics were available for a given patient in the dataset, the measurement closest to each assessment window (baseline and follow-up, respectively) was used.
Statistical analysis
Descriptive statistics were used to characterize demographic and clinical characteristics, including counts and percentages for categorical variables and means, standard deviations, quartiles, and minimum or maximum values for continuous variables. Changes in body weight from baseline to each follow-up period (52 and 68 weeks) and changes in HbA1c, lipids, and BP from baseline to each follow-up period were analyzed descriptively and compared using a paired t-test. A two-sided p value of < 0.05 was considered statistically significant.
Results
Baseline characteristics of the study population
Overall, 4,424 patients met all study eligibility criteria and escalated to and stayed on semaglutide 2.4 mg in the 52-week follow-up period (). Mean (SD) age was 46.7 (10.0) years, and the population was primarily women (76.9%). Fewer than half the patients were White (47.3%; ). Mean (SD) BMI was 36.6 (3.6) kg/m2, mean (SD) body weight was 106.6 (19.5) kg, and 60% of patients had obesity. The most common obesity-related conditions were dyslipidemia (38.0%), hypertension (37.5%), anxiety (27.0%), and depression (22.9%; ). Baseline and clinical characteristics for the cohort analyzed over the 68-week follow-up period are included in Supplemental Table S11 and Table S12.
Body weight and BMI outcomes
The mean change in body weight from baseline to the 52-week follow-up was −15.5 kg, corresponding to a 14.5% reduction in weight (p < 0.01; n = 594; ). From baseline to the 68-week follow-up, the mean change in body weight was −15.9 kg, corresponding to a 14.8% reduction in weight (p < 0.001; n = 391). At 52 weeks, 522 of the 594 patients with BMI measurement at baseline and end of follow-up (87.9%) experienced body weight reduction ≥5%, 429 (72.2%) experienced ≥10% reduction, 290 (48.8%) experienced ≥15% reduction, and 172 (29.0%) experienced ≥20% reduction. The mean change in BMI from baseline was −4.8 kg/m2 (n = 1,124) at 52 weeks and −4.9 kg/m2 (n = 700) at 68 weeks (). At the 52-week follow-up, reductions in mean body weight and corresponding reductions in percentage change in weight were observed in BMI class I (−14.0 kg [−15.4%]; n = 167), class II (−16.5 kg [−15.6%]; n = 189), and class III (−15.9 kg [−13.0%]; n = 210; ). Reductions in mean BMI were also observed across BMI classes ().
Across BMI classes, larger reductions in weight from baseline (15% or more) were generally observed among those with class I and II obesity, with smaller relative reductions in weight observed in those with class III obesity. Proportion of participants who achieved weight reductions of ≥5%, ≥10%, and ≥15%, respectively, were 88.0%, 76.6%, 57.5% (n = 167) for class I, 91.5%, 75.7%, 49.7% (n = 189) for class II, and 84.3%, 64.8%. 38.1% (n = 210) for class III. Greater mean and percentage reductions in weight were observed for HbA1c ranges corresponding with normoglycemia (−15.7 kg [−14.8%]; n = 99) and prediabetes (−16.8 kg [−15.1%]; n = 66) than with diabetes (−14.3 kg [−11.9%]; n = 15).
Cardiometabolic outcomes
Statistically significant improvements were observed in SBP, DBP, HbA1c, LDL-C, and triglycerides from baseline to the 52-week follow-up (). Overall, mean HbA1c decreased from 5.8% to 5.4% over 52 weeks (a mean change ± SD from baseline of −0.4 ± 1.3; p < 0.001; n = 272). Among patients with diabetes (HbA1c ≥6.5%) at baseline and available data at follow-up (n = 24), the reduction in HbA1c from baseline to 52 weeks was from 8.4% to 6.6%. For the 625 patients with available baseline and follow-up BP values, statistically significant mean (SD) changes in SBP and DBP of −6.3 mmHg (13.8 mmHg; p < 0.0001) and −3.1 mmHg (−10.0 mmHg; p < 0.001), respectively, were observed.
Statistically significant changes in mean (SD) LDL-C (−8.1 mg/dL [25.0 mg/dL]; n = 146) and triglycerides (−38.4 mg/dL [55.1 mg/dL]; n = 161) were also observed from baseline to the 52-week follow-up. Change in mean (SD) HDL-C of 1.0 mg/dL (7.8 mg/dL; n = 165; p = 0.109) was not statistically significant (). Change in cardiometabolic biomarkers at 68 weeks is shown in Supplemental Table S13.
Discussion
This real-world retrospective extension of our earlier work, which showed a 10% weight reduction after 6 months of treatment [Citation15], corroborates findings from controlled clinical trials demonstrating that treatment with semaglutide 2.4 mg for 1 year led to significant weight reduction. In the present analysis, real-world patients experienced a mean weight reduction of 15.5 kg from baseline to the 52-week follow-up, representing a 14.5% reduction from baseline (p < 0.001). When the follow-up period was extended to 68 weeks, the reductions were 15.9 kg and 14.8% of initial weight. Our study also demonstrated reductions in BMI (mean, 4.8 kg/m2; p < 0.001) at 52 weeks and weight reductions across BMI classes. More than one-third of patients achieved ≥15% body weight reduction (class I, 57.5% [n = 96]; class II, 49.7% [n = 94]; class III, 38.1% [n = 80]) compared with baseline.
Mean reductions in body weight at 52 weeks were comparable for those with baseline HbA1c < 5.7% and HbA1c 5.7%–6.4%, with changes from baseline weight of −14.8% and −15.1%, respectively. The mean weight change was smaller, −11.9%, in those with baseline HbA1c ≥6.5%. These findings are consistent with a large body of evidence showing smaller weight reductions among patients with T2D, compared with those without T2D [Citation16]. These findings are also similar to the 14.9% mean weight reduction observed with 68 weeks of treatment with semaglutide 2.4 mg in the STEP 1 trial, which excluded patients with T2D, and to the 9.6% reduction observed in the STEP 2 trial, which only included patients with T2D [Citation6,Citation11].
Our study observed an overall body weight reduction of 14.5%, while other real-world studies with durations ranging from 6 to 12 months reported reductions ranging from 4.4% to 13.4% [Citation12–15]. Compared with the recent 12-month real-world study by Ghusn et al., our study demonstrated slightly higher overall body weight percent reduction (14.5% vs. 13.4%) and reductions across all body weight categories. However, the study of Ghusn et al. differed from ours in that they included all doses of semaglutide and provided a comparative analysis of total body weight loss between patients with and without T2D [Citation13].
Similar to our previous study’s findings [Citation15], in our study, semaglutide 2.4 mg treatment was also associated with improvements in other weight-related parameters. Significant reductions in mean HbA1c were observed at 52 weeks; the greatest reductions in mean HbA1c were observed among patients with baseline HbA1c ≥6.5%. Consistent with this, the overall change in HbA1c at 52 weeks in this study (−0.4%) was similar than was observed at 68 weeks in the STEP 1 trial of patients without diabetes (−0.5%) [Citation11,Citation12]. The STEP 2 trial reported a greater reduction in mean HbA1c compared with the findings of this study at 52 weeks (−1.6% vs. −0.4%) [Citation6], likely due to differences in study populations, since our study included individuals with and without T2D, whereas the STEP 2 trial focused exclusively on patients with T2D and had significantly higher mean baseline HbA1c compared with our study (8.1% vs. 5.7%).
Improvements in lipid profiles were also observed at 52 weeks, including substantial reductions in LDL-C (−8.1 mg/dL) and triglycerides (−38.4 mg/dL). These improvements were greater than those observed in the 6-month study assessing semaglutide, in which changes were −5.6 mg/dL in LDL-C and −18.3 mg/dL in triglycerides [Citation15]. Improvements in lipid profiles after treatment with semaglutide 2.4 mg were also observed in the STEP 1 trial at week 68 and in the 1-year, real-world study assessing the effects of semaglutide on weight loss and risk of cardiovascular disease [Citation11,Citation13]. Similar to these two studies, HDL levels in our study generally remained unchanged. Reductions in lipids, particularly LDL-C, have been linked to better health outcomes such as a reduction in cardiovascular morbidity and mortality [Citation19].
At 52 weeks changes were −6.3 mmHg in SBP and −3.1 mmHg in DBP, which were greater than STEP 1’s reported changes of −6.2 mmHg in SBP and −2.8 mmHg in DBP, and STEP 2’s changes of −3.9 mmHg in SBP and −1.6 mmHg in DBP [Citation6,Citation7,Citation11,Citation12]. Even more notably, in our study change in SBP among patients with initial HbA1c > 6.5% was −14 mmHg (−10.6%). Meta-analyses of 48 trials involving more than 300,000 participants found that over an average 4 years of follow-up, the risk of major cardiovascular events was reduced by approximately 10% for each reduction of 5 mmHg in SBP [Citation20,Citation21]. In the SELECT trial, semaglutide 2.4 mg reduced major cardiovascular events by 20% compared with placebo in patients with established cardiovascular disease treated for a mean of 34 months [Citation22]. Longer-term studies are needed to examine the potential for semaglutide 2.4 mg in sustaining cardiometabolic health benefits for patients living with overweight or obesity. Although not a focus of our assessment, the ability of semaglutide 2.4 mg to reduce the need for lipid-lowering and anti-hypertensive medications was demonstrated in a posthoc analysis of the STEP clinical trials [Citation23].
Strengths of this study included the large sample size, a follow-up period of a full year, and the use of robust data from electronic health records and claims records. The integration of claims data ensured continuous monitoring of semaglutide 2.4 mg use and verified insurance coverage, contributing to the reliability of the findings. This comprehensive approach underscores the utility of leveraging real-world data to assess the long-term effectiveness of therapeutic interventions in diverse patient populations. In addition, this study is one of the few to examine the impact of semaglutide 2.4 mg on cardiometabolic outcomes in a real-world setting. The requirement for adherence to treatment for 12 months, as evidenced by continuous use of semaglutide 2.4 mg, demonstrates the effectiveness of the treatment in a real-world setting among individuals adhering to their treatment. However, this can also be interpreted as a limitation, as patients who were not seeing a benefit of treatment may have discontinued treatment earlier and not be represented in our results. Furthermore, while laboratory-based outcomes provided reliable measures for tracking changes over time, it is important to acknowledge that these outcomes depend on the availability and quality of laboratory data in the EMRs and administrative claims records. Laboratory results were only available for a small subset of patients, who may not be representative of the full cohort. Reliance on data from EMRs and claims records meant that variations in data completeness and accuracy could have affected the study’s findings. The study does not account for potential confounders such as concurrent lifestyle modifications, dietary interventions, and other behavioral factors that could influence the observed outcomes. Additionally, assessment of body composition including adiposity was outside the scope of the current analysis, however may be of interest for future studies [Citation24]. Finally, the studied population included female and commercially insured patients, most of whom were living with obesity (1.4% had overweight but not obesity). Caution should be used in extrapolating these results to other populations.
Conclusions
This study highlights the long-term effectiveness of treatment with semaglutide 2.4 mg for weight reduction, lowering BMI, and improving overall cardiometabolic health, including blood pressure, HbA1c, and lipids. These health improvements were observed at both 52 and 68 weeks and are consistent with clinical trial results as well as previous real-world studies.
