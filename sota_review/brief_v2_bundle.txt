## Scope, assumptions, and how “AI-driven restructuring” is treated in this review
- **Scope**: peer‑reviewed, English‑language **journal articles** on how AI (including machine learning and, where covered, generative AI) is restructuring labor markets as part of the **Fourth Industrial Revolution (4IR)**—i.e., the broader integration of AI with digital platforms, data infrastructures, cyber‑physical systems, and new organizational forms.
- **Definition of “restructuring”** used here: persistent changes in **(i)** task composition within jobs, **(ii)** occupational and skill mix within firms/industries, **(iii)** hiring and training strategies, **(iv)** wage structures and bargaining power, and **(v)** market structure and geographic concentration.
- **Evidence constraint**: I only cite journal articles. Where the research record contains only bibliographic indications of a paper’s existence (without extractable results), I either do not use it or explicitly limit claims to what is visible on the journal landing page.

---

## 1) Core conceptual frameworks used to analyze AI-driven labor-market restructuring

### 1.1 Task-based view of AI: displacement, augmentation, reinstatement, and job redesign
A dominant framing treats occupations as **bundles of tasks**, with AI affecting subsets of tasks rather than “automating entire jobs.” Brynjolfsson, Mitchell, and Rock argue that ML (as a subfield of AI) changes which tasks are technically automatable by circumventing parts of **Polanyi’s paradox**, implying that the key impact is often **job redesign and business-process reengineering** rather than immediate occupation replacement [[1]](https://dspace.mit.edu/bitstream/handle/1721.1/120302/pandp.20181019.pdf). They emphasize that “most occupations” contain some ML-suitable tasks but few contain only such tasks, implying widespread **partial automation** and **task rebundling** [[1]](https://dspace.mit.edu/bitstream/handle/1721.1/120302/pandp.20181019.pdf).

Acemoglu and Restrepo’s task-based logic (in their “direction of AI” argument) emphasizes that automation always reduces labor’s share in the automated tasks, and net labor-demand effects depend on countervailing mechanisms such as productivity/scale effects and new task creation (reinstatement), with the warning that “so-so automation” may displace labor without large productivity gains [[113]](https://economics.mit.edu/sites/default/files/publications/The%20Wrong%20Kind%20of%20AI%20-%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20and.pdf). This framework is widely used to interpret heterogeneous outcomes (job loss vs job transformation) and motivates measurement strategies that map AI capabilities to tasks.

**Restructuring implication**: even when aggregate employment effects are small in the short run, firms may still be actively reorganizing work—shifting hiring away from some roles, rewriting skill requirements, and investing in complementary training and organizational changes.

---

### 1.2 AI as a General-Purpose Technology (GPT) with long lags and complementary co-invention
Brynjolfsson, Mitchell, and Rock explicitly frame ML as a **general-purpose technology** (pervasive, improving over time, and generating complementary innovation), emphasizing **diffusion lags** and the need for co-invention and organizational redesign to unlock value [[1]](https://dspace.mit.edu/bitstream/handle/1721.1/120302/pandp.20181019.pdf). They also highlight that realized macro effects may lag behind technical potential because adoption requires reconfiguring workflows, measurement, incentives, and coordination inside firms [[1]](https://dspace.mit.edu/bitstream/handle/1721.1/120302/pandp.20181019.pdf).

**Restructuring implication**: labor-market impacts may appear first as within-firm changes (tasks, job design, training, monitoring) and only later as sectoral reallocation, wage structure shifts, and measurable aggregate employment changes.

---

### 1.3 Strategic management and organizational complements: exposure, capability, and uneven adoption
Felten, Raj, and Seamans emphasize that empirical progress depends on better measures of **exposure to AI**, proposing occupational, industry, and geographic exposure constructs to enable systematic study across units (firms, industries, places) [[11]](https://collaborate.princeton.edu/en/publications/occupational-industry-and-geographic-exposure-to-artificial-intel/). A recurring theme in this strand is **complementarity**: AI’s value depends on data, talent, and organizational design.

Consistent with this, Han et al. show that AI investment (proxied by AI-skill demands in postings) can increase **firm resilience** to natural disaster shocks, but realized productivity benefits are constrained by a lack of complementary organizational designs [[94]](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/isre.2022.0440). This reinforces the idea that restructuring is partly about building complementary organizational capabilities (workflows, governance, integration) rather than simply “installing AI.”

---

### 1.4 Labor-process and organizational-control perspectives: algorithmic management as restructuring of power and supervision
Beyond production tasks, AI-enabled systems restructure **management, monitoring, and control**. Kellogg, Valentine, and Christin synthesize “algorithmic control” as a contested terrain, identifying mechanisms through which algorithms direct, evaluate, and discipline workers (“6 Rs”), and documenting worker resistance (“algoactivism”) and new algorithmic occupations [[117]](https://onwork.edu.au/bibitem/2020-Kellogg,Katherine+C-Valentine,Melissa+A-etal-Algorithms+at+Work+The+New+Contested+Terrain+of+Control/). De Stefano and Taes focus on algorithmic management as a qualitative transformation of work (surveillance, automated decision-making, privacy, discrimination) and argue for the regulatory role of collective bargaining [[116]](https://lirias.kuleuven.be/retrieve/722036).

**Restructuring implication**: AI’s labor-market effects are not only about job counts and wages, but also about job quality, autonomy, stress, and bargaining power—often mediated by data rights, transparency, and institutional protections.

## 2) Empirical measurement strategies: how journal studies operationalize “AI exposure/adoption” and impacts

### 2.1 Task-content mapping (O*NET) and ML suitability scoring
Brynjolfsson, Mitchell, and Rock operationalize AI exposure through a **Suitability for Machine Learning (SML)** rubric applied to O*NET tasks/activities, producing task- and occupation-level indices and highlighting within-occupation variance as a marker for reorganization potential [[1]](https://dspace.mit.edu/bitstream/handle/1721.1/120302/pandp.20181019.pdf). They are explicit that SML captures technical feasibility and not the full set of adoption constraints (legal, organizational, cultural) [[1]](https://dspace.mit.edu/bitstream/handle/1721.1/120302/pandp.20181019.pdf).

**Strength**: transparent mapping from capabilities to tasks; supports job redesign analysis.  
**Limitation**: feasibility ≠ adoption; indices can become stale as frontier capabilities change.

---

### 2.2 Occupational / industry / geographic exposure indices
Felten, Raj, and Seamans introduce an **AI Occupational Exposure (AIOE)** measure and extend it to industry and geography (AIIE, AIGE), explicitly motivated by data limitations and the need to quantify exposure systematically across economic units [[11]](https://collaborate.princeton.edu/en/publications/occupational-industry-and-geographic-exposure-to-artificial-intel/).

**Strength**: enables cross-sectional comparisons and macro/meso research designs.  
**Limitation**: exposure measures can conflate “can be affected” with “is affected,” and may not capture within-occupation heterogeneity.

---

### 2.3 Establishment-level adoption signals from online vacancies (near-universe postings)
Acemoglu, Autor, Hazell, and Restrepo use establishment-level data on the near-universe of online vacancies to track **AI-related vacancies** as an adoption-related signal and examine how hiring changes within establishments [[90]](https://link.springer.com/10.1007/s11187-025-01168-2). In the accessible description of their findings, AI-exposed establishments increase AI-related vacancies, reduce hiring in non-AI positions, and alter skill requirements of remaining postings, while aggregate occupation/industry employment and wage effects were too small to detect in the study period [[3]](https://econpapers.repec.org/RePEc:ucp:jlabec:doi:10.1086/718327).

**Strength**: high-frequency, establishment-level behavior; can observe shifting skill demands in real time.  
**Limitation**: postings reflect intended hiring, not realized employment; AI vacancy postings may understate embedded AI use that doesn’t require specialized hiring.

---

### 2.4 Skill-demand identification in postings and wage premia for AI skills
Alekseeva et al. identify AI vacancies via lists of AI-related skills in postings and document rapid growth in AI skill demand across industries and occupations, plus sizeable posted-wage premia (11% within firm; 5% within job title) and complementary skill bundles (software, cognitive, social, project management, people management) alongside substitution patterns for customer-service skills [[92]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0927537121000373).

**Strength**: fine-grained skill measurement; links diffusion of AI to pay and complementarity in skill bundles.  
**Limitation**: posted wages may differ from realized wages; skill lists can misclassify evolving AI terminology.

---

### 2.5 Large-scale firm surveys of AI adoption (economy-wide coverage)
McElheran et al. use the U.S. Census Annual Business Survey to measure early adoption of multiple AI technologies (e.g., ML, machine vision, NLP), showing low firm-level prevalence but much higher adoption among very large firms, and strong geographic concentration in “superstar” cities/hubs [[15]](https://ideas.repec.org/a/bla/jemstr/v33y2024i2p375-415.html).

**Strength**: economy-wide representativeness; adoption measures not limited to postings or patents.  
**Limitation**: early-wave adoption measures may miss embedded or emergent generative AI uses; survey questions can lag technical change.

---

### 2.6 Firm-level AI investment measures from resumes and causal identification via AI graduate supply
Babina et al. propose a firm-level AI investment measure using employee resumes, documenting increased AI investment across sectors, and show AI-investing firms have higher sales and **employment growth**, driven primarily by product innovation; results are robust to an IV strategy using exposure to universities’ supply of AI graduates [[23]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0304405X2300185X). They also link AI investment to higher industry concentration and growth concentrated among larger firms (“superstar” dynamics) [[23]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0304405X2300185X).

**Strength**: micro firm-level investment metric; explicit causal strategy (IV); connects AI to employment growth and market structure.  
**Limitation**: resume-based measures may emphasize AI talent accumulation more than AI usage intensity in production.

---

### 2.7 Firm productivity and IV designs using AI adoption surveys (Germany)
Czarnitzki et al. use German firm survey data on AI adoption and estimate positive productivity associations, implementing IV estimators to address endogeneity [[89]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0167268123001531).

**Strength**: links adoption to productivity; explicit econometric handling of endogeneity.  
**Limitation**: survey-based adoption can be noisy; generalizability depends on country/industry context.

---

### 2.8 Event-study designs using exogenous shocks to identify AI’s operational value and complements
Han et al. apply a pooled event study around natural disasters, finding AI investment (proxied via job-posted AI skill demands) increases resilience, but productivity is restrained without complementary organizational designs [[94]](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/isre.2022.0440). This is valuable for labor restructuring because it highlights that AI changes the **production function under volatility**, potentially reshaping roles in operations, risk, and supply-chain response.

---

### 2.9 Establishment-level causal evidence on training changes after AI adoption (staggered DiD)
A 2025 Journal of Economic Behavior & Organization paper uses staggered difference-in-differences on German establishment data and finds AI adoption increases new apprenticeships among training firms (intensive margin) and shifts training resources toward higher-skilled employees over time [[95]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0167268125003257).

**Strength**: causal DiD framing; directly observes a key restructuring channel (training investment).  
**Limitation**: training effects may depend on institutional context (Germany’s apprenticeship system).

---

### 2.10 Text mining of firm disclosures and recruitment-based indicators (China)
Evidence from Chinese listed firms uses text mining of firm disclosures to construct AI indices and links AI application to wage dynamics and job restructuring (routine jobs displaced; creative/social jobs created), with narrowing wage inequality between executives and regular employees [[96]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1059056024007317). Recruitment-data-based AI indicators are also used to study TFP and human-capital structure optimization, with stronger effects where intellectual capital and experienced AI human capital are higher [[97]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S105905602500824X).

**Strength**: alternative adoption measures beyond postings and surveys; connects AI to within-firm occupational restructuring and distributional outcomes.  
**Limitation**: disclosure text may reflect signaling; identification depends on modeling choices not fully visible from excerpts.

## 3) What the journal evidence says about labor-market impacts (employment, wages, inequality, and mechanisms)

### 3.1 Short-run aggregate effects can be small even while firm-level restructuring is visible
The vacancies-based establishment evidence indicates AI adoption is associated with **reduced non-AI hiring** and changed skill requirements within establishments, yet aggregate impacts on employment and wage growth in exposed occupations/industries were too small to detect over the period studied [[3]](https://econpapers.repec.org/RePEc:ucp:jlabec:doi:10.1086/718327)—a pattern consistent with GPT diffusion lags and within-firm reorganization preceding macro reallocation [[1]](https://dspace.mit.edu/bitstream/handle/1721.1/120302/pandp.20181019.pdf).

**Interpretation**: early AI impacts often show up first as **hiring mix and skill-requirement restructuring**, not immediate mass unemployment.

---

### 3.2 Wage effects are heterogeneous and technology-type-specific; AI can correlate with wage gains in some settings
Using patent-text-based exposure measures across AI/software/robots, Fossen et al. find AI exposure is associated with wage increases, while software and robot innovations correlate with wage decreases; they also report sector heterogeneity (AI wage gains in services; robot-related wage declines in manufacturing) and stronger AI-wage associations in later years of their sample [[14]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9237468/). (This evidence is correlational rather than a clean causal estimate.)

Alekseeva et al. document **posted wage premia** for AI skills, with particularly high premia in management roles, and show that firms demanding AI skills pay more even in non-AI postings, consistent with complementarities and rent-sharing in AI-integrating firms [[92]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0927537121000373).

**Mechanism link**: these findings align with a “restructuring” story in which AI complements higher-level cognitive/managerial and integration skills, while displacing some routine or codifiable tasks.

---

### 3.3 Employment growth at AI-investing firms and “superstar” reallocation dynamics
Babina et al. provide strong firm-level evidence that AI-investing firms grow faster in sales and employment and that growth is driven by product innovation; however, the gains are concentrated among larger firms and associated with higher industry concentration [[23]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0304405X2300185X). This indicates AI can create jobs within frontier firms even as it drives **between-firm reallocation** that may reduce competition and shift bargaining power.

McElheran et al. similarly show adoption is far more common among large firms (especially when employment-weighted), and that adoption is geographically concentrated—foreshadowing an “AI divide” across locations [[15]](https://ideas.repec.org/a/bla/jemstr/v33y2024i2p375-415.html).

---

### 3.4 Cross-country evidence: exposure alone does not mechanically predict job loss
Using an AI occupational impact measure across 23 OECD countries, Georgieff and Hyee report no clear overall relationship between AI exposure and occupational employment growth; however, in occupations with high computer use, greater AI exposure is linked to higher employment growth, and there is suggestive evidence of reduced hours in low-computer-use occupations [[114]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35620279/).

**Interpretation**: outcomes depend on complementary digital skills and work organization—supporting the complementarity narrative from GPT and organizational-design perspectives [[1]](https://dspace.mit.edu/bitstream/handle/1721.1/120302/pandp.20181019.pdf), [[94]](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/isre.2022.0440).

---

### 3.5 Direction of innovation matters: automation-biased vs augmentation/task-creating AI
Acemoglu and Restrepo argue that the economic and social consequences of AI are not preordained: AI can be directed toward automation (labor replacement) or toward new tasks that expand labor demand. They warn that recent technological change has been biased toward automation, potentially contributing to inequality, a declining labor share, and weak productivity growth, especially if automation is “so-so” (adopted but not sufficiently productivity-enhancing to offset displacement) [[113]](https://economics.mit.edu/sites/default/files/publications/The%20Wrong%20Kind%20of%20AI%20-%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20and.pdf).

**Implication for restructuring**: whether AI ultimately polarizes or broadens opportunity hinges on innovation incentives, complementary investments, and institutions that shape which applications get built and deployed.

## 4) Industry-specific restructuring pathways (documented in journal literature)

This section focuses on *how* work and value chains are reorganized inside industries—not only on employment totals.

### 4.1 Manufacturing and Industry 4.0: reconfiguration, digital twins, and customization pressures
A detailed manufacturing systems case study shows how a synchronized **Digital Twin** can increase the efficiency of manufacturing system reconfiguration, reducing setup/conversion time and enabling more customer-specific production [[49]](https://www.degruyter.com/document/doi/10.1515/auto-2020-0003/html). While not a labor-market econometrics study, it documents a concrete restructuring pathway in “smart manufacturing”: reconfiguration work shifts toward digital modeling, synchronization, and architecture selection activities [[49]](https://www.degruyter.com/document/doi/10.1515/auto-2020-0003/html).

**Labor implications**: increased demand for hybrid roles combining operations engineering with data/system integration; more frequent reconfiguration can change maintenance, planning, and line-setup occupations.

(Additional 4IR context: Scientific Reports frames Industry 4.0 as integration of cyber‑physical systems, IoT, analytics, AI, and cloud, emphasizing organizational transformation and decision-making as core to implementation [[50]](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-15688-0).)

---

### 4.2 Healthcare (radiology): workflow-wide AI integration rather than isolated diagnostic automation
A radiology review maps AI to multiple phases of the imaging workflow—not only interpretation—including prescan decision support and scheduling, acquisition, worklist prioritization/interpretation, and reporting/communication [[57]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1546144025005988). It highlights intermediate outcomes such as prioritization and turnaround times for critical findings and reduced cognitive/manual burden, while noting the evidence base is still stronger on intermediate workflow metrics than on downstream health/economic outcomes [[57]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1546144025005988).

**Restructuring pathway**: AI shifts radiology work toward exception handling, oversight, communication, and integration across the care pathway, while automating or accelerating subcomponents of the workflow [[57]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1546144025005988). This can change staffing models (triage/prioritization, reporting support) and raise governance needs (validation, monitoring, accountability).

---

### 4.3 Finance: automation of decisioning (trading), digitized advisory processes, and platformization of investment management
- In trading, algorithmic systems can make buy/sell decisions in high-frequency contexts, implying substitution away from some human decisioning tasks and reallocation toward model design, risk oversight, and infrastructure roles (the paper documents the core shift to machine decisioning within particular time-spaces) [[52]](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/24694452.2021.1963658).
- In consumer finance, robo-advisors are described as algorithm-driven platforms that automate financial planning and investment management, reflecting platformization of advisory functions [[53]](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/ijcs.70131).
- In banking, AI-driven digital servitization is described as reshaping established work practices and job roles in customer advisory and sales processes (snippet-level evidence) [[51]](https://www.emerald.com/ijbm/article/doi/10.1108/IJBM-05-2025-0353/1318938/Tensions-in-AI-driven-digital-servitization-in-the).

**Restructuring pathway**: front-office advisory and sales become more data-driven and tool-mediated; standardized portfolio decisions shift to platforms; compliance/model risk functions expand.

---

### 4.4 Transportation and freight: autonomous trucking as a cost-structure shock
An economic analysis of autonomous truck technology highlights that removing the human driver from long-haul operations fundamentally changes the cost structure of freight transportation [[66]](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/03081060.2026.2636745). Even before full autonomy, this points to restructuring in dispatch, safety, remote monitoring, maintenance, and regulatory compliance.

**Restructuring pathway**: a shift from labor-intensive driving toward capital-intensive autonomous systems plus new oversight and maintenance roles; transitional hybrid models may reallocate work rather than eliminate it immediately.

---

### 4.5 Media and creative industries: generative AI and contested substitution of “human creative labor”
A media-industry article directly addresses generative AI’s disruptive potential and the perceived disappearance of human creative labor, situating these concerns in real industry conflict (e.g., Writers Guild of America and SAG strikes) and debates about the value and meaning of human-created content [[78]](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/25741136.2024.2355597). It argues that discussions often reduce impacts to “task replacement,” and suggests a “meaningful work” framing that emphasizes amplifying human creativity rather than merely supplementing/supplanting it [[78]](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/25741136.2024.2355597).

**Restructuring pathway**: creative production becomes reorganized around human–AI co-creation, new IP and compensation disputes, and renegotiation of occupational identity and value—affecting contracts, workflows, and bargaining.

---

### 4.6 Professional services and the public sector: accommodation of ML and algorithm-mediated discretion (evidence present but limited in extractable detail)
- Professional service firms: a study explicitly addresses how professional service firms accommodate ML algorithms (bibliographic evidence with limited extractable content in the record) [[74]](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/01708406241252930).
- Public sector decision-making and street-level work: articles examine human–AI interactions in public decision-making [[80]](https://academic.oup.com/jpart/article/33/1/153/6524536) and street-level algorithms in bureaucratic contexts [[81]](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3449114), implying workflow redesign and altered discretion/accountability even when job counts do not change quickly.

**Note on evidence limitation**: the research record here does not include extractable results detailing occupational mix shifts or measured labor outcomes in these sectors, so claims must remain at the level of documented research focus.

## 5) Firm- and market-level reallocation: productivity, innovation, concentration, and geographic sorting

### 5.1 Productivity and resilience effects: AI often complements capabilities and organization
- Firm productivity: AI adoption is positively associated with firm-level productivity in German data, with IV estimators used to address endogeneity [[89]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0167268123001531).
- Operational resilience: AI investment can improve resilience to exogenous shocks, but benefits depend on organizational complements; under-performing firms may benefit more in principle yet be constrained by organizational design gaps [[94]](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/isre.2022.0440).
- TFP and human capital structure: recruitment-based AI indicators link AI to higher TFP through product competitiveness and optimized human-capital structure; effects are stronger with intellectual capital and experienced AI human capital [[97]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S105905602500824X).

**Restructuring implication**: AI-driven labor-market change is often indirect—AI changes the structure of production, raising the returns to certain organizational forms, skill mixes, and intangible assets.

---

### 5.2 Innovation-led firm growth and employment expansion—paired with concentration risks
Babina et al. show AI investment is linked to higher sales and employment growth, primarily via increased product innovation, but these gains concentrate among large firms and correlate with increased industry concentration [[23]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0304405X2300185X). This is a central “restructuring” narrative: AI may create jobs and value in leading firms while intensifying **market power and reallocation** toward incumbents.

---

### 5.3 Geographic and size-based diffusion divides
Using U.S. Census data, McElheran et al. show AI adoption is limited among firms overall but widespread among very large firms (especially on an employment-weighted basis) and geographically concentrated in major hubs, suggesting uneven diffusion and potential divergence across regions [[15]](https://ideas.repec.org/a/bla/jemstr/v33y2024i2p375-415.html).

---

### 5.4 Wage structure and within-firm job mix changes
Evidence from Chinese listed firms suggests AI applications can raise wages for regular employees via job restructuring (routine jobs displaced; creative/social jobs created) and may narrow wage inequality between executives and regular employees [[96]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1059056024007317). Together with AI skill wage premia in U.S. postings [[92]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0927537121000373), this indicates AI can reshape pay structures through both **skill premia** and **occupational rebalancing** within firms.

---

## 6) Job quality, monitoring, and bargaining power: AI as a change in the employment relationship

### 6.1 Monitoring and well-being outcomes
A meta-analysis of electronic monitoring finds monitoring slightly decreases job satisfaction and increases stress, finds no relationship with performance, and shows a small positive relationship with counterproductive work behavior; performance targets/feedback can exacerbate negative effects [[29]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2451958822000616). While not exclusively “AI monitoring,” it is directly relevant because AI systems often scale and intensify monitoring via automated analytics.

**Restructuring implication**: even absent job loss, AI-enabled monitoring can worsen job quality and shift power toward management.

---

### 6.2 Algorithmic control as a contested terrain; new algorithmic occupations and resistance
Kellogg, Valentine, and Christin describe algorithmic control mechanisms that restrict/recommend, record/rate, and replace/reward, and emphasize that algorithmic systems can generate new occupations while also triggering worker resistance (“algoactivism”) [[117]](https://onwork.edu.au/bibitem/2020-Kellogg,Katherine+C-Valentine,Melissa+A-etal-Algorithms+at+Work+The+New+Contested+Terrain+of+Control/). This reframes “labor-market impact” as the redesign of control, evaluation, and discipline—central to how work is organized.

---

### 6.3 Collective bargaining and regulation of algorithmic management
De Stefano and Taes argue that future-of-work debates overfocus on job quantity and underemphasize qualitative transformations (surveillance, automated HR decisions), advocating collective bargaining as a key regulatory instrument and giving examples of bargaining initiatives addressing algorithmic management and human oversight [[116]](https://lirias.kuleuven.be/retrieve/722036).

---

### 6.4 Data protection and automated decision-making in employment
In the EU context, Lukács and Váradi analyze GDPR-compliant AI-based automated decision-making in employment, noting that AI decision systems are fueled by personal data and raise specific compliance and governance challenges in the world of work [[119]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0267364923000584).

---

## 7) Institutions and policy: what the journal literature implies about managing AI-driven restructuring

### 7.1 Steering the “direction” of AI innovation
Acemoglu and Restrepo argue that societies can influence whether AI is deployed toward labor-replacing automation or toward new-task creation/augmentation, and warn that automation-biased trajectories can worsen labor share and inequality [[113]](https://economics.mit.edu/sites/default/files/publications/The%20Wrong%20Kind%20of%20AI%20-%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20and.pdf). This suggests policy levers around R&D priorities, adoption incentives, and complementary investments (skills, standards, governance) are central to labor-market outcomes.

---

### 7.2 Collective bargaining as a moderator of automation’s labor-market harm (and plausibly AI’s)
In cross-country evidence on automation/robotization, collective bargaining coordination moderates the association between automation and unemployment: weak bargaining is associated with stronger positive automation–unemployment links, while coordinated systems mute these relationships, especially for medium-skilled workers [[115]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0954349X23001078). While this study is about automation broadly, it provides an institution-based template for studying AI: outcomes likely depend on bargaining structures that shape adjustment margins (training, redeployment, wage setting).

---

### 7.3 Wages and incentives to adopt labor-saving technology
Minimum wage hikes can induce establishment technology adoption: evidence shows significant minimum wage increases raise IT budgets in affected establishments and are associated with small average employment declines, larger where routine/low-wage work creates stronger incentives to automate [[120]](https://www.cambridge.org/core/services/aop-cambridge-core/content/view/8ED8E2E5B8926B87BB7463855684403C/S0022109023000376a.pdf/minimum-wage-hikes-and-technology-adoption-evidence-from-us-establishments.pdf). This suggests that labor costs and labor-market regulation influence the profitability and timing of automation-like investments, relevant for AI adoption decisions.

---

### 7.4 Training and active labor-market policy under automation pressure
Using Austrian administrative data, evidence indicates automation risk reduces job-finding probability for unemployed workers, but training mitigates this negative effect—though training efficiency declines over time and may involve trade-offs with re-employment wages except for the most affected workers [[121]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0014292121001549). Complementarily, establishment evidence from Germany shows AI adoption can increase apprenticeships among training firms and shift training resources toward higher-skilled workers [[95]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0167268125003257).

**Policy tension**: training can buffer displacement risks, but targeting, timing, and incentives matter; training investments may also disproportionately benefit already-advantaged groups unless designed for equity [[121]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0014292121001549).

## 8) Synthesis: what we can say with confidence, debates, and open research gaps

### 8.1 Points of convergence across the journal literature
1. **AI restructuring is often task-level and organizational before it is aggregate.** Task-based frameworks and establishment vacancy evidence emphasize within-firm reorganization (hiring mix, skill requirements) and gradual diffusion consistent with GPT lags [[1]](https://dspace.mit.edu/bitstream/handle/1721.1/120302/pandp.20181019.pdf), [[3]](https://econpapers.repec.org/RePEc:ucp:jlabec:doi:10.1086/718327).
2. **Complementarities are central.** Productivity/resilience gains and employment outcomes depend on complementary organizational designs, intellectual capital, experienced AI human capital, and management/coordination skills [[94]](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/isre.2022.0440), [[97]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S105905602500824X), [[92]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0927537121000373).
3. **AI can be associated with job and wage growth in frontier firms**, particularly via product innovation, but this may coincide with **greater concentration** and superstar dynamics, shifting rents and power [[23]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0304405X2300185X), [[15]](https://ideas.repec.org/a/bla/jemstr/v33y2024i2p375-415.html).
4. **Job quality and control are a major restructuring channel.** Monitoring and algorithmic management can reshape well-being and autonomy; governance and bargaining are pivotal [[29]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2451958822000616), [[117]](https://onwork.edu.au/bibitem/2020-Kellogg,Katherine+C-Valentine,Melissa+A-etal-Algorithms+at+Work+The+New+Contested+Terrain+of+Control/), [[116]](https://lirias.kuleuven.be/retrieve/722036), [[119]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0267364923000584).

---

### 8.2 Core debates
- **Will AI be mainly automation-biased or task-creating/augmentation-biased?** This is the central disagreement about long-run labor demand, labor share, and inequality trajectories [[113]](https://economics.mit.edu/sites/default/files/publications/The%20Wrong%20Kind%20of%20AI%20-%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20and.pdf).
- **Will AI widen inequality via skill premia and concentration, or compress it by augmenting less-experienced workers?** The reviewed journal evidence supports both possibilities in different settings: strong AI skill premia and superstar concentration risks [[92]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0927537121000373), [[23]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0304405X2300185X), alongside evidence of wage gains and restructuring toward creative/social jobs in some firm contexts [[96]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1059056024007317).
- **Do institutions and regulation mitigate harms or slow beneficial adoption?** Evidence suggests institutional structure can moderate adverse unemployment associations for automation [[115]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0954349X23001078), while regulation (data rights, contestability, bargaining) is argued to be necessary to address algorithmic management risks [[116]](https://lirias.kuleuven.be/retrieve/722036), [[119]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0267364923000584).

---

### 8.3 Major open problems for research (especially post‑2022 generative AI)
1. **Measurement drift**: indices based on tasks or skill lists require continuous updating as frontier AI capabilities shift [[1]](https://dspace.mit.edu/bitstream/handle/1721.1/120302/pandp.20181019.pdf), [[92]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0927537121000373).
2. **Causal identification of labor-market impacts**: many studies observe restructuring signals (postings, exposure) but identifying net employment and wage effects remains difficult; some of the strongest causal strategies appear in firm investment IV designs and staggered DiD training studies [[23]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0304405X2300185X), [[95]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0167268125003257).
3. **General equilibrium effects**: firm-level job growth can coexist with sector-level displacement if reallocation is strong; more work is needed to decompose within- vs between-firm employment changes under AI [[23]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0304405X2300185X), [[15]](https://ideas.repec.org/a/bla/jemstr/v33y2024i2p375-415.html).
4. **Quality-of-work outcomes**: more causal evidence is needed on how AI-enabled monitoring and algorithmic HRM affect stress, turnover, discrimination, and worker voice beyond correlational patterns [[29]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2451958822000616), [[116]](https://lirias.kuleuven.be/retrieve/722036), [[117]](https://onwork.edu.au/bibitem/2020-Kellogg,Katherine+C-Valentine,Melissa+A-etal-Algorithms+at+Work+The+New+Contested+Terrain+of+Control/).
5. **Sector coverage**: rigorous, outcome-based labor-market studies remain uneven across sectors; healthcare workflow integration and media-industry labor conflict have clearer documented pathways in this record [[57]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1546144025005988), [[78]](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/25741136.2024.2355597), while other sectors have less extractable, quantified evidence here.

## Concluding integration (in literature-review style)
The peer-reviewed journal literature portrays AI as a 4IR driver that restructures labor markets primarily through **task reallocation and organizational redesign**, with **diffusion lags** typical of GPTs [[1]](https://dspace.mit.edu/bitstream/handle/1721.1/120302/pandp.20181019.pdf). Establishment and posting-based studies show that AI adoption is already changing **who firms hire and what skills they demand**, even when aggregate employment and wage effects remain hard to detect in early windows [[3]](https://econpapers.repec.org/RePEc:ucp:jlabec:doi:10.1086/718327), [[92]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0927537121000373). At the firm level, AI investment is strongly linked to **innovation-driven growth and employment expansion**, but also to **greater concentration** and potential superstar-firm dynamics that can reshape bargaining power and inequality [[23]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0304405X2300185X), [[15]](https://ideas.repec.org/a/bla/jemstr/v33y2024i2p375-415.html). Cross-country evidence suggests exposure alone does not mechanically imply job loss; outcomes depend on complementary digital skills and work organization [[114]](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35620279/). Meanwhile, labor-process research emphasizes that AI restructures the employment relationship via **algorithmic control and monitoring**, raising job-quality and governance concerns and motivating institutional responses through bargaining and regulation [[117]](https://onwork.edu.au/bibitem/2020-Kellogg,Katherine+C-Valentine,Melissa+A-etal-Algorithms+at+Work+The+New+Contested+Terrain+of+Control/), [[29]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2451958822000616), [[116]](https://lirias.kuleuven.be/retrieve/722036), [[119]](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0267364923000584). The most consequential unresolved question is the **direction** of AI development—automation-heavy versus augmentation/new-task creation—and how policy and institutions can steer AI toward broadly shared prosperity rather than displacement without commensurate productivity and demand gains [[113]](https://economics.mit.edu/sites/default/files/publications/The%20Wrong%20Kind%20of%20AI%20-%20Artificial%20Intelligence%20and.pdf).

# The Restructuring Impact of Artificial Intelligence on the Labor Market: A Peer-Reviewed Literature Review

## Abstract

**Objective.** This review synthesizes peer-reviewed evidence on the restructuring impact of artificial intelligence on labor markets, situating AI as the signature technology of the Fourth Industrial Revolution and asking what the literature supports about its effects on employment, wages, tasks, the labor share, and labor-process autonomy.

**Methods.** The review draws on approximately 96 English-language peer-reviewed journal articles from economics, management and operations, sociology and technology studies, and applied health and education venues, spanning the early 1990s through April 2026. Evidence is organized into three technology eras — computerization and polarization, industrial robots and narrow AI, and generative AI — with sectoral heterogeneity and cross-cutting analyses nested within.

**Findings.** Heterogeneity, not displacement, is the central peer-reviewed finding. Recent meta-analyses report average robot–labor-market associations close to zero, though individual causal studies (notably Acemoglu and Restrepo, 2020) identify significant local effects. Institutional mediation proves decisive across five levels — platform, firm, regional, national, and informal-sector — determining how exposure translates into realized outcomes. The generative-AI era shows augmentation within firms alongside displacement on open platforms, while exposure measurement itself is methodologically contested.

**Contributions.** The review develops eight original syntheses, each epistemically tagged by evidentiary status: the inversion of exposure from routine to cognitive work; the freelance–population paradox; augmentation as skill compression; the autonomy–employment distinction; the Turing Trap as endogenous technology choice; the jagged frontier and the limits of exposure measurement; the gender exposure reversal; and the Global South's informality buffer as an institutional analogue to European coordinated-bargaining systems.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The Fourth Industrial Revolution and the Labor Market Question

Klaus Schwab's framing of the Fourth Industrial Revolution (4IR) cast the convergence of digital, physical, and biological technologies as a civilizational inflection comparable to the industrial revolutions of the late eighteenth century, the late nineteenth century, and the mid-twentieth century (Makridakis, 2017; Koutroukis, Chatzinikolaou, Vlados, & Pistikou, 2022). Artificial intelligence — understood here to encompass industrial robots, narrow machine learning systems, and the generative models that emerged from 2022 onward — is the signature general-purpose technology of this phase. Scholarly and policy discourses alike have projected large-scale labor market transformation: mass displacement in routine occupations, new forms of polarization, collapse of the labor share, and fundamental reorganization of what it means to work.

Yet when one turns from projection to peer-reviewed evidence, a more complicated picture emerges. The headline causal findings of Acemoglu and Restrepo's (2020) commuting-zone study for the United States — a decline of roughly 0.2 percentage points in the employment-to-population ratio and 0.42 percent in wages per additional robot per thousand workers — coexist with Dauth, Findeisen, Suedekum, and Woessner's (2021) finding that German manufacturing displacement was fully offset by service-sector job creation, with Kauhanen and Rouvinen's (2025) null effect on Finnish wages two years after ChatGPT's release, and with two recent meta-analyses (Guarascio, Piccirillo, & Reljic, 2025; Jurkat, Klump, & Schneider, 2025) reporting average robot–labor-market associations close to zero across the full body of peer-reviewed studies.

This review treats that tension as its organizing puzzle. What the literature documents is neither a uniform collapse of labor demand nor a frictionless augmentation of human work, but a sharply heterogeneous set of outcomes mediated by technology generation, sector, institutions, worker characteristics, and — crucially — the level at which measurement is conducted. Understanding AI's restructuring impact therefore requires attention not only to employment counts and wages but also to task composition, skill evolution, autonomy and control on the shop floor, the functional distribution of income, and the institutions that buffer or transmit technological shocks.

### 1.2 Scope, Methods, and Source Selection

This review is restricted by design to peer-reviewed, English-language journal articles. The corpus was assembled through iterative database searches across economics journals (*Quarterly Journal of Economics*, *American Economic Review*, *Journal of Political Economy*, *Journal of Labor Economics*, *Journal of Economic Perspectives*, *European Economic Review*, *Review of Economic Dynamics*, *Structural Change and Economic Dynamics*), management and operations journals (*Management Science*, *Organization Science*, *International Journal of Production Economics*, *Industry and Innovation*, *Strategic Management Journal*), sociology and technology-studies journals (*Work, Employment and Society*, *New Technology, Work and Employment*, *Organization Studies*, *Human Relations*, *Technological Forecasting and Social Change*, *Technology in Society*, *Social Forces*), and applied health and education journals (*JAMA Network Open*, *Cogent Education*) where sector-specific evidence is relevant. Approximately ninety-six articles comprise the core evidence base.

The temporal scope spans the early 1990s through April 2026, tracing the theoretical evolution from skill-biased technological change through the routine-biased reconceptualization to the task-based framework that dominates contemporary analysis. Empirical emphasis falls on the 2018–2026 window, during which the most rigorous causal studies of industrial robots, narrow AI, and generative AI have been published.

Three epistemic caveats attach to the peer-reviewed constraint. First, publication bias is not merely hypothetical: Guarascio et al. (2025) and Jurkat et al. (2025) both document preferential selection of negative wage results in U.S.-focused robot studies. Second, high-quality working papers — notably Webb's 2019 patent-exposure measure — are excluded even where they have shaped the field; where essential, we reference them as contextual rather than primary evidence. Third, the peer-review process privileges strong causal identification, which systematically underweights qualitative, ethnographic, and descriptive cross-country work. We compensate by drawing explicitly on labor-process scholarship in sociology and industrial relations journals, where methodological norms favor ethnography and case analysis.

### 1.3 Roadmap

The review proceeds in six substantive sections. Section 2 develops the conceptual framework, bridging the Fourth Industrial Revolution lens associated with Schwab and the management-studies tradition with the economics task-based architecture of Acemoglu and Restrepo. Section 3 organizes the empirical evidence into three technology eras — computerization and polarization in the 1990s and 2000s, industrial robots and narrow AI from the 2010s through 2022, and generative AI from 2022 forward — with sectoral heterogeneity nested within each era. Section 4 treats cross-cutting dimensions that a pure era-by-era narrative would obscure: wages versus employment and the labor share; distributional, gender, and demographic effects; the labor-process dimension that standard metrics miss; and the institutional mediation that transmits or buffers technological shocks across platform, firm, regional, national, and informal-sector contexts. Section 5 develops eight original syntheses, each epistemically tagged according to the strength of supporting evidence. Section 6 surveys peer-reviewed policy responses. Section 7 identifies limitations and frontier questions. Section 8 concludes with a compact statement of what the peer-reviewed evidence does and does not support.

## 2. Conceptual Framework

### 2.1 The 4IR Lens

The Fourth Industrial Revolution concept, as articulated by Klaus Schwab in his 2015 *Foreign Affairs* essay and 2016 book, distinguishes itself from prior industrial revolutions in three respects: the convergence of cyber-physical-biological systems rather than a single dominant general-purpose technology, the velocity and exponential scope of change, and the systemic character of its implications across production, employment, institutions, and identity. The framing has been widely adopted across management, sociology, policy analysis, and engineering studies (Koutroukis et al., 2022; Oosthuizen, 2022; Beier et al., 2022; Makridakis, 2017).

Two closely related concepts orbit the 4IR label. Industry 4.0, originating in a 2011 German policy initiative, refers specifically to cyber-physical manufacturing systems, the Internet of Things in production, autonomous robotics, advanced data analytics, and digital twins. It can be read as the operational, manufacturing-specific expression of the broader 4IR thesis (Cirillo, Rinaldini, Staccioli, & Virgillito, 2021; Frank, Sturgeon, Benitez, Marodin, & Cunha, 2025). Employment 5.0 and Industry 5.0, now emergent, add an explicit human-centric layer emphasizing collaboration between humans and machines rather than autonomous automation (Kolade & Owoseni, 2022). For the labor market literature, 4IR functions primarily as a periodization and a meta-narrative that names the technological moment.

The empirical labor studies that adopt 4IR framing cluster in particular venues. Koutroukis et al. (2022) explicitly trace their analysis to Schwab and argue that 4IR is worsening labor-market inequalities and restructuring labor relations across macro, meso, and micro levels. Beier et al. (2022) survey practitioners in Brazil, China, and Germany, finding more job opportunities for highly skilled employees, greater job-loss exposure for manufacturing and assembly workers, and rising qualification requirements across all three countries. Oosthuizen (2022) applies a STARA (Smart Technology, Artificial Intelligence, Robotics, Algorithms) lens to industrial-psychology responses. Kolade and Owoseni's (2022) systematic review of sixty-eight publications identifies three recurring theoretical lenses within the 4IR literature — socio-technical systems, skill-biased technological change, and political economy.

This review treats 4IR as a useful periodization but declines to adopt it uncritically. Two recent peer-reviewed critiques matter. Howcroft and Taylor (2023), writing in *New Technology, Work and Employment*, argue that 4IR narratives risk technological determinism: they understate the degree to which automation outcomes are shaped by existing technologies, economic structures, social relations, gender, and the state. Joyce, Umney, Whittaker, and Stuart (2023), introducing a special issue of the same journal, push back against reified terms like *algorithmic control* and *platform capitalism* where those labels obscure workplace variation and contestation. The social-shaping-of-technology tradition these authors invoke insists that technological change is not an autonomous force to which labor must adjust, but a socially embedded process whose outcomes are endogenous to institutions, bargaining power, and design choices.

The productive move, we argue, is to treat the 4IR framing as complementary to — not a substitute for — a mechanism-based economic analysis. The 4IR lens names the period and foregrounds systemic, cross-domain change. The task-based economic framework developed in Section 2.2 supplies the specific mechanisms by which particular tasks get reallocated between capital, AI, and different kinds of labor. Neither is sufficient alone: 4IR framing without mechanism is narrative, and the task-based framework without periodization risks missing the claim that this technological moment is distinctive.

### 2.2 Three Complementary Analytical Lenses

The economics of technology and labor has produced three major analytical frameworks over the past three decades, each of which emerged to resolve empirical anomalies that its predecessor could not fully explain. Presenting them as strictly nested generalizations would overstate the case; they are better understood as complementary lenses operating at different levels of abstraction, each with distinct empirical domains of applicability.

**Skill-biased technological change (SBTC).** The first framework, consolidated in Katz and Murphy (1992), treats labor as divided into skill groups — typically college-educated versus non-college — and technology as factor-augmenting. Technical change is skill-biased when it raises the productivity of skilled workers more than that of the less-skilled, driving up the relative wage of skilled labor. Acemoglu (1998, 2002) supplied a directed-technical-change foundation: a larger supply of skilled workers makes skill-complementary technologies more profitable to develop, so the direction of innovation responds endogenously to factor supplies. SBTC explained the post-1970s rise in the U.S. college wage premium well, but by the early 2000s it struggled with a new empirical pattern — the hollowing out of middle-skill, middle-wage jobs — that a simple skilled-versus-unskilled partition could not accommodate.

**Routine-biased technological change (RBTC).** The second framework, introduced by Autor, Levy, and Murnane (2003), reconceptualized the unit of analysis. Rather than aggregating labor into two or three skill groups, RBTC classifies jobs by their underlying *task* content and argues that computerization substitutes for routine, codifiable tasks — both cognitive and manual — while complementing non-routine analytical and interpersonal tasks. Because middle-wage jobs tended to be routine-intensive, the hollowing out of the middle became the signature empirical prediction. Empirical confirmations followed in Autor, Katz, and Kearney (2006), Goos and Manning (2007) for Britain, Autor and Dorn (2013) for U.S. commuting zones, and Goos, Manning, and Salomons (2014) for Europe. By 2015, Autor's synthetic *Journal of Economic Perspectives* essay had become the canonical statement of why jobs nonetheless persisted: automation raises productivity, which expands demand elsewhere, while new labor-intensive tasks emerge at the margin. RBTC succeeded in explaining polarization but remained silent on two increasingly salient phenomena: the decline of labor's share of national income, and the capacity of technologies to create entirely new tasks for which the codifiable-versus-non-codifiable distinction offered little guidance.

**The task-based framework with displacement and reinstatement.** The third framework, developed by Acemoglu and Restrepo across a series of articles (2018, 2019, 2022a, 2022b, 2024), generalizes RBTC in two crucial ways. First, the task allocation between capital and labor is explicitly endogenous: firms choose to automate tasks where the cost of capital has fallen relative to labor's productivity on that task. Second, the framework recognizes that technology can *create* new labor-intensive tasks — the reinstatement effect — which counteracts the displacement effect of automation on labor demand and the labor share. In their 2018 *American Economic Review* article, Acemoglu and Restrepo show that automation always pushes the labor share down, while new tasks push it up; the balance determines long-run factor shares. Their 2019 *Journal of Economic Perspectives* exposition argues that slower employment growth in recent decades reflects faster displacement, especially in manufacturing, and weaker reinstatement. The 2022 *Econometrica* extension attributes between fifty and seventy percent of the rise in U.S. wage inequality since 1980 to automation-driven task displacement. The 2024 *Oxford Open Economics* synthesis explicitly extends the framework to contemporary AI and generative models, arguing that the same displacement-reinstatement calculus applies to current debates about large language models.

The three frameworks are complementary rather than competitive. SBTC remains the clearest model for relative skill demand and the college wage premium. RBTC remains indispensable for understanding polarization during the computerization era. The task-based framework subsumes the phenomena both describe within a more general model of endogenous task allocation, and it provides the apparatus needed to analyze the labor-share decline and the generative-AI era's particular reallocation of cognitive work — developments the earlier frameworks did not anticipate. A rigorous reading of the field therefore treats these as layered lenses to be deployed according to the question at hand, not as rival theories competing for the same explanatory ground.

### 2.3 AI as Prediction Technology Complementary to Judgment

A complementary mechanism-level framework has been developed by Agrawal, Gans, and Goldfarb across a sequence of peer-reviewed journal articles. Their central move is to define recent advances in machine learning economically as a reduction in the cost of producing *predictions* — that is, the inference of unobserved states from available information. Prediction is valuable because it is an input into decision-making, not because it is the whole decision itself. In their 2018 *AEA Papers and Proceedings* piece, they formalize *judgment* as the human task of discovering the payoffs attached to action-state pairs. Their 2019 *Journal of Economic Perspectives* article (Agrawal, Gans, & Goldfarb, 2019a) translates the framework into task-level labor implications: cheaper prediction can automate prediction tasks themselves, automate complementary decision tasks when machine action becomes relatively more valuable, augment human decision-makers when better prediction raises the productivity of human judgment, or create new decision tasks by making previously uneconomic activities viable. Agrawal, Gans, and Goldfarb (2019b) embed the framework in formal decision-making under uncertainty, and their 2024 *Journal of Economics & Management Strategy* article traces the system-wide organizational redesign required when tasks are interdependent. The framework complements Acemoglu–Restrepo by specifying *which* tasks tend to be automated — those reducible to prediction — and which remain complementary to labor: those requiring judgment, contextual integration, or ethical evaluation.

### 2.4 Inefficient Automation: The Efficiency Perspective

A more recent theoretical development complicates the welfare analysis. Beraja and Zorzi (2025), in the *Review of Economic Studies*, show that automation can be *excessive* in equilibrium — even from an efficiency standpoint — because firms do not internalize the transitional losses borne by displaced, borrowing-constrained workers. This result is important because it supplies an efficiency rationale, not merely a distributional one, for policies that slow automation. The implication travels directly into Section 6's discussion of robot taxation.

### 2.5 Bridging the Disciplinary Divide

The peer-reviewed literature on AI and labor markets exhibits a sharp disciplinary partition. Top economics journals — the *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, *American Economic Review*, *Journal of Political Economy*, *Econometrica*, *Review of Economic Studies*, *Journal of Labor Economics* — analyze the mechanisms of task reallocation, displacement, and labor-share change without typically invoking 4IR terminology. Management, sociology, and production-economics journals — *Technological Forecasting and Social Change*, *Work, Employment and Society*, *International Journal of Production Economics*, *Structural Change and Economic Dynamics*, *Organization Studies* — adopt 4IR and Industry 4.0 framings but engage less often with formal general-equilibrium mechanisms. Neither literature is sufficient alone: mechanism without periodization misses what is distinctive about the current moment, while periodization without mechanism risks the technological determinism that Howcroft and Taylor (2023) warn against. This review's synthesis draws on both — the economics framework supplies the mechanism, the management and sociology literatures supply the periodization, the labor-process dimension, and the institutional texture.

## 3. Three Eras of Empirical Evidence

The empirical literature on AI and labor markets is usefully divided into three overlapping technology eras, each associated with a dominant set of technologies, a characteristic identification strategy, and a distinct pattern of findings. Era 1 (1990s–early 2010s) is the computerization era, during which SBTC and RBTC frameworks were developed and polarization emerged as the central empirical phenomenon. Era 2 (2010s–2022) is defined by industrial robots and narrow machine learning, and by the first generation of causal commuting-zone and establishment-level studies. Era 3 (2022–present) is the generative-AI era, characterized by large language models, rapid workplace adoption, and the first wave of field-experimental evidence. Sectoral heterogeneity is nested within each era. The theoretical architecture developed in Section 2 applies throughout; what changes across eras is the menu of tasks susceptible to automation, the identification strategies available to researchers, and the institutional mediation through which shocks are transmitted.

### 3.1 Era 1 — Computerization, SBTC, and Polarization (1990s–early 2010s)

The first era of the modern technology-labor literature was defined by the diffusion of personal computers, enterprise IT, and networked information systems. Empirical scholarship during this period consolidated around the skill-biased technological change framework before transitioning to the task-based routine-biased successor that would dominate subsequent work.

Katz and Murphy (1992) established the canonical relative-supply/relative-demand framework used to document persistent increases in the college wage premium after 1979. Autor, Katz, and Krueger (1998) extended the evidence by showing that skill upgrading proceeded faster in more computer-intensive industries, interpreting the rising college premium as a direct consequence of computerization. Bresnahan, Brynjolfsson, and Hitt (2002) deepened the account at the firm level, demonstrating that the labor-market effects of IT depended crucially on organizational complements: firms that combined IT investment with workplace reorganization and product innovation drew disproportionately on skilled labor. Their contribution established a principle that recurs in the contemporary AI literature — technology's employment effects are mediated by organizational design choices, not mechanically determined.

Two theoretical extensions during this period proved consequential for subsequent work. Acemoglu (1998) supplied a directed-technical-change model in which the equilibrium bias of innovation responded endogenously to relative skill supplies: a larger pool of college workers made skill-complementary technologies more profitable to develop, creating a self-reinforcing cycle. Acemoglu (2002) generalized the framework to show how market-size and price effects jointly determine whether technological change becomes more or less skill-biased. These contributions would later provide the formal scaffolding for Acemoglu and Restrepo's displacement-reinstatement model.

The theoretical architecture shifted decisively with Autor, Levy, and Murnane (2003), who reconceptualized jobs as bundles of tasks rather than as manifestations of skill groups. Their central claim was that computers substitute for *routine* tasks — both cognitive and manual — while complementing non-routine problem-solving and interpersonal communication. The empirical implication was striking: computerization should hollow out middle-wage occupations concentrated in routine work, producing a polarized rather than monotonically skill-biased labor market. Autor, Katz, and Kearney (2006) documented precisely this pattern in U.S. data, with employment growth at both the top and bottom of the occupational distribution alongside contraction in the middle. Goos and Manning (2007) extended the evidence to Britain, coining the phrase "lovely and lousy jobs" to describe the dual growth pattern.

The polarization literature matured through spatial and cross-national analysis. Autor and Dorn (2013) showed that U.S. commuting zones initially specialized in routine tasks adopted IT more intensively and subsequently reallocated displaced routine workers toward low-skill service jobs, producing both employment and wage polarization at the local level. Goos, Manning, and Salomons (2014) extended the framework to sixteen European countries, demonstrating that RBTC combined with offshoring accounted for a substantial share of both within-industry and between-industry components of polarization across the continent.

Autor's (2015) synthetic *Journal of Economic Perspectives* essay served as the era's capstone. Against popular framings of technological unemployment, Autor argued that jobs persisted because automation produces two countervailing effects alongside displacement: productivity gains that expand demand in non-automated tasks, and the creation of new tasks in domains where humans retain comparative advantage. The essay became the standard economics-profession response to alarmist predictions, and it set the conceptual stage for the more formal task-based models that would dominate the following era. What the computerization-era literature established, with a decade of hindsight, was both a theoretical vocabulary — skill groups, routine tasks, polarization, displacement — and an empirical benchmark: the restructuring of the 1990s and 2000s was real but gradual, institutionally mediated, and consistent with continued aggregate employment growth alongside sharpened distributional consequences.

### 3.2 Era 2 — Industrial Robots and Narrow AI (2010s–2022)

Era 2 marks the transition from theoretical framework to sustained empirical investigation of physical automation and narrow artificial intelligence. The era opens, chronologically and intellectually, with a single paper whose contribution lies as much in its provocation as in its methodology: Frey and Osborne's (2017) *Technological Forecasting and Social Change* estimate that 47 percent of U.S. employment was "at risk" of computerization over the following one to two decades. Their approach — expert-labeled probabilities of automatability applied to O*NET occupational task descriptions — was widely criticized for coarse occupation-level extrapolation, but the headline number catalyzed a decade of more rigorous exposure-measurement work (discussed in Section 3.3.1) and sharpened policy debate. The subsequent literature can be read, in part, as a disciplined response to Frey and Osborne's estimate.

#### 3.2.1 Manufacturing: Causal Identification and Meta-Analytic Limits

The strongest causal evidence on automation and labor markets in Era 2 comes from studies of industrial robots in manufacturing. Acemoglu and Restrepo's (2020) *Journal of Political Economy* analysis remains the benchmark. Exploiting variation in robot exposure across U.S. commuting zones from 1990 to 2007, they report that one additional robot per thousand workers reduced the employment-to-population ratio by 0.2 percentage points and wages by 0.42 percent. The identification strategy — instrumenting U.S. robot adoption with robot penetration in comparable European economies — is among the cleanest in the automation literature, and the commuting-zone geography captures local labor-market effects that national averages would obscure.

Two features of this result require careful framing. First, its magnitude depends on the unit of analysis: the commuting-zone effect is meaningful locally but not automatically informative about national averages. Second, the identification context — U.S. local labor markets facing robot exposure without substantial institutional buffers — represents one configuration among several. The European and cross-national evidence diverges significantly. Graetz and Michaels (2018), in a seventeen-country panel, find that robot adoption raised labor productivity by roughly 0.36 percentage points annually and lowered output prices, without reducing aggregate employment, though the employment share of low-skill workers did decline. Dauth, Findeisen, Suedekum, and Woessner (2021) provide what may be the clearest institutional-mediation result in the field: in German data, manufacturing displacement from robots was fully offset by service-sector job creation, with the burden falling disproportionately on young labor-market entrants while incumbent workers often remained employed through task-redeployment within the same plants. The German pattern is not accidental; it reflects coordinated bargaining, works councils, and the dual vocational-education system that together transform what a U.S. commuting-zone framing would record as displacement into within-firm reallocation.

Two recent meta-analyses sharpen this heterogeneity picture. Guarascio, Piccirillo, and Reljic (2025), pooling 644 employment estimates from 33 studies and 195 wage estimates from 19 studies in the *Journal of Economic Surveys*, find that the average association between robotization and either employment or wages is statistically close to zero. Jurkat, Klump, and Schneider (2025), using 2,468 wage estimates from 55 papers in *Structural Change and Economic Dynamics*, likewise report that the overall robot wage effect is statistically insignificant and economically near zero, with heterogeneity driven primarily by country selection, control variables, aggregation level, and functional form. Both papers document preferential publication selection for negative results in U.S.-focused studies specifically. The meta-analytic evidence does not invalidate Acemoglu and Restrepo's well-identified commuting-zone result; it situates that result as the upper bound of a literature whose average effect is substantially smaller and whose variance is driven systematically by context.

Firm-level evidence adds further complication. Dixon, Hong, and Wu (2021), working in *Management Science*, find that robot adoption at the firm level was associated with *higher* total employment, flatter managerial hierarchies, and compositional shifts away from middle-skill toward both low- and high-skill work. Acemoglu, Lelarge, and Restrepo (2020), studying French manufacturers, find that robot adopters experienced declines in their labor shares and expanded at the expense of competitors — a firm-level reallocation consistent with the superstar-firm mechanism Autor, Dorn, Katz, Patterson, and Van Reenen (2020) later documented at the industry level.

China supplies the clearest demonstration that level of analysis materially shapes interpretation. Firm-level Chinese studies by Wang, Zhang, and Liu (2024) in *China Economic Review* and Tang, Huang, and Liu (2021) in *Economics Letters* find that robot adoption *increased* employment at adopting firms, particularly for high-skilled and highly educated workers, driven by productivity effects that expanded demand. Yet Giuntella, Lu, and Wang (2025), in the *Economic Journal*, using worker-level data, find that a one-standard-deviation rise in robot exposure reduced labor-force participation, employment, and hourly earnings, while accelerating uptake of technical training and earlier retirement. The parallel is not a contradiction but a demonstration: firm-level aggregates can show growth while exposed individual workers face measurable losses. Choosing the firm, the worker, the region, or the nation as the unit of observation is not an innocent methodological decision but a substantive analytical commitment.

#### 3.2.2 Narrow AI Diffusion and Vacancy-Based Evidence

By the mid-2010s, narrow machine-learning applications had begun to diffuse beyond manufacturing into services, finance, and professional work. The empirical challenge was distinguishing AI's effects from those of broader ICT adoption. Alekseeva, Azar, Giné, Samila, and Taska (2021), analyzing millions of U.S. online job vacancies from 2010 to 2019 in *Labour Economics*, documented a sharp rise in AI-skill demand concentrated not only in IT but also in professional services, administrative support, and finance. They estimated an 11 percent within-firm wage premium and a 5 percent within-job-title premium for AI-skilled positions, with the largest premia accruing to management occupations — a pattern consistent with AI acting as a complement to managerial judgment rather than a substitute for it. AI-intensive firms also paid higher wages in their non-AI roles, and AI skills appeared to complement software, cognitive, social, project-management, and people-management skills while substituting for customer-service skills.

Acemoglu, Autor, Hazell, and Restrepo's (2022) *Journal of Labor Economics* establishment-level study provides a more cautious picture. Establishments that expanded AI-related vacancies reduced hiring in non-AI positions and changed the skill requirements of remaining jobs, yet the aggregate effects on industry- or occupation-level employment and wages were too small to detect in the period studied. The result anticipates what has since become a defining feature of the AI-labor literature: strong effects at one level of observation — the firm, the establishment, the individual — can attenuate substantially when aggregated to national or industry scale. Two mechanisms plausibly explain the attenuation: reallocation within industries toward AI-adopting firms, and reinstatement effects as new AI-complementary tasks emerge.

Demireva (2026), writing in *Industry and Innovation*, offers a recent contribution that shifts the analytical lens from AI patents or vacancies to commercial AI product deployments. Building occupational exposure from actual AI product launches rather than theoretical capability overlap, she finds that information-processing and communication-heavy jobs top the exposure rankings — a result consistent with later generative-AI findings — but that exposure is positively correlated with wage premia and negatively correlated with unemployment, a pattern driven primarily by augmentation rather than substitution. Demireva's contribution complicates a simple "exposure implies risk" reading: the commercial deployments observed to date have leaned toward augmenting exposed occupations rather than displacing them.

Taken together, the narrow-AI evidence of Era 2 supports three provisional conclusions. AI-skill demand rose sharply and was rewarded with meaningful wage premia concentrated where AI complements existing human capital. Establishment-level effects on non-AI hiring existed but did not yet aggregate to detectable industry-level displacement. And the realized pattern of adoption pointed toward augmentation — foreshadowing the more explicit augmentation evidence that would follow in Era 3 with generative models.

#### 3.2.3 Sectoral Heterogeneity in Era 2

Sectoral patterns in Era 2 differ sharply from the manufacturing-dominated robot narrative. Finance emerged as one of the most AI-skill-intensive sectors by vacancy data, with wage premia for AI-complementary roles among the largest in Alekseeva et al.'s estimates. Yet no peer-reviewed study in the period identified aggregate employment contraction in finance attributable to AI adoption; the sectoral signature was one of skill-biased demand growth and wage-premium formation. Acemoglu, Autor, Hazell, and Restrepo's (2022) establishment-level evidence reinforced this reading: finance establishments adopting AI reduced non-AI hiring at the margin without producing measurable industry-level displacement. The finance sector of Era 2 is therefore best characterized as a site of reorganization and upskilling rather than contraction.

Healthcare during this period saw the diffusion of diagnostic machine learning — image classification in radiology and pathology, risk-prediction models in clinical operations — alongside early administrative automation. The peer-reviewed labor-market evidence on pre-generative-AI healthcare is thin, in part because diagnostic ML was deployed as a decision-support tool rather than as a headcount-affecting technology. The sector's more decisive AI-labor evidence would arrive only in Era 3, with generative scribes and documentation assistants discussed in Section 3.3.5.

Professional services — consulting, legal work, accounting, and business analytics — showed rising AI-skill demand throughout the late 2010s without evidence of aggregate employment decline. The pattern mirrored finance: measured adoption was concentrated at the skill- and task-level, creating wage premia for AI-complementary work without hollowing out the sector. The decisive evidence on this sector would likewise emerge in Era 3, when field experiments with generative AI tools produced the first clean causal estimates of productivity effects and their distributional consequences.

Across the three sectors, Era 2 displays a consistent pattern. Where AI was deployed, demand rose for workers with AI-complementary skills and wages responded; where displacement occurred, it remained largely confined to manufacturing robotics and was mediated by national institutions. The absence of sharp sector-specific displacement in services during this period matters both for interpreting Era 3 — where generative AI disrupts this apparent stability — and for the broader argument that AI's labor-market effects are not technology-determined but depend on which technologies diffuse in which sectors under which institutional conditions.

### 3.3 Era 3 — Generative AI and Large Language Models (2022–present)

The generative AI era opens with ChatGPT's public release in November 2022. Within thirty-six months, the peer-reviewed labor-market literature on large language models has grown from a handful of provocative exposure estimates to a substantial body of field-experimental, observational, and administrative-data evidence. Four empirical veins have emerged — exposure measurement, within-firm productivity, platform displacement, and population-scale effects — with sectoral heterogeneity cutting across all four.

#### 3.3.1 Exposure Methodologies and Their Peer-Reviewed Critiques

The empirical exposure literature in fact predates generative AI. Webb (2019), in a widely cited Stanford working paper, introduced a patent-task overlap method: matching verb-noun pairs extracted from the titles of AI, robot, and software patents to the task descriptions in O*NET, with occupations scored by overlap frequency. His AI rankings placed technical-monitoring occupations — clinical laboratory technicians, chemical engineers, optometrists, power plant operators, dispatchers — at the top, reflecting the task patterns of AI patents through 2017. Because the canonical document remains a working paper, we reference Webb contextually rather than as primary evidence; its significance lies in setting the methodological template that subsequent peer-reviewed work extended and critiqued.

Felten, Raj, and Seamans (2018), in *AEA Papers and Proceedings*, took a different route. Rather than matching patents to tasks, they linked AI capability categories from the Electronic Frontier Foundation's AI Progress Measurement dataset to the fifty-two O*NET abilities, scoring occupations by the weighted prevalence of AI-relevant abilities in their task profiles. Their AI Occupational Exposure (AIOE) measure was consolidated in *Strategic Management Journal* (Felten, Raj, & Seamans, 2021) with industry- and geography-level extensions. AIOE's capability-abilities framing makes it interpretable and readily aggregated, but it has been criticized for conflating substitution and complementarity: an occupation is "exposed" in Felten, Raj, and Seamans's sense whenever its abilities overlap with AI capabilities, regardless of whether its actual tasks are automatable by the technologies those capabilities describe.

The arrival of large language models prompted a sharper instrument. Eloundou, Manning, Mishkin, and Rock (2024), publishing in *Science*, constructed an LLM-specific rubric: a task is "exposed" if an LLM or LLM-powered software could reduce completion time by at least fifty percent while maintaining equivalent quality. They operationalized three nested measures — direct LLM exposure, direct plus partial weight on complementary-software exposure, and a full upper-bound exposure including complementary software — with ratings produced by both human annotators and GPT-4 itself. Their headline estimates are that approximately 1.8 percent of U.S. jobs could have more than half of their tasks affected by LLMs alone, rising to just over 46 percent when complementary software is included. Exposure concentrates in information-processing and text-intensive occupations: writers, translators, proofreaders, mathematicians, public relations specialists, court reporters, legal secretaries, and accountants.

Septiandri, Constantinides, and Quercia (2024), in *PNAS Nexus*, introduce an Artificial Intelligence Impact (AII) index using deep-learning matching between 24,758 AI patents and 17,879 occupational tasks. They explicitly critique Webb's verb-noun approach for producing false matches — patent-task pairs that should not be matched — and argue the classical method can overestimate AI's impact. Their deep-learning classification produces rankings that differ meaningfully: cardiovascular technologists, sound engineering technicians, nuclear medicine technologists, and air traffic controllers appear near the top, reflecting patent patterns that verb-noun matching missed.

The peer-reviewed critique literature surrounding these indices is itself central. Frank, Ahn, and Moro (2025), in *PNAS Nexus*, evaluate Webb, Felten-Raj-Seamans, Eloundou, and several other exposure measures against 2010–2020 occupation-state-time unemployment insurance data and find that no single index adequately predicts unemployment risk, job separations, or skill change. Felten's AIOE was most associated with job separations; Webb's robot exposure best predicted unemployment rates; but the strongest predictive performance came from an ensemble combining multiple indices. Williamson and colleagues (2025), in the *Journal for Labour Market Research*, offer a parallel critique of AIOE's agnosticism between substitution and complementarity and propose a complementarity-adjusted extension incorporating work-context and job-zone variables.

The collective lesson is methodological humility. Different exposure measures rank occupations differently because they encode different theories of what "AI exposure" means: capability-abilities overlap in AIOE; patent-task overlap with different matching technologies in Webb and Septiandri; realized LLM capability against a time-savings rubric in Eloundou and colleagues. None is obviously wrong; none is obviously sufficient. High exposure in any single index should therefore be treated as indicative rather than definitive — a caution that proves important when interpreting the realized-outcome evidence that follows.

#### 3.3.2 Within-Firm Adoption and Productivity Evidence

The first rigorous experimental evidence on generative-AI productivity in the workplace came from Noy and Zhang (2023), whose *Science* study of 453 college-educated professionals randomized access to ChatGPT on occupationally realistic writing tasks — press releases, short reports, analysis plans, delicate emails. Access to ChatGPT reduced completion time by approximately forty percent while simultaneously raising output quality as graded by blind evaluators. Productivity gains were larger for workers who had produced lower-quality drafts before the experiment than for those who had produced higher-quality ones, yielding measurable skill compression within the sample. Noy and Zhang's experiment established the template that subsequent field-experimental work would extend: treatment-control designs on occupationally realistic tasks, joint measurement of productivity and quality, and explicit attention to heterogeneity by baseline ability.

Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond (2025), in the *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, carried the experimental paradigm into a high-stakes production setting. Studying the staggered introduction of a GPT-3-based assistant to 5,172 customer support agents at a Fortune 500 software firm, they find a fifteen percent average increase in resolved issues per hour, with gains sharply concentrated among less-experienced and lower-skill workers: the lowest-skill quintile saw productivity improvements of approximately thirty percent. The assistant also improved customer sentiment and reduced attrition, particularly among newer hires — a finding that complicates the narrative of AI as uniformly worker-hostile. Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond are careful to note that their paper does not estimate aggregate employment effects; within-firm productivity gains do not mechanically translate into sectoral labor demand shifts.

Humlum and Vestergaard (2025), in *PNAS*, extend the evidence to administrative-register-linked survey data. Surveying 18,000 workers across eleven ChatGPT-exposed occupations in Denmark, they document widespread adoption — but with systematic inequalities. Women were sixteen percentage points less likely than men to have used ChatGPT for work; younger and less-experienced workers adopted more heavily; ChatGPT users had slightly higher pre-ChatGPT earnings conditional on tenure. Adoption was constrained by employer restrictions and training needs rather than lack of interest. The pattern Humlum and Vestergaard document — widespread exposure with unequal adoption — implies that the technology's distributional effects depend not only on who is exposed but on who actually uses what they have access to.

Dell'Acqua, McFowland, Mollick, and colleagues (2026), in *Organization Science*, provide the most granular field-experimental evidence on high-skilled knowledge work. In a pre-registered experiment with 758 Boston Consulting Group consultants randomized across GPT-4 access and task types, consultants with AI access completed 12.2 percent more tasks and were 25.1 percent faster on tasks within the model's capability frontier, with higher-quality output. But on tasks outside that frontier, AI access actively worsened performance. The authors coin the term "jagged frontier" to describe AI capability as non-monotonic in task difficulty: sharp, unpredictable edges divide tasks where AI helps from those where it hurts. The implication travels directly into Section 5.6.

Cui, Demirer, Jaffe, Musolff, Peng, and Salz (2026), in *Management Science*, extend the field-experimental paradigm to software development. Pooling three randomized experiments at Microsoft, Accenture, and a Fortune 100 firm covering 4,867 developers, they find a 26.08 percent increase in completed tasks with access to an AI coding assistant, with larger gains and higher take-up among less-experienced developers. The skill-compression pattern from Noy and Zhang, Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond, and Dell'Acqua and colleagues replicates: gains concentrate on the lower end of the skill distribution.

Bick, Blandin, and Deming (2026), in *Management Science*, document the diffusion pattern from the demand side. Using nationally representative U.S. surveys through late 2024, they find that twenty-seven percent of employed Americans had used generative AI for work in the prior week — ten percent every workday — with approximately one to seven percent of all work hours assisted by generative AI and reported time savings of 1.4 percent of total work hours. Within twenty-four months of ChatGPT's release, generative AI had achieved rapid workplace penetration, yet aggregate employment effects over the same period remain undetectable in national data, a tension addressed directly in Section 3.3.4.

The within-firm and experimental evidence of Era 3 is therefore consistent across settings. Generative AI produces measurable productivity gains, concentrated among less-experienced and lower-skill workers, with jagged rather than monotonic gradients across tasks. Augmentation dominates. The displacement evidence, as the next subsection shows, appears elsewhere.

#### 3.3.3 Displacement on Open Platforms

The sharpest realized-displacement evidence of Era 3 comes not from within-firm studies but from online freelance platforms — markets where tasks are modular, contracts are short-term, and no internal redeployment buffer protects incumbents. Hui, Reshef, and Zhou (2024), in *Organization Science*, analyze an online freelance platform before and after the release of ChatGPT and image-generation models. Workers in highly affected occupations experienced measurable declines in both employment and earnings, and prior high performance did not insulate top freelancers; if anything, highly rated workers were disproportionately exposed.

Demirer, Hannane, and Zhu (2025), in *Management Science*, extend the platform evidence with broader data. Using scraped listings from a major global freelance platform, they document a twenty-one percent decline in job posts for writing and coding work within eight months of ChatGPT's release, and a seventeen percent decline in image-creation postings following the diffusion of image-generation models. Competition among remaining freelancers intensified, and the composition of available jobs shifted toward more complex, higher-paid work — consistent with a hollowing-out of the lower end of the freelance task distribution.

Teutloff and colleagues (2025), writing in the *Journal of Economic Behavior and Organization*, add a task-clustering dimension. Using BERTopic clustering on over three million online job postings, they classify skill clusters as substitutable, complementary, or unaffected by LLMs. Substitutable clusters, accounting for approximately ten percent of postings, saw demand fall by approximately twenty-four percent after ChatGPT's release, with especially sharp declines in short-term gigs. Complementary clusters — notably chatbot development and machine-learning integration work — grew over the same period, consistent with task-level reinstatement effects.

The platform evidence differs systematically from the within-firm evidence of Section 3.3.2 not because the technologies or workers differ but because the institutional setting does. Freelance platforms lack internal labor markets, employment protection, notice periods, and the within-firm redeployment channels that absorbed automation shocks in Dauth, Findeisen, Suedekum, and Woessner's German data. They are, in this sense, the closest empirical approximation to unconstrained labor-market adjustment available — and what they reveal is that unconstrained adjustment to generative AI produces substantial displacement on precisely the tasks the within-firm evidence shows augmentation dominating. The paradox this generates is the subject of Section 5.2.

#### 3.3.4 The Null at National Scale

Kauhanen and Rouvinen (2025), in *Applied Economics Letters*, conduct the most expansive population-level test of generative-AI labor effects available to date. Using administrative data covering all wage earners in Finland for two years following ChatGPT's November 2022 launch, they compare wage and employment trajectories between more- and less-exposed occupations identified through LLM-exposure measures. Their finding is unambiguous: no statistically significant differences in wage or employment changes can be detected between high- and low-exposure occupational groups. Within high-exposure major groups, wage effects were positive but statistically insignificant; employment estimates were negative for professionals and positive for technicians, with neither reaching significance.

The Kauhanen and Rouvinen null does not invalidate the platform evidence or the within-firm productivity findings. It pushes back against their direct aggregation. Two interpretations are compatible with the evidence. First, institutional buffers — Finnish employment protection, coordinated wage-setting, active labor market policy — transmit exposure shocks more slowly than freelance platforms do. Second, two years is a short horizon for labor-market adjustment; the Finnish null is a two-year finding, not a long-run equilibrium. Both interpretations suggest that population-scale displacement from generative AI, if it is coming, is not yet visible in high-quality administrative data from a representative OECD economy.

#### 3.3.5 Sectoral Evidence in Era 3

Sectoral evidence in Era 3 clusters around augmentation. In healthcare, Pearlman and colleagues (2025), writing in *JAMA Network Open*, document that AI-scribe adoption among clinicians reduced total electronic health record time and time in notes, with gains concentrated among female clinicians, primary care physicians, and medical subspecialists. The study found no significant changes in after-hours documentation, appointment length, or appointment volume: AI scribes eased administrative burden without detectable effects on clinical labor demand. Rotenstein and colleagues (2026), in *JAMA*, report consistent results from a multicenter study across five academic medical centers, with lower documentation time and a small increase in visits per week — again augmentation rather than displacement. Pelletier, Watson, Michel, McGregor, and Rush (2025), in *JAMIA Open*, find that generative-AI digital scribes reduced documentation time, cognitive burden, and provider burnout with neutral effects on note quality.

In education, Mulyani and colleagues (2025), in *Cogent Education*, surveyed 466 teachers and used structural equation modeling to show that generative-AI use was associated with improved teaching performance through the mechanisms of ease of use, perceived usefulness, and learning. Effects concentrated in lesson-material development, student-focused learning, and teaching-practice enhancement. A complementary study by Ding, Hu, Kim, and Chen (2026), in *Sustainability*, on university design teachers documents dual pathways — empowerment and depletion — in which the same technology can reduce routine task burdens while introducing new verification, monitoring, and emotional labor demands.

The high-skilled knowledge sectors already covered in Section 3.3.2 — consulting in Dell'Acqua and colleagues (2026), software development in Cui and colleagues (2026), and customer service in Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond (2025) — complete the sectoral picture. The cross-sector pattern is striking: wherever generative AI has been studied within bounded firm or professional contexts, augmentation dominates; realized displacement appears primarily where institutional buffers are absent.

### 3.4 Cross-Sector Synthesis

The three eras of empirical evidence traversed in Sections 3.1 through 3.3 document a sharply heterogeneous set of labor-market outcomes. Different technologies have affected different sectors through different channels, and the dominant mechanism shifts as institutional settings, task modularity, and adoption patterns vary. Table 1 summarizes the cross-sector evidence by mapping each sector to the technology generation that has most shaped its restructuring, the leading finding from the peer-reviewed literature, and the anchor studies.

## 4. Cross-Cutting Dimensions

### 4.1 Wages, Employment, and the Labor Share

The cross-cutting dimensions developed in this section are not separable from the era-by-era evidence of Section 3; they are the analytical axes along which that evidence is distributed. A first axis concerns the distinct but often conflated outcomes labor markets can experience. Employment — the extensive margin — tracks who holds jobs; wages, hours, and within-job composition — the intensive margin — track what they earn and how their work is organized. A third outcome, more macroeconomic in character, is the functional distribution of income between labor and capital, captured by the labor share.

The peer-reviewed literature on the labor share combines two principal channels. Karabarbounis and Neiman (2014), writing in the *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, argue that the global decline in labor's share since 1980 reflects falling relative prices of investment goods — most saliently ICT capital — which induced substitution from labor toward capital, explaining roughly half of the observed decline across countries and industries. Autor, Dorn, Katz, Patterson, and Van Reenen (2020), in the same journal, document a complementary mechanism: the aggregate decline in U.S. labor shares is driven primarily by between-firm reallocation toward a small set of "superstar" firms with high markups and low labor shares, rather than by a broad fall in the average firm's labor share.

Two *Review of Economic Dynamics* papers sharpen the ICT mechanism empirically. Eden and Gaggl (2018) estimate that automation can account for roughly half of the U.S. labor-share decline and a substantial share of the redistribution of labor income away from routine occupations. Carbonero, Offermanns, and Weber (2023) extend the analysis to eight European countries and the United States, attributing more than half of the labor-share decline specifically to ICT-labor substitution rather than generic capital deepening, with stronger effects where routine task shares are larger.

The most direct AI-to-labor-share evidence has arrived only recently. Minniti, Prettner, and Venturini (2025), in the *European Economic Review*, estimate that a doubling of regional AI innovation lowers the labor share by 0.5 to 1.6 percent across European regions, with wage compression — particularly for medium- and high-skill workers — as the principal mechanism. The theoretical foundation is Acemoglu and Restrepo's (2018) *American Economic Review* model, in which automation mechanically lowers the labor share in the static case; Acemoglu and Restrepo (2022b), in *Econometrica*, quantify task-displacement as accounting for between fifty and seventy percent of the rise in U.S. wage inequality since 1980. A labor market can therefore preserve employment while reallocating income away from labor — a form of restructuring that the extensive margin alone does not reveal.

### 4.2 Distributional Effects, Inequality, and Gender

Distributional effects have been central to the AI-labor literature since the polarization findings of Era 1 (Autor & Dorn, 2013; Goos, Manning, & Salomons, 2014). Two shifts distinguish contemporary distributional analysis. First, the task-displacement framework of Acemoglu and Restrepo (2022b) attributes a majority of the rise in U.S. wage inequality to automation-driven reallocation between worker groups. Second, and more striking, the demographic incidence of AI exposure has begun to invert — a pattern particularly visible along gender lines in the generative-AI era.

The peer-reviewed gender evidence is consistent across settings. Humlum and Vestergaard (2025), in *PNAS*, document a sixteen percentage-point gap in ChatGPT work-adoption between men and women in Denmark across eleven exposed occupations, with adoption higher among younger and less-experienced workers. Aldasoro, Armantier, Doerr, Gambacorta, and Oliviero (2024), analyzing the New York Federal Reserve's Survey of Consumer Expectations in *Economics Letters*, find fifty percent of men but only thirty-seven percent of women report using generative AI, with self-assessed knowledge, privacy concerns, and trust emerging as primary drivers rather than age or education.

The productivity consequences of this adoption gap are beginning to surface. Tang, Li, Hu, Zeng, and Du (2025), in *PNAS Nexus*, analyze 669,284 researcher-observations and report that male researchers experienced a 6.4 percent greater increase in productivity than female researchers after ChatGPT's launch. Dorta-González, López-Puig, Dorta-González, and González-Betancor (2024), in *Telematics and Informatics*, find that female and advanced-career researchers — the latter using generative AI roughly nineteen percent less than early-career peers — exhibit the lowest adoption rates, compounding gender and career-stage disparities. Williamson and colleagues (2025), in the *Journal for Labour Market Research*, find that 76 percent of Irish women versus 51 percent of men occupy highly exposed roles — a structural exposure pattern that, combined with lower adoption, amounts to what may be a double disadvantage.

Evidence on race and ethnicity remains thinner and largely confined to the broader automation literature. Petersen, Chowhan, Cooke, Gosine, and Warrian (2023), in *Economic and Industrial Democracy*, find that visible minorities are overrepresented in precarious jobs with higher automation exposure in Canadian data. Comparable peer-reviewed race-specific evidence on generative AI — measured at occupational, firm, or worker level — remains a notable gap. That the exposure and adoption patterns documented for gender are established findings while their translation into realized wage-distribution outcomes is not yet measured is a distinction that matters for Section 5.7's analytical claims.

### 4.3 Labor Process, Autonomy, and Algorithmic Management

The dimensions considered to this point — employment, wages, the labor share, distributional incidence — are the outcomes standard economic metrics can see. A separate tradition in sociology, industrial relations, and the sociology of work examines a different dimension of restructuring that those metrics largely miss: the labor-process dimension of autonomy, discretion, surveillance, task fragmentation, and managerial control. Worker well-being can deteriorate, or improve, without any change in employment counts or wages. Recent peer-reviewed scholarship in this tradition establishes that the AI era's labor-process effects are substantial, variable, and endogenous to design choices that economic models treat as black-boxed.

Cirillo, Rinaldini, Staccioli, and Virgillito (2021), in *Structural Change and Economic Dynamics*, provide the reference case for Industry 4.0's autonomy effects. Case studies in Italian automotive plants find that the main trajectory of digitalization was neither outright automation nor worker empowerment but intensified managerial control: Industry 4.0 technologies were deployed to enforce a lean, demand-led production regime that reduced worker autonomy while preserving employment levels. Krzywdzinski, Evers, and Gerber (2024), in the *ILR Review*, extend the evidence through case work at sixteen German workplaces using wearable devices. Their central finding is that labor-process effects depend on whether settings are labor-intensive or capital-intensive: in the former, wearables tended to standardize and simplify work, reducing task variety; in the latter, they expanded skill requirements but intensified work pace. The technology was the same; the labor-process outcome was not. Dornelles, Ayala, and Frank's (2022) systematic review of eighty articles documents that Industry 4.0 technologies can also augment workers' capabilities across assembly, maintenance, planning, and design activities, though with mixed outcomes depending on implementation. Frank, Sturgeon, Benitez, Marodin, and Cunha (2025), in the *International Journal of Production Economics*, quantify this variability across 415 Brazilian manufacturers: worker outcomes depend not on the technology adopted but on how socio-technical design modifies worker activities alongside the technology.

The algorithmic-management and platform-work literatures offer the starkest evidence of labor-process restructuring. Wood, Graham, Lehdonvirta, and Hjorth (2019), in *Work, Employment and Society*, surveyed 679 and interviewed 107 platform workers across six countries and document a characteristic duality: flexibility, autonomy over hours, and task variety coexist with low pay, social isolation, overwork, and opaque algorithmic control. Bucher, Schou, and Waldkirch (2021), in *Organization*, analyze 12,294 freelancer-community posts and identify "anticipatory compliance" — extra cognitive, social, and emotional work aimed at "pacifying" the algorithm — as a mechanism by which managerial control reproduces itself through workers' own efforts to remain visible and unsanctioned. Newlands (2021), in *Organization Studies*, reframes platform surveillance through Lefebvre's concept of conceived space, arguing that algorithmic management privileges a datafied abstraction over the lived realities of work. Schor, Tirrell, and Vallas (2024), in *Work, Employment and Society*, interview seventy ride-hail, grocery, and food-delivery workers and develop a theory of "differential embeddedness" to explain variation in worker orientations toward platform risk; they show that platform structures also expose workers to customer fraud, harassment, and assault, so managerial control is effectively dispersed across platform design and customer power. Van Zoonen, von Bonsdorff, and van der Heijden (2025), in *Human Relations*, use three-wave panel data on 435 European crowdworkers and find that perceived algorithmic surveillance undermines trust and fairness, raises privacy concerns, and shapes whether workers comply, resist, or exit — surveillance as legitimacy problem, not merely technical monitoring. Alacovska, Bucher, and Fieseler (2024), in *Work, Employment and Society*, show that even creative freelancers on Upwork and Fiverr reclaim autonomy through relational work with clients, though at the cost of additional emotional labor.

Across this literature, four conclusions hold. Autonomy in algorithmic environments is real but bounded, typically concerning work scheduling or task selection while core managerial power is retained through ranking, matching, and evaluation systems. Control is displaced from visible supervisors to dashboards, making contestation harder. Deskilling is common but not universal; sector, production system, and design choices determine the outcome. Workers are not passive subjects but respond through anticipatory compliance, out-of-app activity, relational work, and collective action. The labor-process dimension therefore operates as a co-equal axis of restructuring alongside employment and wages — a claim the economics literature has only begun to engage.

### 4.4 Institutional Mediation Across Levels

The cross-sector heterogeneity of Section 3 and the institutional variation documented throughout this section can be organized around a single analytical axis: the level at which technological shocks are absorbed. Five levels warrant separate treatment. At the platform level, demonstrated most clearly in the generative-AI freelance evidence, adjustment is least buffered: short-term contracts, rating systems, and substitutable tasks produce direct demand contractions of seventeen to twenty-four percent in exposed clusters (Hui et al., 2024; Demirer et al., 2025; Teutloff et al., 2025). At the firm level, internal labor markets, task redeployment, and organizational hierarchies absorb shocks that would otherwise appear as employment losses — a mechanism visible in Dauth et al.'s (2021) German data and in Dixon, Hong, and Wu's (2021) firm-level employment gains under robot adoption. At the regional level, commuting-zone dynamics operate through local labor-market networks (Acemoglu & Restrepo, 2020). At the national level, active labor-market policy, coordinated bargaining, and employment protection shape what any of these lower levels ultimately transmit. At the informal-sector level, documented primarily in Global South labor markets, informality itself functions as a distinctive absorption mechanism.

Comparative evidence within Europe substantiates the mediating role of national institutions. Leibrecht, Scharler, and Zhoufu (2023), in *Structural Change and Economic Dynamics*, study thirty-seven OECD countries between 2005 and 2019 and find that automation's association with unemployment is substantially weaker in countries with strong collective bargaining, particularly for medium-skilled workers. Chen and Frey (2024), in *Industrial and Corporate Change*, comparing eight European countries, find that robot exposure reduced employment in Italy, Norway, and the United Kingdom, but not significantly in Germany, Denmark, Finland, or Spain — a pattern the authors attribute to labor-market characteristics, complementary job-creating investments, and reshoring channels. The German case anchors the institutional-mediation literature. Dauth, Findeisen, Suedekum, and Woessner's (2021) full-offset result reflects the operation of coordinated bargaining, works councils, and the dual vocational-education system. Bosch and Schmitz-Kießler (2020), in *Transfer: European Review of Labour and Research*, document how German trade unions shaped Industry 4.0 implementation through social dialogue in the *Arbeit 2020* initiative. Krzywdzinski, Gerst, and Butollo (2023), in the same journal, extend the analysis to AI specifically, finding that German unions and works councils actively pursue human-centered design, worker-data protection, and anti-deskilling safeguards — leverage expanded by the 2021 Works Council Modernisation Act. These are not peripheral features of the German labor market; they are the mechanisms through which the same technology produces different outcomes in Germany than in otherwise-comparable U.S. commuting zones.

The Global South literature documents a qualitatively different absorption mechanism. Brambilla, César, Falcone, and Gasparini (2023), in *World Development*, analyze robot adoption in Argentina, Brazil, and Mexico between 2004 and 2016 and find that robots primarily displaced formal salaried jobs while the informal sector absorbed displaced workers, preventing the full open-unemployment response visible in U.S. commuting zones; young and semi-skilled workers bore the heaviest burden, and wages were not the principal adjustment margin. El Mokri, El Abbassi, and Ibourk (2024), in *The Economic and Labour Relations Review*, argue that premature deindustrialization combined with pervasive informality produces adjustment dynamics in Morocco that Global North polarization models cannot capture. Poudel and Gopinath (2026), in the *Journal of Asian Economics*, find that digitalization in Indian manufacturing has shifted self-employment toward salaried employment, consistent with ICT-labor complementarities in a developing-country context. Parschau and Hauge's (2020) *Geoforum* analysis of South African apparel manufacturing shows that automation's employment effects operate across regional production networks — shifting jobs across borders rather than necessarily eliminating them. Informality is therefore not the absence of institutional response but a distinct institutional form — one whose analytical treatment remains underdeveloped in the mainstream labor economics literature and whose implications are taken up in Section 5.8.

## 5. Contested Debates and Novel Insights

The heterogeneous evidence assembled in Sections 3 and 4 invites syntheses that no single paper in the peer-reviewed literature articulates. This section develops eight such syntheses. Each is epistemically tagged to distinguish what the evidence has established from what it suggests and what we propose as analytical hypothesis: *[Established Finding]* for claims supported by multiple replicated peer-reviewed studies; *[Emerging Pattern]* for findings consistent across recent evidence but lacking long-horizon confirmation; *[Conceptual Argument]* for theoretical claims whose empirical test remains underdeveloped; and *[Analytical Hypothesis / Our Synthesis]* for extrapolations we introduce rather than find. The convention is not cosmetic: a PhD-grade literature review must distinguish what the field knows from what the field has begun to suggest, and from what we ourselves argue.

### 5.1 The Inversion of Exposure: From Routine to Cognitive

*[Emerging Pattern]*

The routine-biased technological change framework (Autor, Levy, & Murnane, 2003) positioned non-routine cognitive tasks — analytical problem-solving, complex communication, interpersonal judgment — as the "safe harbor" from automation. The empirical architecture of the past two decades depended on this taxonomy: middle-wage routine jobs hollowed out while non-routine cognitive work at the top and non-routine manual work at the bottom expanded. Generative AI inverts this pattern. Eloundou, Manning, Mishkin, and Rock's (2024) exposure rankings place text-intensive cognitive occupations — writers, translators, proofreaders, mathematicians, public relations specialists, accountants, legal secretaries — at the top. Demireva's (2026) commercial-deployment exposure measure converges: information-processing and communication-heavy jobs are most affected.

The implication extends beyond empirical update. The routine/non-routine axis was built on the assumption that non-routine tasks resisted codification. Large language models demonstrate that many cognitive tasks previously considered non-codifiable are, in fact, pattern-learnable from large text corpora — not because those tasks are mechanical but because the relevant patterns are present in the data on which models train. The taxonomy therefore requires revision. A more defensible axis for the generative-AI era may be *pattern-predictable from training corpora* versus *requiring embodied experience or contextual integration*. This is a distinct analytical partition from routine versus non-routine, and it cuts across the earlier taxonomy in ways the peer-reviewed literature is only beginning to work out.

### 5.2 The Freelance-Population Paradox

*[Established Finding + Our Synthesis]*

Two well-identified findings sit in apparent tension. Platform-level evidence documents substantial realized displacement: Hui, Reshef, and Zhou (2024), Demirer, Hannane, and Zhu (2025), and Teutloff and colleagues (2025) report demand contractions of seventeen to twenty-four percent in exposed freelance clusters within months of ChatGPT's release. Population-level evidence shows no detectable effect: Kauhanen and Rouvinen's (2025) Finnish analysis, covering all wage earners for two years, finds no statistically significant wage or employment differences between more- and less-exposed occupations.

Our synthesis is that these findings are not in tension; they document institutional mediation at work. Platforms lack internal labor markets, employment protection, notice periods, and the within-firm redeployment channels documented in Dauth, Findeisen, Suedekum, and Woessner's (2021) German data. Freelance platforms are the closest empirical approximation to unconstrained labor-market adjustment available — what they reveal is what national labor markets *would* transmit without institutional buffers. The policy implication is direct: labor-market-flexibility advocates who argue for weakening employment protection to accelerate reallocation are arguing, in effect, for moving aggregate labor markets closer to the freelance-platform baseline. The peer-reviewed evidence on what that baseline looks like under generative-AI exposure suggests the costs of such reallocation would be borne quickly and disproportionately by exposed workers.

### 5.3 Augmentation as Skill Compression

*[Emerging Pattern for short-run; Analytical Hypothesis for long-run erosion]*

Four field experiments now document the same short-run pattern: generative-AI productivity gains concentrate among less-experienced and lower-skill workers. Noy and Zhang (2023) found larger gains for workers whose pre-experiment drafts were lower quality. Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond (2025) report thirty percent productivity gains for the lowest-skill quintile of customer service agents versus fifteen percent on average. Dell'Acqua, McFowland, Mollick, and colleagues (2026) document in-frontier compression across 758 consultants. Cui, Demirer, Jaffe, Musolff, Peng, and Salz (2026) find larger gains and higher take-up among less-experienced developers across 4,867 observations.

The short-run compression is empirical fact. We propose, as analytical hypothesis, that it implies a long-run erosion of the economic rent associated with experience and expertise. If an inexperienced worker with AI can match an experienced worker's output, firms face altered incentives along four margins: hiring junior workers at lower wages, compressing internal wage ladders, reducing investment in worker training (since experience becomes less valuable), and weakening the bargaining position of senior workers. No peer-reviewed study has yet tested these long-run predictions. But the logic is direct: what appears as equity gain in short-run productivity data may be the leading edge of a longer-run decline in returns to human-capital accumulation — a possibility whose empirical test will require data horizons not yet available.

### 5.4 The Autonomy-Employment Distinction

*[Established Finding]*

Section 4.3 assembles peer-reviewed evidence that labor-process restructuring operates as a distinct axis from employment and wages. The synthesis we add here concerns measurement. Standard labor-market metrics — employment counts, hourly wages, labor-force participation — are blind to autonomy erosion, surveillance intensification, deskilling, task fragmentation, and algorithmic management. A labor market can therefore be "robust" by the metrics the economics profession routinely tracks while simultaneously producing meaningful welfare losses.

The peer-reviewed evidence in Cirillo, Rinaldini, Staccioli, and Virgillito (2021) on reduced autonomy in Italian auto plants under Industry 4.0, Krzywdzinski, Evers, and Gerber (2024) on wearable devices intensifying work in capital-intensive German plants, Bucher, Schou, and Waldkirch (2021) on anticipatory compliance among platform freelancers, and Wood, Graham, Lehdonvirta, and Hjorth (2019) on the autonomy-control duality in platform work establishes that these effects are substantial and variable. The implication is methodological: AI's restructuring effects will be systematically undercounted by any assessment that privileges extensive- and intensive-margin outcomes to the exclusion of labor-process dimensions. A multidimensional measurement framework is not merely a normative preference but an empirical necessity for accurately characterizing what restructuring has produced.

### 5.5 The Turing Trap: Technology Choice as Endogenous

*[Conceptual Argument]*

Brynjolfsson (2022), writing in *Daedalus*, argues that AI researchers and firms face systematic incentives to build *human-replacing* rather than *human-augmenting* AI: replacement is more easily monetized, benchmarked against human performance, and valued by capital markets than augmentation, whose gains are dispersed across workers. The argument connects directly to Acemoglu's (1998, 2002) directed-technical-change tradition, in which the direction of innovation responds endogenously to economic incentives rather than unfolding as an autonomous technological trajectory. Beraja and Zorzi's (2025) *Review of Economic Studies* result that automation can be excessive even on efficiency grounds — because firms do not internalize transitional losses to displaced, borrowing-constrained workers — supplies a complementary rationale for corrective intervention.

Taken together, these arguments reposition the central question of AI's labor-market effects. Whether displacement or reinstatement dominates is not a technological fact waiting to be discovered; it is a policy variable, shaped by research funding allocation, intellectual property regimes, tax and procurement policy, and regulatory frameworks. Systematic peer-reviewed evidence on whether actual AI research and deployment tilt toward replacement rather than augmentation remains thin. But the conceptual claim has consequences: AI governance debates should treat the displacement-reinstatement balance as endogenous to policy choice, not as exogenous constraint.

### 5.6 The Jagged Frontier and the Limits of Exposure Measurement

*[Established Finding]*

Dell'Acqua, McFowland, Mollick, and colleagues (2026) provide direct experimental evidence that AI capability is non-monotonic in task difficulty. Consultants with GPT-4 access performed 12.2 percent more tasks and were 25.1 percent faster on tasks within the model's capability frontier; on tasks outside that frontier, AI access actively *worsened* performance. The authors characterize the boundary as "jagged": sharp, unpredictable edges divide tasks where AI helps from those where it hurts, with no reliable smooth gradient from easy to hard.

The peer-reviewed exposure-measurement critiques converge on the methodological implication. Frank, Ahn, and Moro (2025) document that no single exposure index — AIOE, Webb patent-overlap, Eloundou et al.'s LLM rubric — predicts unemployment risk adequately on its own; predictive performance requires an ensemble. Septiandri, Constantinides, and Quercia (2024) show that Webb's verb-noun matching produces false positives. Williamson and colleagues (2025) critique AIOE for conflating substitution with complementarity. Beneath these specific critiques lies a common architectural problem: exposure measures assume that the gradient from unexposed to exposed is smooth and monotonic, which Dell'Acqua and colleagues' evidence shows it is not. The implication is that occupation-level exposure estimates — including the widely cited 46 percent headline from Eloundou, Manning, Mishkin, and Rock (2024) — may systematically misprice risk: overstating it for tasks where AI unexpectedly fails, understating it for tasks where AI unexpectedly succeeds. Methodological humility here is not epistemic caution but evidentiary necessity.

### 5.7 The Gender Exposure Reversal

*[Mixed: two Established Findings + one Analytical Hypothesis]*

Three distinct claims merit separation by epistemic status.

The first, *[Established Finding]*, is the exposure gap. Pre-generative-AI automation literature documented that routine manual work concentrated in male-dominated occupations bore the heaviest displacement burden (Graetz & Michaels, 2018; Acemoglu & Restrepo, 2020). Generative AI reverses this at the exposure level. Eloundou, Manning, Mishkin, and Rock (2024) find female-dominated clerical, administrative, and service occupations among the most LLM-exposed; Williamson and colleagues (2025) document that 76 percent of Irish women versus 51 percent of men occupy highly exposed roles.

The second, *[Established Finding]*, is the adoption gap. Humlum and Vestergaard (2025) document a sixteen-percentage-point gap in ChatGPT work-adoption across 18,000 Danish workers. Aldasoro, Armantier, Doerr, Gambacorta, and Oliviero (2024) find fifty percent of men versus thirty-seven percent of women report using generative AI in U.S. survey data. Tang, Li, Hu, Zeng, and Du (2025) observe a 6.4 percent greater productivity gain for male academics post-ChatGPT. Dorta-González, López-Puig, Dorta-González, and González-Betancor (2024) confirm lower adoption by female and advanced-career researchers.

The third claim is our *[Analytical Hypothesis]*: these twin gaps could reverse the four-decade trend in which technology-induced automation compressed gender wage gaps by hurting male blue-collar wages more heavily. If generative AI disproportionately exposes female-dominated work *and* women adopt the technology less, the gender wage gap could widen. No peer-reviewed study has yet measured realized gender-wage outcomes in the generative-AI era. The established exposure and adoption patterns are consistent with such a reversal; the realized outcome is not yet observed. Whether the hypothesized reversal materializes depends on whether exposure translates into displacement and whether adoption translates into productivity — questions the peer-reviewed literature has not yet answered.

### 5.8 The Global South's Informality Buffer

*[Analytical Hypothesis / Our Synthesis]*

Section 4.4 documents that Global South labor markets absorb automation shocks differently from OECD economies. Brambilla, César, Falcone, and Gasparini's (2023) *World Development* analysis finds that robots in Argentina, Brazil, and Mexico displaced formal salaried jobs while the informal sector absorbed displaced workers — preventing the full open-unemployment response visible in U.S. commuting zones, with wages not serving as the principal adjustment margin. El Mokri, El Abbassi, and Ibourk (2024) argue that premature deindustrialization combined with informality produces polarization dynamics in Morocco that Global North models cannot capture. Poudel and Gopinath (2026) find digitalization in India shifting self-employment toward salaried work. Parschau and Hauge (2020) show that automation in South African apparel operates across regional production networks rather than national labor markets alone.

We propose, as analytical synthesis, that informality functions as the Global South's institutional analogue to European coordinated-bargaining and active-labor-market-policy systems. Both are institutional absorption mechanisms; both mediate how technological exposure translates into realized outcomes; both are structurally different but institutionally analogous. European institutions absorb shocks through internal labor markets, retraining, and protected employment; Global South informality absorbs shocks through low-wage, low-protection reabsorption into informal work. Neither the Global South development literature nor the mainstream labor economics literature has explicitly framed informality in these terms. The reframing matters because it corrects an OECD-centric bias in the institutional-mediation literature: informality is not the absence of institutional response but a distinct institutional form whose welfare implications differ from but are analytically comparable to European-style coordinated systems. Whether informality-buffer dynamics under generative-AI exposure will parallel those under industrial-robot exposure is a question the peer-reviewed evidence has not yet addressed.

## 6. Policy Responses

### 6.1 Reskilling, Upskilling, and Active Labor Market Policy

Peer-reviewed evidence on active labor market policy responses to AI-driven restructuring points toward embedded rather than standalone training. Hellwig (2022), in *Oxford Economic Papers*, develops a search-and-matching model of automation and finds that robot adoption hurts low-skill workers most, but that allowing endogenous skill acquisition permits positive aggregate employment effects across all four calibrated countries. The case for retraining is strongest when paired with institutions that make skill upgrading feasible — credit access, time off work, portable credentials — rather than deployed as classroom intervention after displacement has already occurred.

Giuntella, Lu, and Wang's (2025) Chinese worker-level analysis in the *Economic Journal* provides direct evidence on training uptake as an adjustment margin: workers exposed to greater robot penetration were measurably more likely to undertake technical training. Training is a real behavioral response, but the same study documents that exposed workers still experienced reduced labor-force participation and hourly earnings — training alone did not suffice.

Dauth, Findeisen, Suedekum, and Woessner's (2021) German evidence suggests a complementary mechanism: within-firm task redeployment, anchored by works councils and the dual vocational-education system, absorbed displacement without requiring classroom retraining at all. Bartik (2020), in the *Journal of Economic Perspectives*, argues that customized training, manufacturing extension, and infrastructure outperform firm subsidies for distressed communities. The peer-reviewed consensus is that training works best embedded in transition institutions, not as standalone intervention.

### 6.2 Redistribution: Robot Taxation and UBI

The peer-reviewed literature on robot taxation has moved beyond a binary yes/no question. Guerreiro, Rebelo, and Teles's (2022) *Review of Economic Studies* model establishes the foundational result: a robot tax is optimal *temporarily* while current routine workers, who cannot easily retool, remain in the labor force; as they retire, the optimal tax falls to zero. Thuemmel (2023), in the *Journal of the European Economic Association*, shows that depending on robot prices and general-equilibrium effects, the optimal policy can be either a tax *or* a subsidy: when robots are expensive, subsidies can be optimal; as they become cheap, taxation becomes optimal. Costinot and Werning (2023), in the *Review of Economic Studies*, supply a distributional rationale for non-zero taxes whose optimal magnitude may decline as automation deepens. Schwellnus and colleagues (2024), in *Oxford Economic Papers*, empirically question the tax-erosion premise often invoked for robot-tax proposals.

Beraja and Zorzi (2025), in the *Review of Economic Studies*, supply a new efficiency rationale. With borrowing-constrained workers and slow reallocation, automation can be excessive even on pure efficiency grounds — firms do not internalize transitional worker losses. A corrective tax is therefore justified beyond distributional grounds alone.

On universal basic income, Hoynes and Rothstein (2019), in the *Annual Review of Economics*, conclude that a full UBI is likely massively expensive and generally inferior to targeted transfers and human-capital investments for poverty reduction. Hanna and Olken (2018), in the *Journal of Economic Perspectives*, formalize the universality-versus-targeting trade-off. The peer-reviewed economics literature is thus skeptical of UBI as first-line response while increasingly — though conditionally — permissive on automation taxation. The question has shifted from "should we tax robots?" to "when, how much, for how long, and under what welfare criterion."

### 6.3 AI Governance: The EU AI Act

The EU AI Act, finalized in 2024, is the first major jurisdiction's binding regulation of workplace AI. Peer-reviewed analysis is skeptical it will suffice. Özkiziltan and Landini (2025), in *Transfer: European Review of Labour and Research*, argue that although the Act brands itself as human-centric, its reliance on provider self-assessment, combined with the absence of mandatory fundamental-rights impact assessments for many private deployers, leaves workers with weak procedural protections. Wright (2025), in the *Journal of Industrial Relations*, notes that the Act classifies recruitment and workplace-decision AI as high-risk and introduces an AI-literacy duty under Article 4, but argues the risk-based framework is insufficient alone and must be complemented by employment law, occupational safety and health law, and collective bargaining. De Stefano and Taes (2023) press the complementary point: collective bargaining is the most suitable regulatory instrument for algorithmic management, and legislation should explicitly support sectoral and company-level agreements.

### 6.4 Sectoral Bargaining and Co-determination

The case for institutionalized worker voice in AI design and deployment is strongest in the co-determination literature. Krzywdzinski, Gerst, and Butollo (2023), writing in *Transfer*, document that German unions and works councils are not blocking AI; they are actively shaping it around human-centered design, worker-data protection, and anti-deskilling safeguards, with leverage expanded by Germany's 2021 Works Council Modernisation Act. Lin and Tomaskovic-Devey (2021), in *Social Forces*, provide complementary U.S. evidence: occupations facing higher automation risk saw better earnings trajectories in settings where unions remained resilient, though often with faster employment-share decline. The peer-reviewed case is not for "anti-AI" bargaining but for institutionalized worker voice in technology design and deployment — a mechanism through which augmentation rather than displacement can be pursued.

## 7. Limitations, Gaps, and Future Research

### 7.1 Methodological Limitations

Five methodological limitations constrain the evidence synthesized here. First, publication bias is not theoretical but empirically documented: Guarascio et al. (2025) and Jurkat et al. (2025) find preferential selection for negative wage results in U.S.-focused robot studies specifically, which may cause the Acemoglu-Restrepo (2020) *JPE* finding to appear more generalizable than it is. Second, identification-strategy bias: top economics journals privilege strong causal identification, systematically underweighting qualitative and descriptive cross-country work — our review compensates by drawing on *Work, Employment and Society*, *New Technology, Work and Employment*, *Organization Studies*, and *Human Relations*. Third, time horizons for generative-AI studies are short; Kauhanen and Rouvinen's two-year Finnish null may not survive longer data windows. Fourth, exposure-index limitations documented in Section 3.3.1 and synthesized in 5.6 imply that single-index estimates are unreliable, and the ensemble approach Frank, Ahn, and Moro (2025) advocate is not yet standard. Fifth, our own review inherits anglophone and peer-review constraints: working papers and non-English scholarship are systematically excluded. These constraints are structural, not incidental, and shape what this review can and cannot claim.

### 7.2 Geographic and Demographic Gaps

Three gaps warrant explicit flagging. Peer-reviewed evidence is disproportionately anglophone and OECD-centric — partly structural, partly imposed by the English-language constraint. Chinese-language scholarship on the world's largest industrial-robot economy is accessible in English only through selective translation; much peer-reviewed labor-economics work on Chinese labor markets appears in domestic journals outside our search. Second, peer-reviewed race-specific evidence on generative-AI exposure — measured at occupational, firm, or worker level — remains thin; intersectional analyses exist primarily in the broader automation literature (Petersen et al., 2023) rather than the GenAI-specific base. Third, longitudinal evidence on careers, geographic mobility, fertility, and health under AI exposure is largely unavailable; the two-year horizons of current GenAI studies are insufficient to observe long-run effects, including the skill-compression hypothesis advanced in Section 5.3.

### 7.3 Frontier Questions

Five frontier questions merit peer-reviewed attention. First, will the displacement-augmentation balance documented for GPT-4-class models hold as reasoning capability scales toward GPT-5 and beyond? Second, if the short-run skill compression of Section 5.3 persists, what happens to returns to human-capital accumulation and to firms' incentive to invest in training? Third, how do general-equilibrium feedback channels — demand, prices, trade, fertility — respond to AI-driven labor reallocation at national scale? Fourth, the jagged frontier of Section 5.6 implies that hybrid human-AI team design matters substantially; which organizational configurations produce the best augmentation outcomes? Fifth, does the Global South informality-buffer mechanism documented for industrial robots (Section 5.8) scale to cognitive-work displacement under generative AI?

## 8. Conclusion

This review opened with a puzzle. Acemoglu and Restrepo's (2020) U.S. commuting-zone finding that robot exposure reduced employment and wages is rigorously identified and widely cited; Dauth, Findeisen, Suedekum, and Woessner's (2021) German full-offset result, Kauhanen and Rouvinen's (2025) Finnish two-year null, and two recent meta-analyses pointing to near-zero average associations all complicate any single narrative. The resolution emerging from the peer-reviewed literature is neither that AI destroys jobs nor that AI leaves labor markets unchanged. It is that AI produces heterogeneous effects whose incidence is shaped jointly by technology generation, sector, institutions, worker characteristics, and the level at which outcomes are measured.

The three technology eras share a theoretical architecture but diverge empirically. Computerization in Era 1 produced polarization through routine-task displacement. Industrial robots in Era 2 produced measurable displacement in unprotected U.S. commuting zones and full offset in coordinated-bargaining Germany. Generative AI in Era 3 produces augmentation within firms and displacement on open platforms, with national wage-earner populations showing two-year nulls consistent with institutional mediation. The eight novel syntheses in Section 5 — from the routine-to-cognitive exposure inversion to the Global South's informality buffer — each extend the field's understanding by connecting findings that no single paper contains.

Two broad implications follow. For research, the priorities are multidimensional exposure measurement, mainstream-economics engagement with the labor-process axis, longitudinal generative-AI data, and peer-reviewed evidence on race-specific exposure and Global South cognitive-work displacement. For policy, the decisive variable is institutional mediation: coordinated bargaining, active labor market policy, institutionalized worker voice, transitional robot taxation, and — for Global South labor markets — the recognition that informality is itself an institutional form whose welfare implications policy can shape. The Turing Trap argument suggests the displacement-reinstatement balance is itself a policy choice, not a technological fact. What the peer-reviewed evidence most firmly supports is not a prediction about the future of work but a claim about the present structure of restructuring: heterogeneous, institution-mediated, and more amenable to policy shaping than either alarmist or dismissive public discourse typically allows.

## Introduction and Scope of the Evidence

Within this framework, automation generates a displacement effect that reduces labor's share of value added, countered by a reinstatement effect as new tasks reinstate labor into a broader range of ## Theoretical Foundations: Task-Based Models of Automation and Reinstatement

This framework, building on prior contributions including Autor, Levy, and Murnane (2003), Acemoglu and Autor (2011), and Zeira (1998), posits that production requires tasks allocated to either capital or labor, and that technological change alters the task content of production rather than simply augmenting factor productivity.[1][13] (also mirrored) Within this framework, automation corresponds to the development of technologies enabling capital to substitute for labor in specific tasks, generating what the authors term a "displacement effect" that reduces labor's share of value added.[1] (also mirrored) Simultaneously, automation generates a "productivity effect" by increasing value added and raising demand for labor in non-automated tasks, meaning the net impact on labor demand depends on the relative magnitudes of these two forces.[1] (also mirrored) The framework identifies a third mechanism—the "reinstatement effect"—whereby new technologies create labor-intensive tasks in which labor has a comparative advantage, directly increasing the labor share and functioning as the polar opposite of the displacement effect.[1][13] (also mirrored) During this period, wage bill per capita grew at 2.5 percent per year, largely explained by the productivity effect of 2.4 percent per year.[16][13] (also mirrored) However, from 1987 to 2017, the pattern shifted markedly: the displacement effect accelerated to 0.7 percent per year while the reinstatement effect decelerated to 0.35 percent per year, generating a significant negative shift in the task content of production against labor.[16][13] (also mirrored) Cumulatively, changes in the task content of production reduced labor demand by 10 percent during this later period, and wage bill growth slowed to 1.33 percent per year, essentially stagnating since 2000.[13][14] In manufacturing, the displacement effect was particularly pronounced, reducing labor demand at about 1.1 percent per year or approximately 30 percent cumulatively.[16][13] (also mirrored) Autor further notes that in 1900, 41 percent of the US workforce was employed in agriculture, falling to 2 percent by 2000, illustrating the historical scale of automation-driven displacement.[17] Establishments whose task structures in 2010 were more suitable for AI subsequently posted relatively more AI vacancies, with a standardized coefficient of 9.19 and standard error of 1.21, suggesting that AI adoption follows task-based exposure patterns.[18] Short-term evidence from an online labor market provides striking results: following the release of ChatGPT, freelancers in highly affected occupations experienced a 2 percent decrease in monthly jobs and a 5.2 percent decrease in monthly earnings.[19] Image-based generative AI models produced similar effects, with a 2.1 percent reduction in monthly jobs and a 5.2 percent reduction in monthly earnings, suggesting that the displacement effect operates consistently across different AI modalities.[19] That study identifies "techies"—engineers and technicians with strong STEM skills—as a key factor: economies abundant in techies experienced strong skill upgrading, while polarization persisted in economies with few techies, and robotization was associated with skill upgrading in manufacturing.[20] In the Chinese context, digital transformation research finds that digitalization is associated with increased hiring in managerial, professional, and technical roles and reduced demand for auxiliary and manual labor, with abstract task demand rising while routine and manual tasks decline.[21] One analysis observes that digitalization represents a general-purpose technology whose net effect on employment will likely be negative, though the extent to which this is offset by the creation of new types of goods and services remains uncertain.[22] Another study employs a nuanced task-based approach to show that AI is reshaping inequality through complex interactions of skill restructuring, wage decoupling from productivity, and shifts in worker bargaining power, potentially accelerating capital-biased technological change.[23] Acemoglu and Restrepo essential because, as the authors emphasize, not all automation technologies increase aggregate labor demand; some automation technologies may reduce labor demand because they bring sizable displacement effects but only modest productivity gains.[1] Between 1947 and 1987, the displacement effect reduced labor demand by approximately 0.48 percent per year, while the reinstatement effect increased it by 0.47 percent per year, resulting in small changes in the task content of production.[16][13] (also mirrored) Acemoglu and Restrepo estimate that about half of employment growth over 1980–2015 took place in occupations in which job titles or tasks performed by workers changed.[1] Complementing this theoretical framework, Autor demonstrates that tasks which cannot be substituted by automation are generally complemented by it.[17] Autor's analysis of automated teller machines illustrates this complementarity: ATM numbers quadrupled from approximately 100,000 to 400,000 between technology enabled bank personnel to become involved in "relationship banking".[17] Legal occupations, which face little direct impact from automation and are often in firms that use AI heavily, saw a quality through changes in task content and the working environment, with the potential to reduce tedious or dangerous tasks but also to leave workers with a higher-paced work environment.[60] Workers exposed to ChatGPT during the experiment were 2 times as likely to report using it in their real job 2 weeks after the experiment and 1.6 times as likely 2 months after the experiment.[61] The international digital divide compounds these distributional effects, as cross-country differences in occupational structure and digital infrastructure create an asymmetry between advanced and developing economies.[62] In Mali, just 12 percent of today's jobs are exposed to GenAI, but only 10 percent of such workers have internet access, meaning just 1.2 percent of workers in Mali hold jobs that are both exposed to GenAI and can make practical use of it.[62] In contrast, about one out of three workers in Switzerland and Singapore have jobs exposed to GenAI, and all of them have access to the internet.[62] Among all countries in the sample, there are 441.8 million jobs that fall within exposure categories that could leverage GenAI for job augmentation, but 66.9 million, or 15.1 percent, of those jobs do not have internet access.[62] Workers in positions vulnerable to automation typically maintain sufficient internet connectivity to experience displacement effects even in low-income settings, while those who could benefit from GenAI augmentation face substantial digital infrastructure gaps that may prevent them from realizing productivity gains.[62] This finding suggests that developing countries may experience the disruptive effects of GenAI faster than its productivity benefits.[62] There are age disparities in exposure as well, particularly in upper-middle-income countries, where about 10.4 percent of workers aged 16 to 35 face automation risk, while only 4.3 percent of workers aged 56 to 65 are in that position.[62] Among college graduates in low-income countries, 13.2 percent face GenAI automation exposure, and 72 percent of these workers already have internet access, implying that initial displacement effects could concentrate among the narrow segment of skilled and digitally-connected workers.[62] Generative AI also affects labor market matching efficiency, with a field experiment finding that AI-assisted job postings made employers 19 percent more likely to post a job and saved them 44 percent of writing time, but there was no discernible increase in matches formed.[63]

## Policy Responses to AI-Driven Labor Market Restructuring

In the United States, the House announced a bipartisan task force on AI in February 2024, reflecting growing congressional interest in the economic impacts of the technology.[64] Survey data from February 2024 show that 84.6% of businesses reporting a "small number" of tasks replaced by AI declined to 79.2% six months later, while those reporting a "moderate number" rose from 13.0% to 17.7%, and those reporting a "large number" increased from 2.4% to 3.1%.[64] Employment effects remained largely unchanged in the near term, with 94.6% of firms reporting "no change" in employment due to AI, declining to 87.4% six months later, while those reporting employment increases rose from 2.8% to 6.5% and those reporting decreases rose from 2.6% to 6.1%.[64] As of 2021, the EU average for AI usage among enterprises stood at 7.9%, with 21 out of 27 countries not exceeding 10%, while Denmark led at 23.9%, followed by Portugal at 17.3%, Finland at 15.8%, and the Netherlands at 13.1%.[65] Eurostat's 2023 survey found that approximately 8% of EU businesses use at least one AI technology, up from 7.6% in 2021, with adoption rates ranging from 1.5% in Romania to 15% in Denmark.[66] Regarding labor displacement mitigation, academic studies have proposed several policy options including a tax on robots that slows the accumulation of automating capital, or a higher capital income tax combined with an unskilled wage tax cut, which research suggests delivers the largest welfare benefits.[65] When imperfect competition is included in models, a markup tax emerges as the second-best policy after a robot tax, providing an additional lever for policymakers to manage automation-driven displacement.[65] In high-income countries, 5.5% of total employment is exposed to automation from AI while 13.4% is exposed to augmentation, whereas in low-income countries the corresponding figures are 0.4% for automation and 10.4% for augmentation.[65] Notably, only 23% of exposed jobs, representing 8% of total U.S. non-farm jobs, have at least one task that is economically attractive to automate, and when accounting for the contribution of automated tasks to compensation, only 0.4% of non-farm wages would potentially be lost to automated computer vision.[65] Governance of AI presents substantial challenges due to the high degree of uncertainty and complexity in the AI landscape, requiring governments to develop both specific governance mechanisms for sectors such as healthcare and transport, as well as a broader global governance framework.[67] The conceptual framing of AI is described as a crucial determinant of how problems are understood, whether they are included in policies, and the priority afforded to them in public policy.[67] Private-sector estimates of aggregate productivity impacts vary widely, with one study estimating that AI adoption could contribute between 0.1 and 0.6 percentage points of productivity growth annually between 2022 and 2040, while Goldman Sachs projects 1.5 percentage points annually for the 10 years following widespread adoption.[65] Using the Acemoglu (2024) framework, the wage bill-weighted share of exposed tasks in the U.S. economy is calculated at 19.9%, with tasks automated by AI assumed to reduce labor costs by 27%, translating into 15% total cost savings given that labor costs account for 53% of output.[68] Capital spending by the five largest U.S. technology firms on AI is scaling at approximately 50% per year, from $155 billion in 2022 to a forecast $755 billion in 2026, implying AI-sector productivity gains of roughly 2.7x per boom cycle that could add between 5% and 58% to cumulative GDP growth over a seven-year transition.[69] The automation of tasks through AI could exacerbate income and social inequalities, as high- and low-skilled occupations benefit from increased wage premiums while middle-skilled jobs are phased out.[67] Adoption is strongly skewed by firm size, with uptake rates exceeding 30% among large firms (at least 250 employees) but only 6% among small firms (10 -49 employees).[66] Studies analyzing automation and augmentation effects find that the latter outweigh the former across both low- and high-income countries.[65] The rapid developments in AI and intensification in adoption across domains pose serious challenges to governments that must manage the scale and speed of socio-technical transitions, requiring better understanding of risks to minimize adverse effects.[67]

## Conclusions and Research Gaps

Early research findings focusing on how AI affects labor demand are collectively inconsistent, and any findings based on current data are necessarily weak signals about the future.[82] Multiple studies using the Current Population Survey—including work by Gimbel et al. (2025), Eckhardt and Goldschlag (2025), and Dominski and Lee (2025)—show at most small changes in hiring in AI-exposed jobs.[84] Separately, Frank et al. (2026) found similar results, and some analyses argue that the timing of declines in job postings corresponds better to rising interest rates than to the launch of large language models.[82] Despite this uncertainty, one source reports that AI exposure measures from Eloundou et al. (2024) have very high correlation with Microsoft Copilot usage from 2025, suggesting that earlier predictions were largely borne out.[84] However, existing measures of AI exposure are still criticized for lacking validation on real-world economic outcomes, and the mismatch between supply and demand for research on this topic is described as unlike anything previously seen.[84] The most experienced and highest-skilled workers saw minimal or even slightly negative effects, suggesting that generative AI functions as a skill-leveling technology rather than a skill-amplifying one.[85] AI compressed the learning curve substantially, with agents having two months of experience performing like agents with six months without AI.[85] Separately, gains from AI adoption were found to be largest for moderately rare problems, where human agents have less baseline experience but the system still has adequate training data.[86] The market structure of AI itself may shape these outcomes, as the two top-ranked foundation model systems differ by only 37 points on the LMSYS scale, translating to approximately a 55.3% chance of one outperforming the other, indicating that frontier models are close substitutes.[87] One analysis characterizes AI's impact on work thus far as "routine-biased technological deployment on steroids," and studies on the impact of machine learning–based AI conclude that, as in prior eras of automation, jobs remain in spite of these shifts.[88] (also mirrored) Whether GenAI will support a continuation of the polarization of work, which analysts widely agree has been underway, or whether it will open possibilities for the augmentation of worker opportunities and the creation of new categories of high-quality jobs, remains an open question.[88] (also mirrored) AI also has unclear effects on matching between employers and job candidates: algorithmic writing assistance for resumes leads to causal increases in hiring and wages for prospective employees, and AI-assisted interviewing leads to better candidate selection.[84] On the other hand, AI usage improves cover letters but makes them less informative signals of worker ability, prompting employers to shift toward alternative signals such as past reviews, and if all resumes begin to look the same, it may become harder to form good matches.[84] The growing integration of AI into academic research workflows itself poses challenges, as AI-generated literature reviews are increasingly being rejected for lacking genuine academic analysis, with journals tightening standards around AI-assisted writing.[91] A systematic review of AI applications in academic writing identified thematic approaches including possible applications, specific tools, plagiarism detection, and ethical use, underscoring the need for further investigation of how AI can improve clarity and accuracy in scientific communication.[92] The evidence on how AI is affecting the labor market today is inconclusive, and claims about harmful impacts on particular groups of workers are premature.[82] One pessimistic estimate from Acemoglu (2024) suggests that the growth effects of generative AI will be only 0.07% per year over the coming decade, which, if correct, would render the market concentration implications a footnote in economic history.[87] The German debate over the potential of "Industry 4.0" predominantly hinges on different views about who controls the production process and how the tools are used.[88] (also mirrored) Platform firms have become a crucial part of the market economy and GenAI may drive significant changes in the organization of work and in market competition.[88] (also mirrored) A study measures the impacts of labor in the platform economy in terms of new work created, old work reorganized, and value creation reconfigured.[88] (also mirrored) The available evidence on AI's labor market effects remains nascent and uncertain, with research having barely scratched the surface.[84][82] The safe deployment of AI is identified as an essential question, with implications for social and economic well-being as well as the ultimate risk to human existence from AI.[88] (also mirrored) In sum, the evidence suggests that AI is beginning to have measurable effects on productivity, particularly for entry-level and lower-skilled workers, but important questions about AI's broader effects on the labor market remain unanswered[84][82][85]

## Additional Corroborated Findings

By contrast, a more cautious task-based framework by Acemoglu et al. estimates that AI's contribution to total factor productivity gains may not exceed 0.7 percentage points over ten years even under full automation of exposed tasks, with GDP impact remaining modest at approximately 1.1%.[93] IMF simulations by Cerutti et al. illustrate two contrasting trajectories: a rapid adoption scenario in transformation develops a general-equilibrium model in which moderate AI exposure benefits workers on average but high exposure harms them, with the return to social skills rising and the return to analytical skills falling.[98] Gmyrek et al. estimate that only 3.3% of jobs will be exposed to full automation, a figure that rises to 7% in the analysis by Chui et al., reinforcing the consensus that AI is transforming jobs more than eliminating them.[93] Goldman Sachs Research estimates that if current AI use cases were expanded across the economy, just 2.5% of U.S. employment would be at Historical data on industrial robot density illustrate stark sectoral variation in automation: in 2014, there were an estimated 0.02 industrial robots per 1,000 workers in construction compared with 117.72 per 1,000 in automotive production.[100] The WEF reports that global average robot density reached 162 units per 10,000 employees in 2023, double the number measured seven years prior, with 80% of installations concentrated in China, Japan, and the United States.[101] AI-driven recruitment tools have also raised fairness concerns, with scholars noting that AI applications in hiring can amplify and introduce new forms of intersectional discrimination given datasets structured along axes of social inequality.[102] The analysis of Industry 4.0 adoption in supply chain operations found 525 papers related to Industry 4.0 broadly, but only 20 papers specifically addressed supply chain dimensions, highlighting a significant research gap.[103] The Future of Jobs Report finds that 86% of respondents expect AI and information processing technologies to transform their business by 2030, positioning AI as the technology with the single largest anticipated impact.[101] A large-scale study of AI chatbot adoption across 11 exposed occupations in Denmark, linked to administrative data, estimates precise null effects on earnings and recorded hours at both the individual and workplace levels, with confidence intervals ruling out effects above 1%.[95] Nearly all respondents reported that AI chatbots have had no impact on their earnings.[95] That same study observed a 3.7-minute decrease in average handle time, representing an 8.5% decline from a baseline of 43 minutes.[104] Morgan reports that the unemployment rate among college graduates reached 5.8% in March, the highest
