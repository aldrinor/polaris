## What the reference does that POLARIS does not

### 1. It has an explanatory spine

The reference follows a deliberate argument:

1. Define AI inside the interconnected 4IR technological system.
2. Explain displacement, reinstatement, productivity, skill bias, routine bias, substitution, and augmentation.
3. Test those mechanisms against empirical findings.
4. Explain why results differ by technology, method, geography, aggregation level, adoption stage, and industry.
5. Derive consequences for wages, inequality, and skills.
6. Reconcile optimistic and pessimistic scenarios through adoption lags, complementary investment, and technical limitations.
7. Derive policy tensions and a research agenda.

POLARIS has headings resembling this structure, but its sentences generally do not perform those argumentative functions. Its introduction jumps among task theory, firm employment, occupational history, WEF forecasts, AI investment, ImageNet accuracy, tourism, MENA unemployment, and clerical projections without explaining their relationship.

### 2. Its paragraphs synthesize; ours enumerate

The reference repeatedly uses this paragraph pattern:

> analytical claim → evidence from contrasting studies → reason for heterogeneity → boundary condition → implication

POLARIS’s dominant pattern is:

> study A found X.[A] Study B found Y.[B] Study C found Z.[C]

Even the section called “Cross-Study Synthesis and Contradictions” largely enumerates findings. It begins with a sentence fragment, does not systematically identify contradictions, and is followed by a limitation claiming that “No contradictions were detected by the pipeline.” That is a direct internal contradiction in the report’s design.

### 3. It distinguishes levels of analysis

The reference explains why these findings can coexist:

- displacement within particular tasks;
- employment growth inside productive adopting firms;
- losses at competing or non-adopting firms;
- local labor-market effects;
- weak aggregate effects during low diffusion;
- long-run effects after organizational adaptation.

POLARIS presents all of these results but rarely separates task, worker, establishment, firm, sector, local-market, and aggregate estimands. Consequently, apparent contradictions remain unresolved.

This missing distinction is one of the largest insight opportunities.

### 4. It organizes heterogeneity rather than merely acknowledging it

The reference identifies recurring moderators:

- robots versus predictive AI versus generative AI;
- routine versus non-routine tasks;
- exposure versus actual adoption;
- firm-level versus economy-wide outcomes;
- short-run versus long-run effects;
- manufacturing versus services;
- developed versus developing economies;
- worker skill and access to complementary software;
- infrastructure, institutions, and regulation.

POLARIS contains evidence on almost all of these moderators, but they remain scattered. It never turns them into a compact explanatory model.

### 5. It provides actual industry analysis

The reference has dedicated comparative treatment of:

- manufacturing and logistics;
- healthcare;
- finance;
- transportation;
- creative industries and media;
- marketing;
- customer service.

For each, it connects applications to tasks, occupations, substitution, augmentation, and changing skill needs.

POLARIS supplies isolated industry facts—pharmaceutical value added, retail exposure, call-center productivity, robot density, recruiting, construction, tourism—but never constructs an industry comparison. That is a major miss because industry scope has:

- 0.25 of comprehensiveness;
- 0.25 of insight through cross-industry synthesis;
- 0.15 of instruction-following.

### 6. It uses 4IR as an analytical framework

The reference connects AI to:

- IoT, robotics, cloud infrastructure, data, and cyber-physical systems;
- rapid diffusion and general-purpose-technology dynamics;
- organizational redesign and complementary investments;
- simultaneous transformation across physical, cognitive, and service work;
- the potential scale and speed of restructuring.

POLARIS defines 4IR in the introduction but largely drops the framework afterward. Mentioning 4IR is not the same as leveraging it. The judge explicitly distinguishes these.

### 7. It develops theoretical linkages

The reference connects:

- displacement ↔ declining labor share;
- reinstatement ↔ new task creation;
- productivity ↔ firm expansion and economy-wide demand;
- RBTC ↔ historical job polarization;
- generative AI ↔ erosion of the routine/non-routine boundary;
- complementarity ↔ software skills and organizational adaptation;
- adoption lags ↔ the productivity paradox;
- capital ownership and superstar firms ↔ inequality;
- implementation choices ↔ substitution versus augmentation.

POLARIS contains many components of these relationships but seldom states the links. Its “theoretical” section instead repeats Acemoglu and Restrepo facts, often through duplicate mirrors.

### 8. It converts disagreements into explanations

The reference does not merely say that evidence is mixed. It proposes reasons:

- different technologies;
- different time horizons;
- different units of analysis;
- adoption still being too limited;
- methodological choices and controls;
- complementary investment;
- geographic and institutional differences.

POLARIS repeatedly concludes that evidence is “inconclusive” without doing enough to explain the structure of the disagreement.

### 9. It derives policy from mechanisms

The reference connects policies to specific failures:

- displacement and transition costs → income support and active labor-market policies;
- changing task requirements → reskilling and lifelong learning;
- tax bias toward capital → tax rebalancing;
- excessive substitution → incentives for human-complementary AI;
- algorithmic harms → governance and worker protections;
- global diffusion → international coordination.

It also identifies a genuine policy tension: slowing disruptive automation versus accelerating productivity-enhancing adoption.

POLARIS lists robot taxes, capital taxes, governance uncertainty, and adoption statistics, but does not construct a mechanism-to-policy chain.

### 10. Its conclusion performs synthesis

The reference conclusion separately identifies:

- established findings;
- disputed findings;
- reasons for disagreement;
- concrete research gaps;
- dynamic adjustment questions.

POLARIS’s conclusion continues adding new material: LMSYS rankings, academic-writing reviews, existential risk, platform work, and market concentration. A conclusion should compress and adjudicate the review, not reopen retrieval.

### 11. It is substantially cleaner

POLARIS ships:

- 27 “(also mirrored)” markers;
- two mid-sentence or mid-word section openings;
- internal deduplication cross-references;
- repeated facts;
- duplicate manifestations of the same article;
- metadata-as-content;
- missing antecedents such as “The study also documents…”;
- a fragment opening the synthesis section;
- irrelevant material about AI-generated literature reviews;
- an unprofessional pipeline-telemetry limitation section.

These are not subtle style preferences. They visibly signal that the report was assembled by a malfunctioning pipeline.

### 12. POLARIS does have evidence the reference lacks

The current report has potentially valuable newer material on:

- skill compression;
- digital freelancing;
- Danish administrative-data null effects;
- informal workplace adoption;
- the digital divide;
- entry-level career ladders;

This is the material that could beat the reference—if recovered from eligible journal versions and synthesized. It currently does not compensate for the weaker organization and source violations.

### The reference is not flawless

The reference itself appears to cite or name non-journal manifestations such as an MPRA meta-analysis and a Diva Portal item, lacks visible conventional in-text citations for many claims, leaves the “supporting sources” column of its policy table blank, and sometimes gives generic sector descriptions while admitting limited journal evidence.

Therefore, merely imitating it is insufficient. The winning strategy is:

> reference-level conceptual architecture and synthesis + POLARIS’s newer empirical evidence + materially stricter source compliance.

## Ordered plan, ranked by expected points per effort

The point estimates below are absolute Overall-score hypotheses: `+0.010` means one leaderboard point. They are not additive guarantees because criteria overlap and RACE’s target/reference transformation is nonlinear.

### 0. Establish a cross-task evaluation protocol

**Expected gain:** 0 directly; prevents false optimization  
**Effort:** Small–medium

Task 72 is useful for diagnosis, not for selecting a SOTA system. Before further tuning, choose a stratified development set spanning at least 15–20 RACE tasks, covering different domain, source, recency, format, and instruction constraints.

Record criterion-level results, not only four dimension totals. Freeze evaluator, reference, generation settings, and report artifacts.

**Failure mode:** The development set resembles task 72 too closely, or repeated evaluations are treated as independent when judge errors are correlated.

**Measurement:** Every subsequent arm must be evaluated as a paired change on the same tasks. Promote only if:

- the cross-task mean improvement is positive with a confidence interval excluding zero;
- at least roughly two-thirds of tasks improve;
- no high-weight criterion experiences a material regression;
- task 72 improvement alone is never sufficient.

For task 72 alone, do not interpret a single delta below approximately `+0.02`. Bundle small fixes before evaluating.

### 2. Reinstate a hard journal-eligibility firewall

**Expected gain:** `+0.015 to +0.025`  
**Effort:** Medium  
**Points per effort:** Very high

Source identity must be separated from retrieval URL. A Google Scholar profile, ResearchGate page, author PDF, NBER version, or repository mirror can help locate an article, but it is not itself the cited source.

For every cited work, require:

- canonical article identity and DOI where available;
- publication in an English-language peer-reviewed journal;
- journal title, year, volume/issue or article number;
- evidence that the exact relied-upon claim appears in the journal article;
- work-level deduplication across preprint, working paper, repository, conference, and journal versions;
- a defensible quality threshold excluding predatory or obviously marginal outlets.

If a valuable finding has no qualifying journal version, omit it. Do not silently relax the constraint for relevance or coverage.

**Targets**

- Instruction-following: English-language articles, weight 0.10

- Metadata says “journal article” but the venue is low quality.

**Measurement:** Mechanical eligibility must reach 100%, followed by manual auditing of a random source sample. But promotion requires an actual rise in the two source-related evaluator criteria. Compare:

### 4. Rebuild generation around an evidence-comparison matrix

**Expected gain:** `+0.030 to +0.045`  
**Effort:** Large  
**Points per effort:** High; this is the essential SOTA lever

Before prose generation, normalize each finding by:

- technology: robots, predictive AI, generative AI;
- mechanism: substitution, augmentation, reinstatement, productivity;
- unit: task, worker, firm, occupation, industry, region, economy;
- outcome: employment, wages, productivity, task content, job quality;
- horizon: immediate, medium-run, long-run;
- industry;
- geography and institutions;
- adoption status: exposure, reported use, operational deployment;
- method and identification strength;
- direction and magnitude;
- applicable boundary conditions.

Generate paragraphs from comparison bundles, not individual source cards. Each substantive paragraph should do four things:

1. state an analytical proposition;
2. compare multiple eligible studies;
3. explain agreement or disagreement using the matrix;
4. state the implication or unresolved uncertainty.

For example:

> Controlled studies show large task-level productivity gains, especially for less-experienced workers, while administrative labor-market studies find near-zero effects on earnings and hours. These are not necessarily inconsistent: experimental efficacy conditions on tool use in bounded tasks, whereas aggregate outcomes also depend on diffusion, workflow redesign, demand responses, and firm competition.

That is the kind of synthesis the reference performs and POLARIS does not.

**Targets**

- Insight: mechanisms, weight 0.25
- Insight: critical synthesis, weight 0.25
- Insight: emergent themes and theoretical linkages, weight 0.20
- Comprehensiveness: representative literature, weight 0.15
- Comprehensiveness: balance, weight 0.10
- Readability P1, weight 0.15

**How it could fail:**

- The model invents explanations not supported by the cited studies.
- Strict verification rejects legitimate synthesis because no source states the cross-study inference verbatim.
- Formulaic “study A versus study B” paragraphs replace the current formula without adding insight.
- Weak studies are given equal weight to causal or longitudinal evidence.
- Excessive normalization strips important context.

**Mitigation:** Distinguish three provenance types:

- directly reported findings;
- author interpretations;
- review-level inferences.

Review-level inferences may combine sources, but their premises must be individually grounded and the prose should signal inference rather than attribute it to a paper.

**Measurement:** This must be judged on the three named insight criteria. Counts of multi-source paragraphs, citations per paragraph, or “synthesis sentences” are diagnostic only. Run a direct ablation: identical eligible corpus and outline, serial-summary generator versus comparison-matrix generator.

### 6. Make a small number of defensible cross-study theses explicit

**Expected gain:** `+0.012 to +0.022` beyond the basic synthesis rewrite  
**Effort:** Medium  
**Points per effort:** Medium–high

## How to measure against the noise floor

Use actual RACE scores and criterion scores as the dependent variables. Everything else is only a release gate.

For each major arm:

1. Freeze the eligible evidence corpus.
2. Change one generation component.
3. Generate reports for the same stratified development tasks.
4. Evaluate paired artifacts under the same current judge.
5. Repeat enough times to estimate evaluator variance.
6. Report mean change, confidence interval, task win rate, and criterion-specific changes.

For task 72:

- run at least four or five evaluations per major arm if affordable;
- require a mean improvement of roughly `+0.025` before calling the result convincing on that task alone;
- bundle tiny renderer fixes rather than attempting to resolve sub-noise effects separately.

For cross-task promotion:

- use at least 15–20 heterogeneous tasks during development;
- require a confidence interval excluding zero;
- require broadly distributed gains, not one or two large wins;
- reject arms whose gains come mainly from task-specific 4IR wording.

For the final claim:

- evaluate all 100 tasks;
- use repeated full runs if feasible;
- target a mean near or above 0.545;
- require the uncertainty interval, not merely one run, to clear 0.5265.

## What to stop optimizing

Stop using these as objectives:

- total words;
- verified sentence count;
- citations per section;
- 15 sources per section;
- source density;
- raw claim density;
- number of quantitative facts;
- number of headings;
- contradiction counts produced by a detector;
- “all sentences grounded” as a sufficient quality condition.

Grounding and source eligibility remain necessary gates. They are not the winning objective.

The central change is straightforward but substantial: POLARIS must stop writing from isolated facts and start writing from structured disagreements, mechanisms, and boundary conditions. That is where the reference’s advantage lies, and insight is both the largest current deficit and the highest-weighted dimension.
