## Introduction: AI and the Restructuring of Labor Markets

Global business investment in AI increased 13-fold from US$14.57 billion in 2013 to US$189 billion in 2023, with 35% of businesses already using AI in their operations and more than 50% intending to implement AI technologies.[1] One systematic review of peer-reviewed literature identifies three core themes in AI-induced labor disruption: task automation, skill polarization, and industry-specific disruptions across sectors such as healthcare, education, and creative industries.[1] The International Data Corporation projected that global spending on AI would rise from $85.3 billion in 2021 to more than $204 billion in 2025, representing a compound annual growth rate of 24.5%.[2] Research tracking AI adoption from 2010 to 2023 found that when AI can perform most of the tasks that make up a particular job, the share of people in that role within a company falls by about 14%, but when AI's impact is concentrated in just a few tasks, employment in that role can actually grow.[3] A large increase in AI use is linked to about 6% higher employment growth and 9.5% more sales growth over five years, suggesting that firms adopting AI do not necessarily shed workers but can grow and use workers more efficiently.[3] Following ChatGPT's public launch in November 2022, job postings for automation-vulnerable roles decreased 13% while demand for augmentation-prone positions increased 20% through March 2025, indicating a bifurcation of labor demand favoring roles where human judgment complements algorithmic capability.[4] One analysis notes that AI exposure is greatest in high-paying roles, which contrasts with earlier technology waves in the 1980s through early 2000s that mainly displaced middle-skill, routine jobs.[3] Among white-collar employment specifically, experts predict just 2% job growth between 2025 and 2030, well below the pre-existing trend of 6.8%, with one in four experts predicting outright losses above 4%.[5] An IMF working paper co-authored by Joseph Stiglitz warns that advances in artificial intelligence and automation have the potential to be labor-saving and to increase inequality and poverty around the globe, giving rise to winner-takes-all dynamics that advantage highly skilled individuals and countries at the forefront of technological progress.[6] The scope of this literature spans multiple academic fields including management, technology, ethics, economics, and the social sciences, reflecting widespread interest in how AI may restructure work.[1] Most research to date acknowledges that AI poses a threat to human employment rather than being purely complementary to it, and the perception of a rising risk of technological unemployment is diffusing throughout the literature.[1]

## Occupational Exposure to AI: Susceptibility Indices and Assessments

Eloundou and colleagues estimated that roughly 1.8% of jobs could have over half their tasks affected by LLMs with simple interfaces and general training, but when accounting for complementary software developments this share jumps to just over 46% of jobs.[7] A text-mining analysis of the International Standard Occupation Classification found that 32.8% of occupations could be fully impacted by ChatGPT, 36.5% might experience a partial impact, and 30.7% are likely to remain unaffected.[8] More recent work using LLM-based task evaluations reveals cross-country variation by income level, with higher-income economies experiencing greater task-level exposure; the mean automation scores are 0.453, 0.427, and 0.404 for high-, middle-, and low-income groups respectively.[9] Similarly, Morgan Stanley research concluded that AI's impact on labor markets has been modest so far, with little evidence of broad-based job losses, echoing past innovation cycles that ultimately expanded employment over time.[10] California has even launched an AI job loss tracker linking multiple measures of occupational AI exposure to monthly unemployment insurance claims data to monitor for signs of labor market disruption in real time.[11]

## Employment Displacement and Hiring: Evidence from Real-World Labor Markets

Acemoglu and Restrepo's task-based framework establishes that automation produces a displacement effect whereby capital takes over tasks previously performed by labor, reducing labor's share of value added.[12] Their decomposition reveals that between 1947 and 1987, the displacement effect reduced labor demand by about 0.48 percent per year, but an equally strong reinstatement effect increased labor demand by 0.47 percent per year, leaving the overall task content of production roughly unchanged.[12] From 1987 to 2017, however, displacement accelerated to 0.7 percent per year while reinstatement slowed to 0.35 percent per year, producing a significant negative shift in the task content of production against labor.[12] These effects span both extensive and intensive margins: freelancers were 1.2% less likely to receive any employment in a given month and took 4.7% fewer jobs conditional on employment.[13] Image-based generative AI models produced qualitatively similar effects, with a 2.1% reduction in monthly jobs and a 5.2% decrease in monthly compensation in affected occupations.[13] Conversely, among occupations with low computer use, greater AI exposure is linked to a 0.60 percentage point greater drop in usual weekly working hours, equivalent to 13 minutes per week.[14] Generative AI had a substantial adverse effect on workers' employment outcomes, with freelancers in more affected occupations experiencing a 2% decrease in monthly jobs and a 5.2% decrease in monthly earnings following ChatGPT's release.[13] The authors find that a 1% increase in freelancers' earnings is associated with an additional 0.5% reduction in the number of jobs for text-based services.[13] In occupations where computer use is high, a one standard deviation increase in AI exposure is associated with 5.7 percentage points higher employment growth.[14] The share of job postings requiring AI skills remains very low overall, averaging 0.24% across occupations in 2019, up from 0.10% in 2012.[14]

## Productivity Gains from Generative AI: Field Experiments and Workplace Evidence

A preregistered online experiment involving 453 college-educated professionals found that ChatGPT substantially raised productivity in midlevel professional writing tasks, with average time taken decreasing by 40% and output quality rising by 18%.[15] Workers exposed to ChatGPT during the experiment were 2 times as likely to report using it in their real job 2 weeks afterward and 1.6 times as likely 2 months after the experiment.[15] The experiment also found that inequality between workers decreased, while participants' enjoyment of tasks rose by 0.5 standard deviations in the treatment group compared to the control.[16].[15] Complementing these experimental findings, a nationally representative U.S. survey conducted in August 2024 showed that 28% of all workers used generative AI at work to some degree.[17] Nearly one-third (31.9%) of workers who used generative AI at least once in the previous month spent an hour or more per workday using it, while another 47.0% used it between 15 and 59 minutes daily.[17] Among daily users, 52.0% reported using generative AI an hour or more per day, compared with only 7.3% of those who used it in the previous month but not the previous week.[17] When factoring in all workers, including non-users, workers saved 1.4% of total hours because of generative AI.[17] A standard model of aggregate production combined with these survey data implied that self-reported time savings from generative AI translate to a 1.1% increase in aggregate productivity.[17] Using these data, researchers estimated that, on average, workers are 33% more productive in each hour that they use generative AI.[17] Productivity gains varied significantly across occupations: workers in the computer and mathematics occupation used generative AI in nearly 12% of their work hours and reported this saved them 2.5% of work time, while workers in personal service occupations used this technology in only 1.3% of their work hours and saved only 0.4% of work time.[17] Across industries, information services had both the largest share of work hours spent using generative AI (14.0%) and the highest time savings (2.6%), while leisure, accommodation, and other services had both the lowest share (2.3%) and the lowest time savings (0.6%).[17] Despite these gains, only 5.4% of firms had formally adopted generative AI as of February 2024, suggesting that worker adoption remained mostly informal.[17] In the previous week, 9% of U.S. workers reported using generative AI every workday and 14% for at least one but not every workday.[17]

## Wage Inequality and Skill Polarization in the AI Era

Their analysis provides evidence that, on average, occupations impacted by AI experience a small but positive change in wages, while showing no significant change in employment levels.[18] These findings suggest that AI has the potential to exacerbate labor market polarization, as access to complementary skills and technologies becomes a key determinant of labor-market outcomes.[18] Cazzaniga and others (2024) assume that AI shocks would reduce the labor share by 5.5 percentage points, based on the historical change observed in the United Kingdom between 1980 and 2014.[19] Education moderates but does not eliminate these disparities: college graduates face exposure rates ranging from 32.5 percent in low-income countries to 37.7 percent in high-income countries.[20] In low-income countries, 13.2 percent of college graduates face GenAI automation exposure, and 72 percent of these workers already have internet access, implying that displacement effects could concentrate among the narrow segment of skilled and digitally-connected workers.[20] Critically, the positive wage correlation is driven primarily by occupations with higher software skill requirements, suggesting that access to complementary skills and technologies may play an important role in determining the impact of AI.[18] Higher-income occupations exhibit a strong positive relationship between AI impact and wages.[18] The model features search and matching frictions and shows that sectoral reallocation can be costly because of a potential mismatch between the skills of unemployed workers and those required by firms.[19] Employed workers are not fully insured against idiosyncratic income risk and, therefore, attempt to self-insure through precautionary saving.[19] The ILO's analysis reveals that employment exposure to generative AI positively correlates with economic development, with advanced economies exhibiting the highest exposure rates.[20] Digital infrastructure disparities amplify these inequalities: in Mali, only 1.2 percent of workers hold jobs that are both exposed to GenAI and have internet access, whereas in Switzerland and Singapore, approximately one-third of workers are exposed to GenAI.[20]

## Job Quality and Skill Transformation: New Tasks, Reskilling, and Augmentation

The effects of artificial intelligence on the labor market are most visibly felt not through outright job loss but through changes in the tasks workers perform and shifts in job quality.[21] A systematic literature review underscores that AI's impact on employment is multifaceted, creating new high-skilled jobs and rising demand for interdisciplinary talent while simultaneously replacing low-skilled jobs and polarizing the employment structure.[22] The demand for skills driven by AI is moving towards higher levels of expertise and multidisciplinary integration, requiring workers to adapt to new task profiles.[22].[23] Indeed, the share of the employed population with AI skills remains small — at most 0.3% of those employed in OECD countries on average — though it is growing rapidly, and nearly half of the AI workforce in EU countries falls within the top two deciles of the labour earnings distribution.[21] One standard deviation increase in exposure to AI is associated with a 0.4 percentage point increase in wage growth, largely driven by occupations requiring high familiarity with software.[21] However, OECD case studies find that AI has led to little wage change so far, and men — particularly in finance — were more likely than women to expect a wage increase and less likely to expect a decrease, suggesting AI may put further pressure on existing wage inequalities.[21] Clerical work is the broad occupation most highly exposed, with 24 per cent of clerical tasks considered highly exposed and an additional 58 per cent with medium-level exposure, whereas for other occupational groups the share of highly exposed tasks oscillates between 1 and 4 per cent and medium-exposed tasks do not exceed 25 per cent.[24] The potential employment effects vary widely across country income groups: in low-income countries only 0.4 per cent of total employment is potentially exposed to automation effects, whereas in high-income countries the share rises to 5.5 per cent.[24] Augmentation effects are substantially larger, potentially affecting 10.4 per cent of employment in low-income countries and 13.4 per cent in high-income countries, though infrastructure constraints in lower-income countries will likely impede adoption and increase the productivity gap.[24] The effects are also highly gendered, with more than double the share of women potentially affected by automation compared to men.[24] On the organizational side, businesses are actively integrating AI for automation of repetitive and simple tasks, focusing on enhancing overall efficiency, and pursuing skill-based planning to align their workforce with AI advancements.[23] Beyond earnings, AI can reduce tedious or dangerous tasks but may also leave workers with a higher-paced work environment, and using AI to support managers' tasks affects the job quality of their subordinates.[21] The most important impact of the technology is likely to be augmenting work rather than fully automating occupations, automating some tasks within an occupation while leaving time for other duties.[24] Strategies to address these challenges include learning methods to bridge the skills gap and a balanced approach to developing both hard and soft skills, ensuring individuals can effectively collaborate with and leverage AI technologies.[23] Workers who use AI report higher enjoyment in their jobs.[21]

## Cross-Study Synthesis: Convergence, Contradictions, and Evidence Strength

Regarding inequality, studies diverge on AI's distributional consequences: one analysis predicts AI will reduce 90:10 wage inequality because it is directed at high-skilled tasks, while an IMF working paper warns that AI gives rise to winner-takes-all dynamics that advantage highly skilled individuals and frontier countries, potentially increasing inequality and poverty.[25][6] Methodological limitations remain significant: existing AI exposure measures are criticized for lacking validation on real-world economic outcomes, though one validation study found high correlation between Eloundou et al.'s exposure measures and Microsoft Copilot usage from 2025.[26] The early evidence on how AI is affecting the labor market is collectively inconclusive, and claims about harmful impacts on particular groups of workers are premature, while multiple studies using the Current Population Survey show at most small changes in hiring in AI-exposed jobs.[27][26]

## Conclusions and Unresolved Research Gaps

Current data necessarily provide only weak signals about future impacts, and existing research on labor demand represents just one corner of a vast landscape of urgent questions, leaving many plausible channels of impact under-explored.[27] However, work from the Stanford Digital Economy Lab provides some of the earliest large-scale evidence consistent with the hypothesis that AI is beginning to have a significant and disproportionate impact on entry-level workers in the American labor market.[26] One systematic review emphasizes the dual nature of AI as both a disruptor and an enabler, highlighting the need for policy interventions and upskilling to mitigate risks associated with automation.[28] Validation of exposure measures remains incomplete, though one study found that AI exposure measures from Eloundou et al. (2024) show very high correlation with Microsoft Copilot usage from 2025, suggesting some predictive validity.[26] The collective evidence on AI's labor-market effects is inconclusive, with one analysis finding that early research on AI's impact on labor demand is collectively inconclusive.[27] Multiple studies—including Gimbel et al. (2025), Eckhardt and Goldschlag (2025), and Dominski and Lee (2025)—use the Current Population Survey to show at most small changes in hiring in AI-exposed jobs.[26] While AI exposure measures converge toward high-wage jobs, the effects of GenAI on employment remain unresolved.[29] The matching process between employers and job candidates is another area of uncertainty; one study shows that algorithmic writing assistance for resumes leads to causal increases in hiring and wages, another finds that AI-assisted interviewing improves candidate selection, and other research highlights that AI usage may dilute labor-market signals by making cover letters less informative.[26] Yet these potential gains from complementarity may not be realized due to infrastructure challenges such as limited access to digital tools and skills.[29] In high-income countries, women are disproportionately affected by automation, with 8.5% of female employment classified as high automation potential compared to 3.9% for men.[29] Earlier labor-market disruptions, such as the China shock documented by Autor, Dorn, and Hanson (2013), show that geographically concentrated shocks can produce notable localized economic and political effects that tend to persist.[27] The need for better data and more research on the relationship between AI and the labor market remains enormous.[27]
