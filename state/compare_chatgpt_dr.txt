Tirzepatide for Glycemic Control and Weight Loss
in Adults With Type 2 Diabetes
Executive summary
Across the randomized evidence base in adults with type 2 diabetes, tirzepatide delivers unusually large and
consistent reductions in both HbA1c and body weight. In the core SURPASS program, mean HbA1c
reductions were generally about 1.9% to 2.6%, and mean weight reductions ranged from roughly 5 kg to 13
kg, depending on baseline therapy, dose, and comparator. The eﬀect was seen in monotherapy, after
metformin, after metformin plus SGLT2 inhibitor therapy, in people at high cardiovascular risk, and in
patients already using basal insulin. Beneﬁt was durable: in SURPASS-4, glycemic and weight separation
versus insulin glargine persisted through 104 weeks, and in SURPASS-CVOT, metabolic advantages over
dulaglutide were maintained over years of follow-up. 
The best direct comparator data are especially compelling. In SURPASS-2, tirzepatide 10 mg and 15 mg were
superior to semaglutide 1 mg for HbA1c and all three tirzepatide doses were superior for weight loss; at 40
weeks, the HbA1c treatment diﬀerences versus semaglutide were −0.15%, −0.39%, and −0.45%, and the
weight diﬀerences were −1.9 kg, −3.6 kg, and −5.5 kg for tirzepatide 5, 10, and 15 mg, respectively. Against
insulin comparators, tirzepatide also outperformed insulin degludec, insulin glargine, and insulin lispro,
while usually reducing weight rather than causing gain. In SURPASS-6, adding tirzepatide to basal insulin
was superior to adding prandial lispro, with a pooled HbA1c diﬀerence of −0.98% and a pooled weight
diﬀerence of −12.2 kg at 52 weeks. 
The main trade-oﬀ is tolerability, not eﬃcacy. Gastrointestinal adverse events are dose-related and class-
consistent, especially nausea, diarrhea, vomiting, and dyspepsia; they occur most often during up-titration
and then decline. Severe hypoglycemia is uncommon when tirzepatide is used without insulin or
sulfonylureas, but risk rises when it is combined with those agents. Pancreatitis and gallbladder events are
uncommon but clinically important. Injection-site reactions occur more often than with placebo, though
usually infrequently overall. Pre-approval pooled cardiovascular analysis from the U.S. Food and Drug
Administration
 found no excess major adverse cardiovascular event risk, and SURPASS-CVOT later
showed noninferiority versus dulaglutide for major cardiovascular events, with metabolic advantages
favoring tirzepatide but without proving cardiovascular superiority. 
Clinically, tirzepatide is strongest when the treatment goal is simultaneous HbA1c lowering and substantial
weight loss, especially for people with overweight or obesity, for those with poor glycemic control despite
oral therapy, and for those on basal insulin in whom avoiding basal-bolus intensiﬁcation is desirable. The
main reasons to hesitate are prominent gastrointestinal intolerance, concern for rapid glycemic shifts in
vulnerable eyes, prior pancreatitis, active gallbladder disease, severe gastrointestinal disease such as
gastroparesis, or U.S.-speciﬁc contraindications related to medullary thyroid carcinoma or MEN2.

Evidence base and study architecture
This report prioritizes the core SURPASS publications, the pre-approval statistical review from the FDA, the
current U.S. prescribing information, the product information from the EMA, and higher-quality systematic
and network meta-analyses. All pivotal SURPASS studies were sponsored by Eli Lilly and Company
. The
program was designed to test tirzepatide across the type 2 diabetes treatment continuum: diet and exercise
alone, metformin background therapy, metformin plus SGLT2 inhibitor therapy, high-cardiovascular-risk
populations on oral agents, and patients already using basal insulin. Primary eﬃcacy endpoints were
usually change in HbA1c at 40 or 52 weeks, with body weight, HbA1c target attainment, fasting glucose, and
selected metabolic markers as key secondary endpoints. 
Methodologically, the program mixes double-blind placebo-controlled trials and open-label active-
comparator trials. That matters: open-label designs may inﬂuence symptom reporting, treatment
discontinuation, and behavioral co-interventions, although HbA1c itself is an objective endpoint. Another
architectural point is comparator selection. The most important direct GLP-1 receptor agonist comparison,
SURPASS-2, used semaglutide 1 mg once weekly. Higher semaglutide doses are now used in current
practice, including 2 mg weekly for type 2 diabetes; therefore some modern tirzepatide-versus-semaglutide
comparisons rely on indirect evidence rather than direct head-to-head randomization.

The population gradient in these trials is clinically important. Mean age rose from the mid-50s in SURPASS-1
and -3 to the mid-60s in SURPASS-4 and CVOT; mean baseline HbA1c ranged from roughly 7.9% to 8.8%;
mean body weight was about 86 kg to 95 kg; and mean diabetes duration rose from 4.7 years in
monotherapy to 14 years in the basal-insulin intensiﬁcation setting. The program therefore covers early
disease and late disease, but generalizability is weaker for people with prior pancreatitis, advanced diabetic
retinopathy requiring treatment, severe gastrointestinal disease, or very advanced kidney disease, because
such patients were often excluded or speciﬁcally cautioned against.

Glycemic eﬃcacy
Tirzepatide’s glycemic eﬃcacy is robust across every major comparison that matters clinically. Against
placebo monotherapy in SURPASS-1, the highest dose reduced HbA1c by 2.07% from a baseline around
7.9%, with 87% to 92% of patients reaching HbA1c <7%. Against semaglutide 1 mg in SURPASS-2, superiority
was statistically demonstrated at all doses for HbA1c, with the largest eﬀect at 15 mg. Against basal insulin
strategies in SURPASS-3 and SURPASS-4, tirzepatide lowered HbA1c by roughly another 0.6% to 1.1% beyond
degludec or glargine. And in SURPASS-6, when the practical decision was “weekly incretin intensiﬁcation”
versus “basal-bolus intensiﬁcation,” tirzepatide again won, with a pooled mean HbA1c diﬀerence of nearly 1
percentage point and a much larger proportion of patients reaching HbA1c <7%. 
Clinically, the target-attainment results are as impressive as the mean-change results. In SURPASS-2, HbA1c
<7% was achieved by 82%, 86%, and 86% with tirzepatide 5, 10, and 15 mg, compared with 79% for
semaglutide 1 mg; HbA1c <5.7% was achieved by 27%, 40%, and 46% versus 19%. In SURPASS-4, the
week-52 rates were more widely separated: HbA1c <7% in 81%, 88%, and 91% versus 51% with glargine;
HbA1c <5.7% in 23%, 33%, and 43% versus 3%. In SURPASS-5, HbA1c <7% occurred in 85% to 90% of
tirzepatide-treated patients versus 34% with placebo despite background basal insulin. Across the earlier
SURPASS program, JAMA’s summary of the program-level experience reported that 81% to 97% of
participants achieved HbA1c <7% and 23% to 62% achieved HbA1c <5.7%, depending on dose and trial
context. 
The time-course also matters. From the trial ﬁgures, HbA1c curves generally separate within roughly 8 to 12
weeks, then stabilize at lower levels by 24 to 40 weeks, while higher tirzepatide doses preserve separation
thereafter. Weight curves often keep diverging after glycemic curves have begun to stabilize. That pattern is
clearest in SURPASS-2 and SURPASS-3, and SURPASS-1 explicitly noted that weight loss had not plateaued by
week 40. In SURPASS-4, durability is unusually well documented: at 104 weeks, HbA1c still averaged 6.43%,
6.13%, and 6.11% with tirzepatide 5, 10, and 15 mg versus 7.47% with glargine. This is partly an inference
from trial trajectory graphs, but it is well supported by the long-term follow-up data. 
Weight loss and cardiometabolic eﬀects
For many clinicians, tirzepatide’s diﬀerentiator is not just HbA1c but the size and consistency of its weight
eﬀect in people with diabetes. In SURPASS-1, tirzepatide produced up to 9.5 kg weight loss, roughly 11.0%
of baseline body weight. In SURPASS-2, the eﬀect was dose-dependent and clearly stronger than
semaglutide 1 mg: at 40 weeks, weight loss was 7.6, 9.3, and 11.2 kg with tirzepatide versus 5.7 kg with
semaglutide, with corresponding eﬃcacy-estimand losses of 8.5%, 11.0%, and 13.1% versus 6.7%. In
SURPASS-3, the highest dose produced about 12.9 kg loss, around 13.9% of baseline weight, whereas
insulin degludec caused gain. In SURPASS-5 and SURPASS-6, tirzepatide continued to reduce weight despite
background insulin, a setting in which weight gain is usually expected. 
Target-based weight outcomes reinforce that impression. In SURPASS-2, at least 15% body-weight loss
occurred in 15%, 24%, and 36% of tirzepatide-treated patients versus 8% with semaglutide. In SURPASS-5,
the rates of at least 5%, 10%, and 15% loss were 48% to 72%, 21% to 42%, and 7% to 24% with tirzepatide
versus 6%, 1%, and 0% with placebo. In SURPASS-6, body-weight loss of at least 5%, 10%, and 15% was
achieved by 62% to 78%, 32% to 57%, and 13% to 37%, respectively, despite ongoing basal insulin. These are

Safety and tolerability
The adverse-event proﬁle is recognizably incretin-like and dose-dependent. In the pooled placebo-
controlled adult type 2 diabetes trials used in the U.S. label, nausea occurred in 12%, 15%, and 18% with
tirzepatide 5, 10, and 15 mg versus 4% with placebo; diarrhea in 12%, 13%, and 17% versus 9%; vomiting in
5%, 5%, and 9% versus 2%; constipation in 6%, 6%, and 7% versus 1%; and dyspepsia in 8%, 8%, and 5%
versus 3%. Gastrointestinal adverse reactions overall occurred in 37.1%, 39.6%, and 43.6% of tirzepatide-
treated patients versus 20.4% with placebo. Discontinuation due speciﬁcally to GI adverse events was 3.0%,
5.4%, and 6.6% versus 0.4% with placebo, and most nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea clustered during dose
escalation and then decreased over time. 
The direct-comparator trials sharpen the clinical picture. In SURPASS-2, nausea was not clearly higher than
with semaglutide 1 mg, but diarrhea, vomiting, and decreased appetite trended upward with higher
tirzepatide doses. In SURPASS-4, nausea rose from 12% at 5 mg to 23% at 15 mg versus 2% with glargine;
diarrhea rose from 13% to 22% versus 4%; and treatment discontinuation due to any adverse event was 9%
to 11% versus 5%. In SURPASS-5, treatment-emergent nausea was 12.9%, 17.6%, and 18.3% versus 2.5%
with placebo; diarrhea 12.1%, 12.6%, and 20.8% versus 10.0%; vomiting 6.9%, 7.6%, and 12.5% versus 2.5%;
and discontinuation due to adverse events 6.0%, 8.4%, and 10.8% versus 2.5%. In SURPASS-6, pooled
gastrointestinal adverse events occurred in 42.3% with tirzepatide and 8.6% with insulin lispro, but serious
adverse events were actually less frequent with tirzepatide than with lispro. 
Hypoglycemia is where tirzepatide’s safety proﬁle becomes context-dependent. In monotherapy or oral-
drug settings, clinically signiﬁcant hypoglycemia is rare: the label reports 0% with tirzepatide monotherapy
versus 1% with placebo for blood glucose <54 mg/dL, and SURPASS-1 reported no clinically signiﬁcant or
severe hypoglycemia. But when background insulin or sulfonylureas are present, risk rises. In SURPASS-5,
hypoglycemia <54 mg/dL occurred in 14.2% to 19.3% versus 12.5% with placebo, and three episodes of
severe hypoglycemia occurred across the 10 mg and 15 mg groups. In contrast, in SURPASS-6 the
comparison was favorable: severe hypoglycemia occurred in 0.4% of tirzepatide-treated patients versus
4.2% of insulin-lispro-treated patients, and clinically signiﬁcant or severe events occurred at 0.4 versus 4.4
events per patient-year. That diﬀerence is one of the strongest practical arguments for using tirzepatide
before escalating to basal-bolus insulin in suitable patients. 
Pancreatitis, gallbladder disease, cardiovascular events, and injection-site reactions deserve separate
attention because they shape monitoring and counseling. The U.S. label states that tirzepatide has not been
studied in patients with a prior history of pancreatitis and warns to discontinue if pancreatitis is suspected.

In SURPASS-2, adjudication-conﬁrmed pancreatitis occurred in four tirzepatide-treated patients and three
semaglutide-treated patients, with no severe cases. SURPASS-5 and SURPASS-6 reported no adjudication-
conﬁrmed pancreatitis, but cholelithiasis or cholecystitis did occur at low frequencies. In pooled placebo-

placebo. Acute gallbladder disease in adult placebo-controlled trials occurred in 0.6% with tirzepatide
versus 0% with placebo. 
Cardiovascular safety is no longer just a pre-approval inference. The FDA’s pooled pre-approval meta-
analysis of seven trials found a MACE-4 hazard ratio of 0.81 with a 97.85% CI of 0.52 to 1.26, clearing the
regulatory noninferiority margin. In SURPASS-4, the pooled hazard ratio for MACE-4 versus glargine was
0.74 (95% CI 0.51 to 1.08), again with no signal of excess risk. SURPASS-CVOT then showed noninferiority to
dulaglutide for the harder contemporary benchmark of 3-point MACE: HR 0.92 (95% CI 0.83 to 1.01),
P=0.003 for noninferiority and P=0.09 for superiority. This is reassuring cardiovascular safety evidence, but
it is not the same as proving superiority over a placebo or over an active comparator with established
cardiovascular beneﬁt. 
Tirzepatide also has some smaller but still practical safety signals. The label reports mean heart-rate
increases of roughly 2 to 4 beats per minute versus about 1 beat per minute with placebo, and the
proportion with sinus tachycardia-associated increases was numerically dose-related. Rapid glycemic
improvement may transiently worsen diabetic retinopathy, especially in patients with pre-existing disease
and large HbA1c falls. Acute kidney injury has been reported in the setting of severe gastrointestinal ﬂuid
loss, so renal monitoring matters in patients with dehydration risk. 
Regulatory guidance and practical prescribing
In current regulatory documents, tirzepatide is an adjunct to diet and exercise for inadequately controlled
type 2 diabetes, with dosing that starts low and escalates for tolerability. The EMA SmPC states that the
starting dose is 2.5 mg once weekly for 4 weeks, then 5 mg once weekly, with further 2.5 mg increments as
needed; the U.S. label similarly uses 2.5 mg weekly for initiation and allows escalation every 4 weeks to a
maximum of 15 mg weekly. No dose adjustment is needed on the basis of age, sex, race, ethnicity, body
weight, renal impairment, or hepatic impairment, although experience in severe renal impairment and
ESRD is limited and caution is advised. 
Regulatory warnings matter as much as eﬃcacy. The U.S. prescribing information lists contraindications for
personal or family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma, MEN2, and serious hypersensitivity; it also warns
about acute pancreatitis, hypoglycemia with insulin secretagogues or insulin, hypersensitivity reactions,
acute kidney injury from volume depletion, severe gastrointestinal adverse reactions, diabetic retinopathy
complications, acute gallbladder disease, and pulmonary aspiration during general anesthesia or deep
sedation. The EMA product information is not identical: hypersensitivity is its formal contraindication, while
pancreatitis and hypoglycemia are handled as warnings and precautions. From a practical standpoint, that
means clinicians should align prescribing with local labeling, not assume the U.S. and EU texts are
interchangeable. 
Patient selection is straightforward in principle. Tirzepatide is most attractive for adults with type 2 diabetes
who need more than one thing at once: substantial HbA1c reduction, meaningful weight loss, avoidance of
insulin intensiﬁcation, or reduction of total insulin exposure. It is especially compelling in patients on
metformin or metformin plus SGLT2 inhibitor therapy who are still above goal, and in basal-insulin users for

Clinical interpretation, gaps, and ongoing trials
The overall clinical interpretation is favorable. If the clinical question is, “Which injectable non-insulin
therapy is most likely to produce the largest combined HbA1c and weight reductions in adults with type 2
diabetes?” the direct-trial and meta-analytic answers both point toward tirzepatide. The strongest patients
for it are those with elevated HbA1c plus overweight or obesity, those for whom insulin intensiﬁcation
would likely worsen weight or hypoglycemia burden, and those seeking a realistic chance of double-digit
percentage weight loss while staying within routine diabetes pharmacotherapy. The biggest counterweights
are gastrointestinal tolerability, cost and access, local formulary restrictions, and the fact that cardiovascular
hard-outcome evidence, while reassuring, currently supports noninferiority versus dulaglutide rather than
clear superiority over an established cardioprotective comparator. 
The most important uncertainties are not about short-term metabolic eﬃcacy but about generalizability
and comparative strategy. Many major SURPASS trials were open-label. The semaglutide head-to-head

and overweight or obesity, and switch strategies from prior GLP-1 receptor agonist therapy to tirzepatide.
Those studies should improve the evidence base for sequencing and for dose optimization beyond the
original pivotal program.

Taken together, the evidence supports a clear conclusion: tirzepatide is one of the most eﬀective available
agents for combined glycemic control and weight reduction in adults with type 2 diabetes, with a beneﬁt
proﬁle that is quantitatively large, durable, and consistent across treatment stages. Its main limitation is not
lack of eﬃcacy but tolerability and selection: the right patient can beneﬁt a great deal, while the wrong
patient may stop because of gastrointestinal symptoms or may need a diﬀerent agent because of comorbid
risk. For clinicians, the practical task is therefore less “does tirzepatide work?” and more “which adults with
type 2 diabetes stand to gain more from its metabolic potency than they risk from its tolerability burden?”
The SURPASS program makes that tradeoﬀ much easier to judge than it was a few years ago.
