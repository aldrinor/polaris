I think the 5-claim structure is close, but I would not treat all five as equal “safety claims.” Claims 1, 2, 3, and 5 are operational safety/performance claims. Claim 4 is a measurement-validity prerequisite: if SME labels are unstable, Claims 1, 2, and 5 lose their ground truth. I would keep it in the contract, but explicitly mark it as a validity gate that must pass before the safety gates are interpretable.

The other structural change: add an explicit evidence/claim-extraction validity layer. A verifier can only miss or catch claims it actually sees and evidence it actually receives. RAGTruth, SAFE, and HHEM-style conventions all separate some version of claim decomposition, evidence grounding, and factual-consistency judgment; they do not magically validate retrieval recall or atomic claim coverage as a single metric. RAGTruth is word/span-level hallucination annotation in RAG outputs, SAFE decomposes long-form answers into facts before verification, and HHEM/FCS evaluates factual consistency against retrieved/search results, not against all truth in the world. That distinction matters for POLARIS. Sources: RAGTruth, SAFE, HHEM docs. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.00396)) ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.18802)) ([docs.vectara.com](https://docs.vectara.com/docs/hallucination-and-evaluation/hallucination-evaluation))

**Claim 1**
For the per-stratum claim-level fabrication bound, I would pick Option α: keep per-stratum gates only, and make per-domain results observability rather than a binding confirmatory gate.

The reason is simple: the domain gate is tempting because it feels safer, but unless domain mix is itself the target estimand, it creates a second family of conditional claims that does not answer the production question. Option β is technically defensible if you write it honestly as “conditional on this artificial gold-set domain construction,” but that is a weak audit claim. It is precise without being very meaningful. I would rather have one strong claim: “for adjudicated fabricated claims in severity stratum S, under the locked gold-set construction, the verifier miss probability is bounded by this UCB.” Then domain is a diagnostic lens: no silent domain collapse, no hidden pocket of badness, but not the headline denominator.

My bigger pushback is independence. MinHash/BGE dedup plus manual split audit removes near-duplicates; it does not create independent observations. If many claims come from the same report, source packet, prompt family, SME construction template, disease area, or benchmark seed, claim-level Wilson intervals will be too optimistic. For Claim 1, either sample so that the independent unit is a “scenario family” and take one claim per family per stratum, or keep all claims but compute a cluster-adjusted sensitivity by scenario/source/report family. I would still let claim-level Wilson be primary only if the construction actively prevents clustered generation.

Also tighten the estimand wording. It should be:

`P(verifier fails to flag/escalate an adjudicated fabrication of severity S | fabricated claim is in severity stratum S and enters the verifier)`

That avoids ambiguity over whether the denominator includes non-fabricated claims.

S0 as “zero observed misses on ≥50 stress items, no UCB target asserted” is fine. Call it a policy sentinel, not a statistical population bound.

**Claim 2**
For the report-level bound, I would use a fourth version: a pre-specified conservative design ICC, informed by surrogate outcomes but not estimated from the zero-event fabrication outcome, with the gate evaluated at the worst pre-registered ICC in a plausible grid.

So, practically: use surrogate outcomes to justify the grid, but gate on the upper design ICC, for example `ICC ∈ {0.01, 0.05, 0.10}` and pass only at `0.10` unless you have strong pre-data justification for a lower cap. I would not say “ICC=0.05 is conservative for most clinical/research applications” as a blanket claim. Cochrane’s cluster guidance notes that values below 0.05 are common, but also that small ICCs can materially widen intervals when clusters are large, and external estimates are often used when direct ICCs are unavailable. ([training.cochrane.org](https://training.cochrane.org/handbook/current/chapter-23))

With zero events, you cannot learn the event ICC from the event data. A bootstrap sensitivity is also weak under all-zero outcomes because naïve resampling just rediscovers zero. The audit-defensible move is to treat ICC as a design assumption, not a fitted fact.

The customer floor is necessary but not sufficient. The binding rule should say:

`UCB(k=0, n_eff) ≤ 5%, where n_eff is computed from the pre-registered conservative ICC and cluster-size-adjusted DEFF; additionally ≥30 customers and no customer >20% of reports.`

If cluster sizes vary materially, use a variable-cluster DEFF, not just `1 + (m - 1)ICC`. And report a customer-level exact zero-event bound as a sensitivity: “0 of C customers had any affected report.” It will be conservative, but it makes the cluster risk legible.

**Claim 3**
I would not use LORD++ or SAFFRON as the primary drift contract. I would keep monthly BH or BY with a persistence rule as the audited primary, and use EWMA/CUSUM as operational side monitors.

Online FDR is mathematically attractive, but it is not the best primary here. SAFFRON/LORD are built for a possibly infinite sequence of p-values where each threshold is chosen from the past and must not depend on the current or future p-values. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/1802.09098)) That is clean in theory, but POLARIS drift is batched, delayed-label, correlated, multi-cell monitoring with operational review cadence. Monthly FDR is easier to explain, easier to reproduce, easier to freeze, and easier to audit. If the cells are strongly dependent, use Benjamini-Yekutieli or a hierarchical/global-then-cell procedure rather than pretending BH independence assumptions are clean. FDA’s multiplicity guidance is also philosophically aligned with grouping and ordering endpoint families rather than letting an implicit family sprawl. ([fda.gov](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/multiple-endpoints-clinical-trials))

I would split drift into two layers:

Formal labeled outcome drift: miss rate, escalation recall, report-level safety events, measured on labeled canary/audit samples.

Operational leading-indicator drift: retrieval depth, evidence-source mix, unsupported-claim score distribution, claim complexity, domain mix, latency/fallback rates.

EWMA/CUSUM belongs especially in the second layer. SPC methods are standard for detecting small process shifts and trends, but they are process-control alerts, not a clean multiple-testing contract over 72 cells. ([jmp.com](https://www.jmp.com/en/statistics-knowledge-portal/quality-and-reliability-methods/control-charts/cusum-and-ewma-control-charts))

Also rewrite the estimand. `P(per-cell drift detected | no true drift)` is an error-control property, not the drift estimand. The estimand should be a change in a named metric relative to a locked baseline, with a minimum material effect size. Then the error-control claim says how often the monitor falsely alerts under no material drift.

**Claim 4**
Keep binary safety-critical agreement as the primary. For ordinal agreement, I would choose linear symmetric weights as secondary. I would not use custom asymmetric κ as a primary audit statistic.

Quadratic weights are common, but they are too forgiving for this severity scale. On a four-level S0/S1/S2/S3 scale, quadratic weighting gives near-agreement too much credit; calling S1 as S2 is exactly the kind of adjacent miss that can matter operationally. Linear weighting is less flattering and easier to defend.

Asymmetric weights are substantively right for loss analysis: under-rating severity is worse than over-rating. But they are not the standard κ convention, and they raise the obvious audit question: did you tune the weights to make agreement look acceptable? The better structure is:

Primary: lower 95% CI on binary safety-critical κ or Gwet AC1/AC2 for S0/S1 vs not.

Secondary: linearly weighted ordinal κ on S0/S1/S2/S3.

Safety-loss table: under-rating rate, especially S0→lower and S1→S2/S3, with exact/Wilson intervals.

Weighted κ literature explicitly recognizes linear and quadratic schemes as frequent choices and warns that weight selection can be subjective and can impair comparability. ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00357-021-09386-5)) FDA COA thinking is more “fit for context of use and reliability” than “use this exact κ weight,” and ICH-GCP is about protocolized, auditable, reliable data rather than prescribing ordinal weights. ([fda.gov](https://www.fda.gov/about-fda/clinical-outcome-assessment-coa-frequently-asked-questions)) ([database.ich.org](https://database.ich.org/sites/default/files/ICH_E6%28R3%29_Step4_FinalGuideline_2025_0106.pdf))

One more point: measure IRR on initial blinded labels before consensus adjudication. Consensus labels are fine for gold-set truth; they are not the object for rater reliability.

**Claim 5**
I would pick A: lock the threshold before validation. More precisely, freeze the classifier version, features, calibration method, and threshold-selection rule before opening the validation labels. If thresholds vary by cell, freeze the per-cell thresholds before validation too.

B can be acceptable only if the whole calibration scheme and threshold derivation are trained on a separate calibration set and then frozen before final validation. If the target precision is selected after seeing validation behavior, it becomes post-hoc operating-point search. C is useful for governance, but cost-ratio arguments are too plastic for a primary audit denominator.

There is an important precision problem in your proposal. In a cell-stratified validation set with enriched positives, raw precision is not production PPV unless the sample prevalence matches production or you reweight to production prevalence. Recall is stable conditional on positives; precision moves with prevalence. So I would make recall the safety-primary classifier metric and treat precision as a workload/triage metric estimated either on production-canary prevalence or via pre-specified prevalence weighting.

Also, the denominator floors need to be metric-specific:

Recall floor: enough true positives, e.g. `N_true_positive ≥ 30`.

Precision floor: enough predicted positives at locked threshold, e.g. `N_pred_positive ≥ 30`.

`N_positive ≥ 30 per metric` is not quite right for precision.

**Missing Pieces**
The biggest missing safety claim is retrieval/evidence sufficiency. If the system fails to retrieve decisive contradictory evidence, the verifier may look good on “given evidence” but fail in production. You need either a separate claim or a prerequisite: for adjudicated S0/S1 fabrications, the evidence pipeline retrieves at least one decisive source/evidence unit within top-k at a locked recall bound. Without that, Claim 1 is really a verifier-on-provided-evidence claim.

The second missing piece is atomic claim extraction recall. A report-level fabrication can evade Claim 1 if the dangerous sentence is never decomposed into an auditable claim. SAFE-style evaluation makes claim decomposition explicit for a reason. POLARIS needs the same: bound the probability that an S0/S1 factual assertion in a report is omitted from the claim set that the verifier evaluates.

The third is contamination/group splitting. Your Claim 1 mentions manual group-split audit, but I would make this a locked prerequisite: no gold item, source packet, report template, or near-duplicate scenario may appear in training/tuning/dev material for the verifier, classifier, threshold selection, prompt iteration, or SME calibration examples. This is not just dedup; it is data lineage.

On multiple testing across the five claims: no, I would not Bonferroni across them if the release rule is conjunctive: all required gates must pass. That is an intersection-union structure. If any one claim is false, global pass requires that false claim to pass its own test, so the global false-pass risk is bounded by that claim’s alpha under the usual conditions. But if you publish five separate “95% confident” marginal claims and want simultaneous coverage for all intervals, then yes, you need simultaneous intervals or adjusted language. The pass/fail gate and the marketing/audit language are different statistical objects.

For pre-registration, use a signed-and-hashed internal protocol as the minimum. Commit the protocol, analysis code, denominator definitions, random seeds, data extract hashes, model/prompt/index versions, threshold values, adjudication rules, and amendment rules before Phase 0b labeling begins. Public OSF/AsPredicted is good for credibility if IP/PHI allows it, but it is not necessary for audit-grade internal governance. A private immutable registry, timestamped commit, or notarized hash is enough if access control and audit trail are clean.

For amendments, write the protocol now. Administrative corrections are allowed with no statistical consequence. Design-changing amendments before relevant labels/outcomes are seen are allowed but must be versioned. Amendments after outcome data are seen turn the old analysis into exploratory evidence; the confirmatory gate needs a fresh sealed holdout, a prospectively defined alpha-spending plan, or a clearly labeled bridging validation. If a gate is over-restrictive after Phase 0b labels are known, that is learning, not grounds to quietly relax the denominator.

The next iteration should be v3.0 drafting, not another broad design round. I would draft it as: “four safety gates plus one label-validity gate, with retrieval sufficiency, claim extraction recall, contamination control, and amendment governance as prerequisites.” That gives you an audit-grade contract without turning every diagnostic into a new statistical claim.

I would not use LORD++ or SAFFRON as the primary drift contract. I would keep monthly BH or BY with a persistence rule as the audited primary, and use EWMA/CUSUM as operational side monitors.

Online FDR is mathematically attractive, but it is not the best primary here. SAFFRON/LORD are built for a possibly infinite sequence of p-values where each threshold is chosen from the past and must not depend on the current or future p-values. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/1802.09098)) That is clean in theory, but POLARIS drift is batched, delayed-label, correlated, multi-cell monitoring with operational review cadence. Monthly FDR is easier to explain, easier to reproduce, easier to freeze, and easier to audit. If the cells are strongly dependent, use Benjamini-Yekutieli or a hierarchical/global-then-cell procedure rather than pretending BH independence assumptions are clean. FDA’s multiplicity guidance is also philosophically aligned with grouping and ordering endpoint families rather than letting an implicit family sprawl. ([fda.gov](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/multiple-endpoints-clinical-trials))

I would split drift into two layers:

Formal labeled outcome drift: miss rate, escalation recall, report-level safety events, measured on labeled canary/audit samples.

Operational leading-indicator drift: retrieval depth, evidence-source mix, unsupported-claim score distribution, claim complexity, domain mix, latency/fallback rates.

EWMA/CUSUM belongs especially in the second layer. SPC methods are standard for detecting small process shifts and trends, but they are process-control alerts, not a clean multiple-testing contract over 72 cells. ([jmp.com](https://www.jmp.com/en/statistics-knowledge-portal/quality-and-reliability-methods/control-charts/cusum-and-ewma-control-charts))

Also rewrite the estimand. `P(per-cell drift detected | no true drift)` is an error-control property, not the drift estimand. The estimand should be a change in a named metric relative to a locked baseline, with a minimum material effect size. Then the error-control claim says how often the monitor falsely alerts under no material drift.

**Claim 4**
Keep binary safety-critical agreement as the primary. For ordinal agreement, I would choose linear symmetric weights as secondary. I would not use custom asymmetric κ as a primary audit statistic.

Quadratic weights are common, but they are too forgiving for this severity scale. On a four-level S0/S1/S2/S3 scale, quadratic weighting gives near-agreement too much credit; calling S1 as S2 is exactly the kind of adjacent miss that can matter operationally. Linear weighting is less flattering and easier to defend.

Asymmetric weights are substantively right for loss analysis: under-rating severity is worse than over-rating. But they are not the standard κ convention, and they raise the obvious audit question: did you tune the weights to make agreement look acceptable? The better structure is:

Primary: lower 95% CI on binary safety-critical κ or Gwet AC1/AC2 for S0/S1 vs not.

Secondary: linearly weighted ordinal κ on S0/S1/S2/S3.

Safety-loss table: under-rating rate, especially S0→lower and S1→S2/S3, with exact/Wilson intervals.

Weighted κ literature explicitly recognizes linear and quadratic schemes as frequent choices and warns that weight selection can be subjective and can impair comparability. ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00357-021-09386-5)) FDA COA thinking is more “fit for context of use and reliability” than “use this exact κ weight,” and ICH-GCP is about protocolized, auditable, reliable data rather than prescribing ordinal weights. ([fda.gov](https://www.fda.gov/about-fda/clinical-outcome-assessment-coa-frequently-asked-questions)) ([database.ich.org](https://database.ich.org/sites/default/files/ICH_E6%28R3%29_Step4_FinalGuideline_2025_0106.pdf))

One more point: measure IRR on initial blinded labels before consensus adjudication. Consensus labels are fine for gold-set truth; they are not the object for rater reliability.

**Claim 5**
I would pick A: lock the threshold before validation. More precisely, freeze the classifier version, features, calibration method, and threshold-selection rule before opening the validation labels. If thresholds vary by cell, freeze the per-cell thresholds before validation too.

B can be acceptable only if the whole calibration scheme and threshold derivation are trained on a separate calibration set and then frozen before final validation. If the target precision is selected after seeing validation behavior, it becomes post-hoc operating-point search. C is useful for governance, but cost-ratio arguments are too plastic for a primary audit denominator.

There is an important precision problem in your proposal. In a cell-stratified validation set with enriched positives, raw precision is not production PPV unless the sample prevalence matches production or you reweight to production prevalence. Recall is stable conditional on positives; precision moves with prevalence. So I would make recall the safety-primary classifier metric and treat precision as a workload/triage metric estimated either on production-canary prevalence or via pre-specified prevalence weighting.

Also, the denominator floors need to be metric-specific:

Recall floor: enough true positives, e.g. `N_true_positive ≥ 30`.

Precision floor: enough predicted positives at locked threshold, e.g. `N_pred_positive ≥ 30`.

`N_positive ≥ 30 per metric` is not quite right for precision.

**Missing Pieces**
The biggest missing safety claim is retrieval/evidence sufficiency. If the system fails to retrieve decisive contradictory evidence, the verifier may look good on “given evidence” but fail in production. You need either a separate claim or a prerequisite: for adjudicated S0/S1 fabrications, the evidence pipeline retrieves at least one decisive source/evidence unit within top-k at a locked recall bound. Without that, Claim 1 is really a verifier-on-provided-evidence claim.

The second missing piece is atomic claim extraction recall. A report-level fabrication can evade Claim 1 if the dangerous sentence is never decomposed into an auditable claim. SAFE-style evaluation makes claim decomposition explicit for a reason. POLARIS needs the same: bound the probability that an S0/S1 factual assertion in a report is omitted from the claim set that the verifier evaluates.

The third is contamination/group splitting. Your Claim 1 mentions manual group-split audit, but I would make this a locked prerequisite: no gold item, source packet, report template, or near-duplicate scenario may appear in training/tuning/dev material for the verifier, classifier, threshold selection, prompt iteration, or SME calibration examples. This is not just dedup; it is data lineage.

On multiple testing across the five claims: no, I would not Bonferroni across them if the release rule is conjunctive: all required gates must pass. That is an intersection-union structure. If any one claim is false, global pass requires that false claim to pass its own test, so the global false-pass risk is bounded by that claim’s alpha under the usual conditions. But if you publish five separate “95% confident” marginal claims and want simultaneous coverage for all intervals, then yes, you need simultaneous intervals or adjusted language. The pass/fail gate and the marketing/audit language are different statistical objects.

For pre-registration, use a signed-and-hashed internal protocol as the minimum. Commit the protocol, analysis code, denominator definitions, random seeds, data extract hashes, model/prompt/index versions, threshold values, adjudication rules, and amendment rules before Phase 0b labeling begins. Public OSF/AsPredicted is good for credibility if IP/PHI allows it, but it is not necessary for audit-grade internal governance. A private immutable registry, timestamped commit, or notarized hash is enough if access control and audit trail are clean.

For amendments, write the protocol now. Administrative corrections are allowed with no statistical consequence. Design-changing amendments before relevant labels/outcomes are seen are allowed but must be versioned. Amendments after outcome data are seen turn the old analysis into exploratory evidence; the confirmatory gate needs a fresh sealed holdout, a prospectively defined alpha-spending plan, or a clearly labeled bridging validation. If a gate is over-restrictive after Phase 0b labels are known, that is learning, not grounds to quietly relax the denominator.

The next iteration should be v3.0 drafting, not another broad design round. I would draft it as: “four safety gates plus one label-validity gate, with retrieval sufficiency, claim extraction recall, contamination control, and amendment governance as prerequisites.” That gives you an audit-grade contract without turning every diagnostic into a new statistical claim.
