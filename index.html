What it is

An autonomous research analyst.

Give it an open-ended question and it does what a human analyst would: it breaks the question into parts, searches many sources, weighs which ones to trust, reconciles the ones that disagree, and writes a structured report in which every claim carries a citation. The answer is not recalled from a model’s memory — it is researched, verified, and written end to end, with no human in the loop.

The comparison that matters

Curated dataset, or the open internet.

There are two kinds of deep-research system, and they are not the same product. One reads from a dataset that has been gathered, cleaned, and frozen in advance. The other reads the live internet as it is — and has to find, judge, and verify the evidence itself, every time. The first is a convenient tool. The second is a general research engine.

Introduction and Scope

The Fourth Industrial Revolution, a term popularized by Klaus Schwab in 2016, is characterized by a fusion of technologies that blurs the lines between the physical, digital, and biological spheres, distinguishing it from previous industrial revolutions through its unprecedented velocity, scope, and systems impact.[1][2] Artificial intelligence sits at the center of this transformation, acting as a key driver that orchestrates technologies such as robotics, automated vehicles, and real-time data analytics to redefine how work is performed across industries.[2] Global business investment in AI increased 13-fold from US$14.57 billion in 2013 to US$189 billion in 2023, with 35% of businesses already using AI in their operations and more than 50% intending to implement AI technologies.[3] This rapid diffusion has prompted predictions that STARA—smart technology, artificial intelligence, robotics, and algorithms—will replace a third of jobs that exist today, with forecasts that by 2025, 85 million jobs may be displaced while 97 million new roles may emerge.[4] Similarly, the World Economic Forum has predicted that AI adoption will make 75 million jobs redundant and create 133 million new ones worldwide by 2022, while economic analyses from PwC, IBM, Deloitte, and Gartner Research project that AI will increase global GDP by 15% by 2030, adding approximately $15.7 trillion.[5] A separate Future of Jobs report projected that 7.1 million jobs will be lost across 15 economic areas from 2015 to 2020, while 2.1 million additional jobs are expected to be created during the same period.[6] The net impact on labor demand depends on how these effects weigh against each other, and the authors find that the sharp slowdown of US wage bill growth over the last three decades reflects weaker-than-usual productivity growth combined with significant shifts in the task content of production against labor.[7] The scope of this disruption spans multiple sectors, reshaping employment through task automation, skill polarization, and industry-specific disruptions in healthcare, education, and creative industries, while also affecting white-collar professionals, gig workers, and freelancers.[3] Industrial robots already exceed 4 million units in operation, with installations steadily increasing due to falling costs and enhanced AI capabilities.[8] Acemoglu and Restrepo's task-based framework posits that automation generates a displacement effect, as capital takes over tasks previously performed by labor.[7]

Task-Based Frameworks and Displacement–Reinstatement

In this framework, production requires the completion of a range of tasks, each of which can be allocated to either capital or labor, and the resulting allocation determines the "task content of production".[7][9] Countervailing the displacement effect is the reinstatement effect, which arises when new labor-intensive tasks are created in which labor has a comparative advantage, directly increasing both the labor share and labor demand.[7][10][11] Historical examples abound: in the 19th century, technological developments generated employment for line workers, engineers, machinists, repairmen, conductors, managers, and financiers.[7] The pattern was particularly pronounced in manufacturing, where the displacement effect reduced labor demand at about 1.1 percent per year, or about 30 percent cumulatively.[10][11] The baseline estimate of the elasticity of substitution between capital and labor at the industry level is 0.8, while firm-level elasticities are estimated between 0.4 and 0.7.[10][11] Complementary research by Autor, using U.S. data from 1940 through 2018, estimates that more than 60 percent of employment in 2018 was found in job titles that did not exist in 1940.[12] Between 1947 and 1987, wage bill per capita grew at 2.5 percent per year, largely explained by the productivity effect of 2.4 percent per year, while the displacement effect reduced labor demand at about 0.48 percent per year and the reinstatement effect increased it by 0.47 percent per year, yielding a near balance.[10][11]

Occupational Exposure and Susceptibility to AI

One framework proposes evaluating the potential impact of large language models by considering their relevance to the tasks workers perform, estimating that roughly 1.8% of jobs could have over half their tasks affected by LLMs with simple interfaces—jumping to just over 46% when accounting for complementary software.[13] AI's potential impact extends beyond routine tasks to nonroutine ones such as diagnosing health conditions, programming computers, and tracking flight routes, though some affected occupations are augmented rather than replaced.[14] Across OECD countries, the occupations most exposed to AI tend to be white-collar roles such as IT professionals, business professionals, managers, and science and engineering professionals; occupations requiring manual skills are the least exposed.[15] In Korea, 32.2% of firms reported that AI increased the kinds of skills required and 38.3% reported an increase in the level of skills required, while 95.5% reported no workforce changes at the department level and 56.5% said AI replaced specific tasks rather than eliminating positions.[15]

Empirical Evidence on Employment and Labor Demand

In occupations where computer use is high, a one standard deviation increase in AI exposure was associated with 5.7 percentage points higher employment growth.[16] Instrumental variable estimates showed AI increased high-skilled employment by 0.376% and medium-skilled employment by 0.397%, while low-skilled employment declined by 0.003%.[17] One study estimated AI will reduce 90:10 wage inequality but will not affect the top 1%.[18] Research on generative AI's short-term effects in an online labor market found it reduced overall demand for workers.[19] Another analysis observed that AI's impact has been modest so far, echoing past innovation cycles.[21] Jobs for AI and Machine Learning specialists have been rising at a compounding rate of 32% per year between 2016 and 2023.[22]

Productivity Effects of Generative AI

In a preregistered experiment, Noy et al. (2023) assigned writing tasks to 453 professionals and randomly exposed half to ChatGPT, finding average time taken decreased by 40% and output quality rose by 18%, while reducing inequality between workers.[24] A systematic review of 194 peer-reviewed articles (2011–2025) concludes AI functions as a general-purpose technology with uneven, context-dependent effects.[25] An ILO research brief across seven countries finds productivity gains are real albeit often unverified and uneven, and worker-reported time savings of a few percent have not yet translated into higher measured output, earnings, or employment.[26] Among workers using generative AI at least once in the previous month, 31.9% spent an hour or more per workday using it.[27] Information services showed the largest share of work hours using generative AI at 14.0% and the highest time savings at 2.6%.[27] A standard model estimated self-reported time savings translate to a 1.1% increase in aggregate productivity.[27]

Wage Inequality, Skill Demand, and Labor Polarization

The share of the employed population with AI skills remains small—at most 0.3% in OECD countries on average—but is growing rapidly, with nearly half the AI workforce in the top two deciles of the labour earnings distribution.[28] A one standard deviation increase in AI exposure is associated with a 0.4 percentage point increase in wage growth, driven by occupations requiring high software familiarity.[28][29] These patterns imply AI has the potential to exacerbate labor market polarization, as benefits accrue disproportionately to workers already at the upper end of the skill and income distribution.[29][30] Job vacancies for AI roles have increased seven-fold since 2012, and jobs requiring AI skills have grown 3.5 times faster than overall vacancies.[31] Notably, generative AI applications benefit the least experienced workers most, with less experienced programmers completing tasks over 50% faster—suggesting AI may under certain conditions decrease performance inequality.[28]

Policy Implications and Frameworks for Managing Disruption

Sectoral reallocation can be costly because of potential mismatch between the skills of unemployed workers and those required by firms.[34] Cazzaniga and others (2024) assume AI shocks would reduce the labor share by 5.5 percentage points based on the historical UK change between 1980 and 2014.[34] A significantly larger share of employment is in occupations with high augmentation potential across regions, ranging from 10.2 percent in Sub-Saharan Africa to 16.1 percent in Southeastern Asia and the Pacific.[35] Online platform workers in the US regularly report median earnings of roughly $2 to $3 per hour, well below the federal minimum wage.[35]

Cross-Study Synthesis and Conclusions

The cross-study evidence reveals a field still in its earliest stages, with convergent acknowledgment that current findings are fragmented and preliminary.[36][37] Early large-scale evidence is consistent with the hypothesis that the AI revolution is beginning to have a significant and disproportionate impact on entry-level workers in the American labor market.[37] Some research found a decline in job postings began in 2022, prior to ChatGPT, corresponding better to rising interest rates than to LLMs, and Frank et al. (2026) found similar results.[36] Important questions about AI's effects on the labor market remain unanswered, and there is a need for more research and better data.[37][36][35]
